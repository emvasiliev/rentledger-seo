# Form 4562 for Canadian Landlords: Vermont Rental Property Depreciation Guide

## What Is Form 4562?

Form 4562 (Depreciation and Amortization) is the IRS form used to calculate and claim depreciation deductions on depreciable assets, including residential rental property. For Canadian landlords owning rental real estate in Vermont, Form 4562 is essential for maximizing deductions on the US federal return.

Depreciation allows you to deduct the theoretical decline in value of your building (not land) over time. While depreciation is a non-cash expense—you don't actually pay money—it reduces your taxable rental income on your US federal return (Form 1040-NR, Schedule E).

The key depreciation period for residential rental property in the United States is **27.5 years** using the straight-line depreciation method. This applies equally to Canadian and US landlords.

## How Form 4562 Applies to Vermont Rental Properties

Vermont is a popular jurisdiction for Canadian landlords, particularly those based in Quebec, due to its geographic proximity and relatively straightforward rental property regulations. However, owning property in Vermont creates a US tax filing obligation that many Canadian landlords underestimate.

When you own residential rental property in Vermont, you must:

1. **File Form 1040-NR** (US Nonresident Alien Individual Income Tax Return) if you have US rental income
2. **Report depreciation on Schedule E** (Supplemental Income and Loss), supported by Form 4562
3. **File a Vermont state return** (Form IN-111 or IN-112, depending on filing status)
4. **Pay Vermont state income tax** at a rate of 8.75% on your net rental income

The depreciation you claim on Form 4562 flows directly to Schedule E, reducing your federal taxable income. However, this same depreciation is also added back on your Vermont state return—Vermont does not conform to federal depreciation deductions for rental real estate in the same manner, creating potential reconciliation issues.

## Who Must File Form 4562

If you meet any of these criteria, you must file Form 4562:

- You own residential rental property in Vermont generating rental income
- You claim depreciation on rental real estate
- You placed property in service during the current tax year
- You claimed bonus depreciation or Section 179 deductions in previous years
- You are depreciating any improvements or additions to the property

**Non-resident alien status:** As a Canadian citizen or permanent resident, you are classified as a nonresident alien for US tax purposes (unless you meet specific presence tests under the Canada-US Tax Treaty). This does not exempt you from Form 4562 filing requirements.

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form 4562 for Vermont Property

### Part I: Election to Expense and Other Depreciation (Not Usually Used for Rental Property)

For most Canadian landlords with Vermont rental properties, Part I is not applicable. This section applies to Section 179 expensing and bonus depreciation, which have limited use in rental real estate.

### Part II: General Depreciation System (MACRS)

This is where you claim depreciation on your residential building. Follow these steps:

**Step 1: Determine Your Depreciable Basis**

Your depreciable basis is the cost of the building only—**not the land**. If you purchased the property for $350,000 and a professional appraisal determined the land value to be $100,000, your depreciable basis is $250,000.

This is critical: land does not depreciate. The IRS requires you to allocate the purchase price between land and building. Use:
- Professional appraisals
- The property's assessed value from the Vermont town assessment record (multiply by the assessment ratio)
- A real estate agent's opinion

**Step 2: Determine the Placed-in-Service Date**

This is the date you first began renting the property or held it for rent. If you purchased the Vermont property on June 15, 2023, and began renting it on July 1, 2023, your placed-in-service date is July 1, 2023. Depreciation begins in the month of placed-in-service.

**Step 3: Calculate Annual Depreciation**

For residential rental property:
- **Depreciable Basis:** $250,000 (building value only)
- **Recovery Period:** 27.5 years
- **Annual Depreciation:** $250,000 ÷ 27.5 = **$9,090.91 per year**

If you placed the property in service mid-year, you claim a partial-year deduction in Year 1. The IRS uses the mid-month convention for real property (assuming placed-in-service mid-month).

**Step 4: Complete Form 4562, Line 19 (Residential Rental Property)**

On line 19, enter:
- Description: "Vermont residential rental property (address)"
- Date placed in service: 07/01/2023
- Depreciable basis: $250,000
- Recovery period: 27.5 years
- Method: Straight-line
- Convention: Mid-month
- Depreciation for current year: $9,090.91

### Part III: Amortization (Usually Not Applicable)

Amortization applies to intangible assets acquired in connection with business activity (e.g., goodwill). For rental property, this section is generally not used.

## Vermont-Specific Considerations

### State Income Tax Reconciliation

Vermont taxes your rental income at 8.75%. When you file your Vermont return (Form IN-111), you must reconcile federal depreciation deductions. Vermont does not allow depreciation deductions for real property in the same manner as federal law. This creates a timing difference:

- **Federal (1040-NR):** You deduct $9,090.91 in depreciation
- **Vermont return:** You may need to add back the depreciation and use an alternative basis calculation

Consult a Vermont tax professional or cross-border tax advisor to ensure proper reconciliation.

### Vermont Property Tax Considerations

Vermont's effective property tax rate is approximately 1.9% of fair market value. While not directly related to Form 4562, property taxes are deductible as rental expenses on Schedule E, further reducing your net income. This is separate from depreciation.

### Foreign Tax Credit

As a Canadian resident, you may claim a foreign tax credit on your Canadian T1 return for US federal and state taxes paid. When you file Form 1040-NR and your Vermont return, calculate the total US tax paid. Report this on your Canadian T1 in the appropriate lines for foreign tax credits.

**Important:** Depreciation recapture on future sale of the property will be subject to US capital gains tax at 15% (federal) plus 3.8% net investment income tax, plus 8.75% Vermont state tax. You'll owe this tax even if you defer recognition through the Canada-US Tax Treaty's real property rules. Plan accordingly.

## Common Mistakes Canadian Landlords Make

1. **Including Land in Depreciable Basis:** Many landlords depreciate the full purchase price. The IRS will disallow this. Separate land from building value before completing Form 4562.

2. **Starting Depreciation in the Wrong Year:** Depreciation begins when the property is placed in service (ready to rent), not when purchased. If you purchased in December 2023 but didn't rent until March 2024, depreciation starts in March.

3. **Not Filing Form 4562 at All:** Some Canadian landlords report rental income on Schedule E without supporting Form 4562. The IRS increasingly scrutinizes nonresident alien returns. Always file the supporting form.

4. **Forgetting Mid-Month Convention:** If you placed property in service mid-month, you don't depreciate a full 12 months in Year 1. Calculate the exact months and days of service.

5. **Mixing Repair Costs with Depreciation:** Capital improvements (roof replacement, addition) depreciate over time. Ordinary repairs deduct fully in the year incurred. Don't depreciate both.

6. **Ignoring Vermont State Tax Implications:** Failing to reconcile federal and Vermont depreciation creates audit risk on both returns.
