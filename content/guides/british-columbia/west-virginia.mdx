## US Rental Property Taxation for BC Residents: A West Virginia Case Study

As a British Columbia resident earning rental income from a West Virginia property, you're navigating two distinct tax systems simultaneously. The United States taxes you on US-source income, while the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) taxes your worldwide income. West Virginia adds a third layer of complexity with its own state income tax. Understanding this three-way obligation—and how they interact—is essential to avoiding penalties and optimizing your tax position.

This guide walks through the specific reporting requirements, tax rates, and deadlines you'll encounter.

## Understanding the Tax Landscape

### Why BC and West Virginia Create Unique Obligations

British Columbia doesn't levy a provincial income tax on out-of-province rental income, but the CRA does tax your worldwide income at federal rates (currently 15%, 20.5%, 26%, 29%, and 33% depending on bracket). Meanwhile, West Virginia imposes a 6.5% state income tax on all rental income earned within its borders, and the US federal government taxes non-residents at either 30% or lower rates under an election (Section 871(d)).

The key challenge: these jurisdictions don't automatically coordinate. You could face withholding at one level, claim a foreign tax credit at another, and still owe money at a third. Proper structuring and timely filing prevent double taxation and penalties.

### Currency Conversion

All US income must be converted to Canadian dollars on your CRA return. For 2025 tax year income, use the Bank of Canada annual average rate of 1 USD = 1.36 CAD (or the daily rate on which you received payment, if you elected to do so in a prior year). Document your conversion method consistently year to year.

## CRA Obligations for BC Landlords

### T776 and Schedule 11 Reporting

You must report all US rental income on Form T776 (Statement of Real Estate Rentals), which attaches to your T1 general return. Convert all US-dollar amounts to CAD using the rate above.

**On Schedule 11, report:**
- Gross rents received (converted to CAD)
- Property expenses (utilities, repairs, insurance, property management fees, property tax, mortgage interest)
- Capital cost allowance (CCA) if you elect to claim depreciation
- Net rental income or loss

The CRA expects you to use the accrual method unless you receive CRA permission otherwise. This means reporting rent when it's earned, not when cash is received.

### Form T1135: Foreign Property Reporting

If your West Virginia property is worth more than CAD $100,000 (at fair market value on June 30 of the tax year), you must file Form T1135 (Foreign Income Verification Statement). Report:
- Description of the property (address, property type)
- Jurisdiction (West Virginia, USA)
- Fair market value in CAD
- Income earned during the year (converted to CAD)
- Cost basis in CAD (original purchase price converted)

Failure to file Form T1135 triggers a minimum penalty of $25 per month (up to $2,500 per return) plus potential gross negligence penalties.

### Foreign Tax Credit Claim

You'll likely pay West Virginia state tax and US federal tax. Both are claimable as foreign tax credits on your CRA return to reduce double taxation.

**On Form T1116 (Calculation of Allowable Dividend Tax Credit and Foreign Tax Credit):**
- Report all US federal and WV state income taxes paid
- Calculate the Canadian tax owing on that US income
- Claim the lesser of US tax paid or Canadian tax on that income

The foreign tax credit is non-refundable, so it can reduce your Canadian tax to zero but won't create a refund if credits exceed tax owing.

## US Federal Tax Obligations

### Obtaining an ITIN

The IRS will not accept a Social Insurance Number (SIN) from a non-resident Canadian. You must apply for an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN) before filing a US tax return.

**Apply using Form W-7 (Application for IRS Individual Taxpayer Identification Number):**
- Submit with photocopies of your passport and proof of West Virginia property ownership (deed, property tax bill)
- Mail to the IRS ITIN processing center for your region
- Processing typically takes 4–6 weeks
- ITINs are issued within days if you apply in person at an IRS Taxpayer Assistance Center in the US (though few exist now)

Once issued, use this ITIN on all future US tax filings. ITINs remain valid for five years of non-filing before expiration.

### Form 1040-NR: Non-Resident Alien Tax Return

You must file Form 1040-NR (U.S. Income Tax Return for Nonresident Alien Individuals) if you have US rental income above the filing threshold. For 2024 tax year, the threshold is $1,050 gross income if married filing jointly (and no investment income subject to different rules) or lower amounts for single filers.

**On Form 1040-NR:**
- Report gross rents on Schedule E (Supplemental Income and Loss)
- Deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses (maintenance, property management, insurance, property tax, mortgage interest, utilities, cleaning, repairs)
- Report net rental income
- Include total US income, and calculate US federal tax

Non-resident aliens use the same tax brackets as US citizens but may not claim the standard deduction—instead, you itemize or take a reduced standard deduction tied to investment income.

### Schedule E: Rental Property Details

Attach Schedule E to report:
- Property address (West Virginia property address)
- Rent received
- Each deductible expense category
- Net income or loss
- Depreciation (if electing CCA/MACRS)

### Section 871(d) Election

**Critical strategy:** Most non-resident landlords file Form 8288-B (U.S. Real Property Interest Gross Income Election) to elect Section 871(d) treatment. This allows you to:
- Report net rental income (not gross rents) for withholding purposes
- Reduce withholding from 30% of gross to approximately 15%–20% of net income
- Avoid excess withholding refund delays

**How it works:**
- File Form 8288-B with your Form 1040-NR
- Notify your property manager or tenant to withhold tax on net income (rent minus allowed expenses) rather than gross rents
- Include a signed copy with any third-party payer (tenant or management company)

Without this election, the default IRS withholding is 30% of gross rents—a substantial cash-flow drag if you have significant deductible expenses.

## West Virginia State Tax Obligations

### West Virginia Income Tax

West Virginia imposes a 6.5% income tax on rental income earned within the state. Non-resident individuals must file Form NR-1 (West Virginia Non-Resident Individual Income Tax Return) if gross income from WV exceeds $1,000 for tax year 2024 (verify current threshold on the WV Department of Revenue website annually).

**On Form NR-1:**
- Report West Virginia-source rental income in CAD, converted at the same rate used for the CRA
- Deduct ordinary and necessary rental expenses
- Calculate West Virginia tax at 6.5%
- Claim any West Virginia tax paid as part of your foreign tax credit on the CRA return

### West Virginia Property Tax

West Virginia property tax is assessed at the county level. The statewide average effective rate is 0.59% of fair market value, but rates vary by county (typically 0.5% to 0.8%). Property tax is deductible against rental income on both the IRS Schedule E and the CRA T776.

Obtain a property tax statement from the West Virginia county assessor's office and deduct the annual property tax bill (converted to CAD) on both forms.

## Selling the Property: FIRPTA Overview

When you sell the West Virginia property, the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA) applies. The US buyer's closing agent must withhold **15% of the gross sale price** and remit to the IRS, unless an exemption applies or you file Form 8288 (Application for Withholding Certificate for Dispositions by Foreign Persons).

**Key points:**
- Withholding is on gross sale proceeds (not net gain)
- The 15% is a withholding, not the final tax—you'll report the actual gain on Form 1040-NR
- File Form 8288 before closing to reduce or eliminate withholding if your expected tax liability is lower
- Report the sale on both your CRA return (T776 as a sale of property) and the IRS return (Form 4797 or Schedule D, depending on your classification)
