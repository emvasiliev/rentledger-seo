## US Rental Property Ownership for Quebec Residents: Virginia Tax Guide

If you're a Quebec resident earning rental income from property in Virginia, you're subject to tax compliance in three jurisdictions: Canada (CRA), the United States (IRS), and Virginia. Each has distinct filing requirements, deadlines, and rates. Understanding these obligations is essential to avoid penalties and optimize your tax position.

### Why This Combination Matters

As a non-resident alien (NRA) in the US, you don't benefit from standard deductions or personal exemptions available to US citizens. Instead, your US rental income is taxed on a net basis (after deductions) if you make a Section 871(d) election, or on a gross basis (25% withholding without proper filings). Simultaneously, Canada treats your worldwide income as taxable, requiring you to report US rental income in Canadian dollars and potentially claim foreign tax credits for US taxes paid. Virginia adds a third layer: a 5.75% state income tax that compounds your overall effective rate.

This guide walks you through each jurisdiction's requirements in practical order.

## Canadian Tax Obligations (CRA)

### Form T776 — Rental Income Statement

You must file Form T776 annually with your personal tax return (T1 General) if you have rental income. On this form, you'll report:

- **Gross rental income** from your Virginia property (converted to Canadian dollars using the Bank of Canada annual average exchange rate for 2025: 1 USD = 1.36 CAD)
- **Expenses** (mortgage interest, property tax, insurance, utilities, repairs, property management fees, advertising)
- **Capital cost allowance (CCA)** if you claim depreciation

**Key point:** Report gross rents in USD first, then multiply by 1.36 to convert. For ongoing expense tracking, use daily Bank of Canada rates; reconcile annually using the year-end average rate for the summary on T776.

### Form T1135 — Foreign Property Disclosure

If your Virginia property's cost exceeds CAD $100,000 (at the time of acquisition), you must file Form T1135 annually. This form requires:

- Cost amount of the property in Canadian dollars
- Fair market value at year-end
- Country of location (USA)
- Income generated (in CAD)

**Filing deadline:** The same day as your personal tax return (typically June 15, 2025 for 2024 income; however, payment is due April 30, 2025).

### Foreign Tax Credit (Form T2209)

This is critical. You'll pay US federal and state taxes on your Virginia rental income. Canada allows a foreign tax credit to prevent double taxation.

**How it works:**
1. Calculate Canadian tax on the US rental income (included in your total worldwide income)
2. Calculate total US federal and Virginia state taxes paid on that income
3. Claim the lesser of (a) US taxes paid, or (b) Canadian tax attributable to that income

File Form T2209 to calculate and claim this credit. Attach copies of your US tax returns (1040-NR and Virginia return) as supporting documentation.

## US Federal Tax Obligations (IRS)

### Obtain an ITIN

Before you can file a US tax return, you need an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN). Canadians use Form W-7 to apply. You can:

- File Form W-7 with your first US tax return (Form 1040-NR), or
- Apply for an ITIN in advance through a Certified Acceptance Agent in Canada or the US

**Processing time:** 6–9 weeks. Obtain your ITIN before the tax deadline to avoid delays.

### Form 1040-NR — US Non-Resident Income Tax Return

File this form annually to report your US rental income. On Form 1040-NR:

- Report gross rents on **Schedule E (Supplemental Income or Loss)**, Part III
- Claim rental expenses (mortgage interest, property tax, utilities, repairs, depreciation)
- Calculate net rental income
- Report on Form 1040-NR, line 21a (or applicable line for business income)

**Key rates:**
- **Federal long-term capital gains:** 0%, 15%, or 20% (depending on income)
- **Federal ordinary income tax:** 10% to 37% (graduated brackets for 2024)
- **Self-employment tax (SE tax):** Generally not applicable to passive rental income

### Section 871(d) Election — Avoid 30% Gross Withholding

Without action, a non-resident's rental income is subject to a 30% US federal withholding tax on **gross** rents (before deductions). This is punitive.

**The fix:** File Form 8288-B (Statement of Withholding on Dispositions by Foreign Persons) or include a statement with your return electing under Section 871(d) of the Internal Revenue Code. This election allows you to be taxed on **net** rental income (after deductions) at graduated tax rates instead.

**What you must do:**
- Include a clear statement: "The taxpayer elects under IRC Section 871(d) to be taxed on a net basis on US real property income."
- Attach it to your Form 1040-NR
- Once made, the election applies to all subsequent years unless revoked

**Impact:** Filing with this election typically results in a much lower tax liability than 30% on gross rents.

### Filing Deadline and ITIN Requirement

- **IRS deadline:** June 15, 2025 for 2024 income (automatic 2-month extension for non-residents; can extend to October 15)
- **Payment due:** June 15, 2025 (no extension for payment)

## Virginia State Tax Obligations

### Virginia Non-Resident Income Tax Return

Virginia requires non-residents with Virginia-source income to file Form 760 (Virginia Individual Income Tax Return). You'll report:

- Gross Virginia rental income (in USD)
- Virginia-source deductions (portion of expenses allocable to Virginia income)
- Virginia state tax: **5.75%** on net Virginia rental income

**Note:** Virginia does not allow deduction of federal income taxes paid. However, Virginia does allow a credit for tax paid to another state if you own property in multiple states; this doesn't apply if Virginia is your only US property state.

**Filing deadline:** June 15, 2025 for 2024 income; extensions available.

### Virginia Property Tax

Virginia's average effective property tax rate is **0.82%** of fair market value, though rates vary by locality. Property tax is deductible on both your Form 1040-NR and your Virginia Form 760, reducing both federal and state liability.

## Selling Your Virginia Property (FIRPTA)

When you sell the property, US federal law (Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act, or FIRPTA) requires the buyer to withhold **15% of the sale price** and remit it to the IRS on your behalf. This withholding applies unless you qualify for an exemption.

### How It Works

1. You list the property for sale and find a buyer
2. Before closing, provide a Form W-8IMY (Certificate of Foreign Status of Beneficial Owner) to the title company or buyer's attorney
3. The buyer's attorney withholds 15% of the gross sale price
4. That withholding is credited against your final US income tax bill for the year of sale
5. File a final Form 1040-NR reporting the sale (using Form 4797 if applicable, or Schedule D for gains/losses)

**Capital gains treatment:** Your gain is taxed as a US capital gain at federal rates (0%, 15%, or 20%). Virginia also taxes capital gains on real property at 5.75%.

## Key Takeaways for Quebec Landlords

- **Report in three jurisdictions:** File T776 and T1135 with CRA, Form 1040-NR with the IRS (with Section 871(d) election to avoid 30% gross withholding), and Form 760 with Virginia.
