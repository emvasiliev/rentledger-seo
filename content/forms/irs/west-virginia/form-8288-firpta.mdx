# Form 8288: FIRPTA Withholding for Canadian Landlords Selling West Virginia Property

## What Is Form 8288?

Form 8288 (US Withholding Tax Return for Dispositions by Foreign Persons of US Real Property Interests) is the Internal Revenue Service form used to report and remit federal withholding tax when a foreign person (including Canadian citizens and residents) sells US real property. Under the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA), buyers of US property from foreign sellers must withhold 15% of the gross purchase price and remit it to the IRS within 20 days of closing.

For Canadian landlords, this withholding is a critical compliance requirement. While the 15% federal withholding may eventually be reduced or eliminated on your US tax return through foreign tax credits and Canadian taxation coordination, failing to ensure proper Form 8288 filing can trigger substantial penalties and complications with both US and Canadian tax authorities.

## How FIRPTA Withholding Applies in West Virginia

West Virginia presents a two-layer tax scenario for Canadian landlords selling rental property: federal FIRPTA withholding and West Virginia state tax obligations.

**Federal FIRPTA Withholding (15%)**

When you sell West Virginia rental property, your buyer is statutorily required to withhold 15% of the total gross sale price under FIRPTA. This applies regardless of:
- Whether the property is residential or commercial
- Whether you have losses or expenses
- Your actual tax liability

The calculation is straightforward: if your West Virginia rental property sells for $500,000, the buyer must withhold $75,000 and remit it via Form 8288 to the IRS.

**West Virginia State Considerations**

West Virginia imposes a 6.5% state income tax on rental income earned by non-residents. While this is an income tax (not a withholding obligation at sale), it intersects importantly with your overall US tax liability. The state requires non-resident landlords to file Form IT-140-NR (West Virginia Non-Resident Individual Income Tax Return) annually while you own the property. Upon sale, capital gain from the disposition is generally subject to West Virginia taxation at the same 6.5% rate, though you may be eligible for certain credits depending on how West Virginia defines gain on real property disposition.

Additionally, West Virginia's effective property tax rate of 0.59% applies to the appraised value of rental properties, affecting your annual deductions and overall investment returns.

**Interaction with Canada-US Tax Treaty**

Under Article XIII of the Canada-US Income Tax Treaty, the US retains primary taxing rights on gains from the disposition of real property situated in the US. Canada generally exempts the capital gain from Canadian tax (subject to certain exceptions), meaning your Canadian tax liability typically arises only through the US system. The 15% FIRPTA withholding is a prepayment of US federal tax. When you file your US Form 1040-NR (Non-resident Alien Individual Income Tax Return) for the year of sale, you'll report the actual gain and may receive a refund if the 15% withholding exceeds your actual federal tax liability.

## Who Files Form 8288?

**The Buyer's Obligation**

Technically, the **buyer** of your West Virginia property is responsible for preparing and filing Form 8288. The buyer must:
- Obtain your taxpayer identification information (typically your US Individual Taxpayer Identification Number, or ITIN, if you don't have a Social Security Number)
- Calculate the 15% withholding
- Remit the withheld amount to the IRS within 20 days of closing
- File Form 8288 with the IRS within that same 20-day window

Many West Virginia real estate closings involve a title company or escrow agent. These intermediaries often facilitate FIRPTA withholding on behalf of the buyer, though the legal obligation remains with the buyer.

**Your Role as the Seller**

While the buyer files Form 8288, you have critical responsibilities:

1. **Providing Tax Information**: Supply your ITIN or SSN to the buyer/closing agent in writing (typically using Form W-9 or its equivalent).

2. **Requesting a Withholding Certificate (Optional)**: You can apply for a reduced or eliminated withholding by filing Form 8288-B (Application for Withholding Certificate) with the IRS *before* the property closes. If the IRS issues a withholding certificate showing a lower withholding rate or amount, the buyer must honor it and withhold only the amount specified on the certificate. This is particularly valuable if your actual tax liability will be much lower than 15% of the sale price.

3. **Monitoring Compliance**: Ensure the buyer confirms withholding and remittance; request documentation of the withheld amount for your US tax return.

## Step-by-Step Completion of Form 8288

Although the buyer completes Form 8288, understanding its structure helps you verify accuracy and coordinate with the buyer's tax representative.

**Key Sections of Form 8288:**

**Part I: Transferor Information**
- Name, address, and TIN of the foreign person (you, the Canadian seller)
- Certification that you are a foreign person

**Part II: Property Description**
- Street address of the West Virginia property
- Property identification/parcel number
- Brief property description (e.g., "rental apartment building")

**Part III: Transfer Information**
- Date of transfer (closing date)
- Selling price (gross consideration)
- 15% withheld amount (calculated as 15% × selling price)
- Any adjustments or exemptions (rarely applicable)

**Part IV: Transferee Information**
- Name, address, and TIN of the buyer (the entity purchasing the property)

**Part V: Certification**
- The buyer's representative signs and dates the form

**Filing Procedure:**

The buyer (or their agent) submits Form 8288 to the IRS Centralized Processing location. In 2024, this is typically the:
- **Internal Revenue Service, P.O. Box 409101, Rockville, MD 20849-9101** (for paper filing)
- Or electronically through the IRS e-Services system (if the buyer qualifies)

The buyer must also send a copy to you, the seller, and to the State of West Virginia (if West Virginia requires state-level reporting on related transactions).

## West Virginia-Specific Considerations

**State-Level FIRPTA Coordination**

West Virginia does not impose a separate FIRPTA-style withholding at the state level. However, the state's 6.5% income tax applies to capital gains from real property sales. If your West Virginia property was used as a rental, the gain is likely subject to state tax. Some buyers or title companies in West Virginia may not be familiar with the distinction between federal FIRPTA withholding and state tax liability, so clarify that:

- Federal Form 8288 remits only to the IRS, not to West Virginia
- Your West Virginia state tax liability must be addressed separately when filing your West Virginia return (or an amended return if you did not file one while owning the property)

**Non-Resident Reporting**

If you held the West Virginia property for rental income, you likely filed Form IT-140-NR annually. Upon sale, you must file a final Form IT-140-NR for the tax year of the disposition, reporting the capital gain. The basis step-up, depreciation recapture, and allocation of gain between real property and personal property can be complex; consult a cross-border accountant.

**Real Property Tax Refunds**

West Virginia may provide a prorated property tax refund if the property taxes have already been paid for the full tax year but you sell mid-year. Coordinate this refund timing with the closing proceeds and withholding calculations.

**Title Company Practices**

West Virginia title companies vary in their FIRPTA expertise. Confirm in writing with the closing agent well before closing that:
- They understand Form 8288 requirements
- They will obtain your ITIN/SSN
- They will withhold 15% (or the amount on any withholding certificate you obtain)
- They will remit and file within 20 days
- They will provide you with documentation of the withheld amount
