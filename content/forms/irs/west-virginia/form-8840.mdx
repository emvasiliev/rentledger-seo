# Form 8840: Closer Connection Exception Statement for Canadian Landlords in West Virginia

## What Is Form 8840?

Form 8840 is an IRS document that allows certain foreign nationals to claim an exception to the Substantial Presence Test (SPT)—a mechanical rule that can classify you as a US resident for tax purposes based purely on the number of days you spend in the United States.

Without Form 8840, if you spend:
- 183 or more days in the US in the current year, OR
- 183 or more days in the US averaged over the current and two preceding years (weighted formula),

you would be classified as a US resident alien for tax purposes, triggering worldwide income taxation by the IRS. Form 8840 allows you to prove that despite meeting the SPT, you maintain a "closer connection" to Canada—your true tax home—and therefore should not be taxed as a US resident.

This distinction is critical for Canadian landlords with US rental properties, as it determines whether you file as a nonresident or resident alien, affects your foreign tax credit eligibility, and impacts how much US tax you owe on rental income.

## How the Substantial Presence Test Works

The SPT uses a weighted formula:

- Days present in current year = 1 day each
- Days present in prior year = 1/3 day each
- Days present in year before that = 1/6 day each

If the total equals or exceeds 183 "weighted" days, you meet the SPT and are presumed to be a US resident alien.

**Example:** A Canadian landlord in West Virginia spends 150 days in 2024, 120 days in 2023, and 90 days in 2022. The calculation is:
- (150 × 1) + (120 × 1/3) + (90 × 1/6) = 150 + 40 + 15 = 205 days

This landlord meets the SPT and must file Form 8840 to claim closer connection status.

## How Form 8840 Applies to West Virginia Landlords

West Virginia presents unique circumstances for Canadian landlords because:

1. **State-Level Tax Requirements:** West Virginia imposes a 6.5% income tax on rental income. Non-residents must file a West Virginia state income tax return (Form IT-40) if they have WV-source income. Form 8840 does not eliminate this requirement; however, it prevents federal classification as a US resident, which can simplify your overall US tax filing position.

2. **Rental Income Sourcing:** Rental income from West Virginia property is automatically sourced to West Virginia under IRC §861-865. You cannot avoid West Virginia taxation through Form 8840, but you may qualify for foreign tax credits on your Canadian return for WV state taxes paid.

3. **Property Tax Obligations:** West Virginia's effective property tax rate averages 0.59%, one of the lowest in the nation. This does not trigger SPT day-counting, but combined with your rental income, it establishes economic ties to the state that Form 8840 may reference.

4. **Canada-US Tax Treaty Considerations:** The Canada-US Tax Treaty (Article IV, Resident) allows you to claim tie-breaker tests if you meet the definition of resident in both countries. Form 8840 is the vehicle through which you assert these tie-breakers for US purposes. The tie-breaker tests include:
   - Permanent home available in one country (typically Canada for a Canadian citizen)
   - Habitual center of vital interests (employment, investments, family)
   - Habitual abode
   - Citizenship (Canadian citizens are presumed tied to Canada)

## Who Must File Form 8840

You must file Form 8840 if **all** of the following apply:

1. You are not a US citizen or green card holder (as a Canadian, you meet this)
2. You meet the Substantial Presence Test for the calendar year
3. You claim closer connection to a country other than the US (Canada)
4. You want to be treated as a nonresident alien for federal tax purposes

**You do not file Form 8840 if:**
- You do not meet the SPT
- You have a US green card (you're treated as resident regardless)
- You choose to be taxed as a US resident (some filers prefer this for treaty benefits)

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form 8840

### Part I: Personal Information
- Enter your name, US taxpayer ID (ITIN if you don't have an SSN), and the tax year.
- Provide your Canadian address (your permanent home).

### Part II: Closer Connection Information
This is the substance of the form. You must establish:

**Question 1: When Did Your US Stay Begin?**
Enter the date you first entered the US for the tax year in question.

**Question 2: Do You Have a US Tax Home?**
Answer "No." This is critical. A "tax home" is where you have a job or business. As a non-resident landlord, your employment and primary business interests are in Canada. Rental property ownership alone does not constitute a tax home; you do not actively manage the property in person.

**Question 3: Did You File a US Tax Return as a Resident?**
Answer "No" (if filing Form 8840 for the first time in this year).

**Question 4: Do You Have Immediate Family in the US?**
Answer based on facts. If your spouse or dependent children reside in Canada full-time, answer "No."

**Question 5: Do You Have Personal Possessions or Property in the US?**
You may indicate the rental property, but emphasize that your principal residence and personal effects remain in Canada.

**Question 6: Do You Have Social and Political Ties to the US?**
Be specific. Do you vote in Canadian federal elections? Maintain provincial health insurance? Hold a Canadian driver's license? Have active memberships in Canadian clubs, professional associations, or community organizations? This is where Canadian landlords strongly satisfy the tie-breaker test.

### Part III: Declaration
Sign and date the form. You must attach:
- A copy of your Canadian tax return (Notice of Assessment from CRA) or a letter from your Canadian employer confirming your Canadian employment/residence
- Documentation of your permanent residence in Canada (e.g., utility bills, lease, property deed)
- Proof of provincial health insurance or driver's license
- A statement explaining why your tax home is in Canada

## West Virginia-Specific Considerations

### State Income Tax Filing
Filing Form 8840 does not exempt you from West Virginia state taxes. You must still file Form IT-40 (West Virginia Nonresident Income Tax Return) and pay the 6.5% tax on rental income. However, form 8840 ensures you're treated as a nonresident for federal purposes, which simplifies your total US filing burden.

### FIRPTA Requirements
If you eventually sell the West Virginia property, the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA) will require withholding of 15% of sale proceeds unless you claim an exemption. This is separate from Form 8840 and applies regardless of resident status.

### Form IT-40 Coordination
Ensure your Form IT-40 (West Virginia) and Form 1040-NR (US federal) are aligned on your filing position. Both should reflect nonresident alien status if Form 8840 is approved.

### Foreign Tax Credit
West Virginia state income tax paid is eligible for credit against your Canadian federal tax under the foreign tax credit mechanism (ITA §126). Report this on Form T2209 when filing your Canadian T1 return. This prevents double taxation on the same rental income.

## Common Mistakes Canadian Landlords Make

1. **Failing to File Form 8840 at All:** Many Canadian landlords overlook this form entirely, defaulting to US resident status unnecessarily. The SPT is automatic; you must actively claim the exception.

2. **Inconsistent Tie-Breaker Evidence:** Submitting evidence that contradicts the closer connection claim. For example, claiming no US tax home while reporting business income from a US entity will weaken your position.

3. **Weak Documentation of Canadian Ties:** Simply asserting you are Canadian is insufficient. Attach concrete evidence: Canadian tax returns, provincial health cards, employer letters, or community memberships.

4. **Missing the June 15 Deadline:** Form 8840 must be filed by June 15 of the year following the tax year in question. Missing this deadline may require amended filings and penalties.

5. **Not Filing WV State Returns:** Assuming Form 8840 eliminates West Virginia state tax obligations. You must separately file and pay WV income tax on rental income.

6. **Overlooking the Canada-US Tax Treaty:** Not leveraging Article IV tie-breaker tests in your Form 8840 narrative. Explicitly reference the treaty in your supporting statement.
