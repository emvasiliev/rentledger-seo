# Form 1040-NR for Canadian Landlords: Maine Rental Property Guide

## What Is Form 1040-NR?

Form 1040-NR (U.S. Nonresident Alien Income Tax Return) is the primary federal income tax return filed by non-resident aliens—including Canadian citizens—who have U.S.-source income subject to taxation. For Canadian landlords with rental property in Maine, this form becomes necessary when you elect under Internal Revenue Code Section 871(d) to treat your rental income as "effectively connected income" (ECI). This election allows you to deduct ordinary and necessary rental expenses directly against your rental income, rather than facing a flat 30% withholding rate on gross rental receipts.

Without the 871(d) election, rental income would be subject to a 30% federal withholding tax under IRC Section 871(a), with limited deduction opportunities. By making the election and filing 1040-NR, you can significantly reduce your actual U.S. tax liability.

## How Form 1040-NR Applies to Maine Rental Property

Maine presents a unique situation for Canadian landlords due to its proximity to Atlantic Canada, competitive real estate markets in areas like Portland and Acadia, and a straightforward state tax structure. Here's what makes Maine-specific:

### Federal Taxation (1040-NR)

When you own a rental property in Maine and make the 871(d) election, your net rental income becomes effectively connected income taxable at federal rates (10% to 37%, depending on income level). You report Maine rental income on Form 1040-NR, Schedule C (if you operate as a sole proprietor) or the equivalent business income section, with full deduction rights for:

- Mortgage interest
- Property taxes (Maine's average effective rate is 1.36%, among the highest nationally)
- Repairs and maintenance
- Insurance
- Utilities (if you cover them)
- Property management fees
- Depreciation
- HOA fees (if applicable)

### Maine State Income Tax (8.92% Corporate or 7.15% Individual)

**Critical point:** Maine also imposes state income tax on rental income at a rate of **7.15%** for individuals (or 8.92% for S-corporations, depending on your structure). You must file **Maine Form 1040-ME** (Maine Individual Income Tax Return) or equivalent state return to report the same rental income and claim the same deductions. Maine allows Canadian residents to claim foreign tax credits for income taxes paid to Canada, subject to limitations under both Maine and federal law.

Maine does **not** tax non-residents on out-of-state income, but rental property income is considered Maine-source income, making you subject to state tax regardless of residency.

## Who Must File Form 1040-NR: Maine Edition

You must file Form 1040-NR if:

1. You are a Canadian citizen or permanent resident without U.S. citizenship or green card status
2. You own rental property in Maine generating net income
3. You have elected—or plan to elect—under IRC Section 871(d) to treat the rental activity as ECI
4. Your gross rental receipts or net income exceed the standard deduction threshold

**Part-year residents:** If you became a U.S. resident during the year, you may file Form 1040 (the standard U.S. return) rather than 1040-NR for that year and subsequent years. Verify your residency status carefully, as it affects filing requirements.

**Dual filers under the Canada-US Tax Treaty:** If you hold a green card or have substantial presence in the U.S., you may be required to file 1040-NR or 1040 and claim treaty benefits to avoid double taxation on the same income.

## Step-by-Step: Completing Form 1040-NR for Maine Rentals

### Step 1: Make the IRC Section 871(d) Election

Before filing 1040-NR, you must formally elect to treat rental income as ECI. This is typically done by:
- Filing Form 8288-B (Statement of Withholding on Dispositions by Foreign Persons) or attaching a statement to your 1040-NR explicitly claiming the 871(d) election
- Keeping records of this election for future years (it applies to the tax year and all subsequent years unless revoked)

### Step 2: Calculate Gross Rental Income

Report all Maine rental income:
- Monthly lease payments (use accrual or cash method consistently)
- Tenant deposits kept as income (do not include refundable deposits)
- Utilities charged back to tenants
- Parking fees or amenity charges
- Late fees collected (taxable unless part of penalty abatement)

**Example:** A Portland duplex generating $24,000 annually ($1,000/month × 2 units × 12 months).

### Step 3: Claim Allowable Deductions

On Schedule C (or equivalent business income section):

| Deduction Category | Maine Example | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Mortgage interest | $8,000 | Deductible; principal payments are not |
| Property taxes | ~$2,000 (at 1.36% effective rate on $150,000 property) | Maine has higher-than-average property taxes |
| Insurance (hazard, liability) | $1,200 | Must be ordinary and necessary |
| Utilities (if landlord-paid) | $1,800 | Common in Maine multi-family properties |
| Repairs & maintenance | $1,500 | Ongoing maintenance; capital improvements depreciated separately |
| Depreciation | $5,454 | Based on building value (27.5 years residential) |
| Property management | $2,400 | If using a management company (typical fee: 10% of rent) |

**Depreciation note:** You depreciate the building structure (not land). For a $200,000 property with $30,000 allocated to land, depreciate $170,000 ÷ 27.5 years = $6,182 annually.

### Step 4: Calculate Net Rental Income (NRI)

Gross Income − Deductions = Net Rental Income (subject to federal and Maine tax)

**Example calculation:**
- Gross rent: $24,000
- Deductions: $22,354
- Net rental income: $1,646 (federal and Maine taxable income)

### Step 5: Complete Form 1040-NR

**Line-by-line for Maine context:**

- **Name and address:** Your Canadian home address (claim foreign address credit if applicable)
- **Part III (Tax Computation):** Report net rental income on the appropriate line (typically Schedule C net income)
- **Schedule NEC:** If you have no U.S. wages subject to withholding, check this box
- **Foreign Address:** You will include your Canadian address; do not file as a "U.S. resident"
- **ITIN or SSN:** Use your ITIN (Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) if you don't have an SSN; apply for an ITIN using Form W-7 if needed

### Step 6: File Federal Return and Claim Treaty Benefits

File 1040-NR with the IRS (address: Kansas City, MO, or electronically via EFIN). Include:
- Schedule NEC with net rental income
- Supporting schedules showing Maine-specific deductions
- Form 8833 (Treaty-Based Return Position Disclosure) if claiming Canadian-US Tax Treaty benefits

### Step 7: File Maine State Return

Simultaneously, file **Maine Form 1040-ME** with the State Tax Assessor, reporting the same net rental income and claiming Maine deductions. Maine recognizes IRC deductions and follows federal definitions closely.

## Maine-Specific Considerations

### Property Tax Complexity

Maine's effective property tax rate of 1.36% is higher than the U.S. average (0.92%). Verify your assessed property value through the local town assessor's office. Property taxes are fully deductible on 1040-NR and 1040-ME.

### State Foreign Tax Credit (Maine)

Maine allows non-residents to claim foreign tax credits for Canadian federal and provincial income taxes paid on the same rental income. However:
- The credit is limited to Maine tax on that income
- Calculate separately: Canadian tax paid on Maine rental income ÷ Canadian total income × Canadian tax = allowable credit (subject to Maine limitation)
- Claim on Maine Form 1040-ME

### No Maine Surtax on Non-Residents

Maine does **not** impose the federal Net Investment Income Tax (3.8% NIIT) or the Maine higher-income surtax on non-residents, despite recent state proposals. Verify current law annually, as this may change.
