# Form W-7 Guide for Canadian Landlords with North Dakota Rental Property

## What is Form W-7?

Form W-7, Application for IRS Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN), is the official IRS application used by non-US citizens and non-resident aliens to obtain a US taxpayer identification number. An ITIN functions like a Social Security Number (SSN) for federal tax purposes but does not authorize work or residency in the United States.

For Canadian landlords, an ITIN is essential when:

- Filing US federal income tax returns as a non-resident alien (Form 1040-NR)
- Complying with Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA) withholding requirements on property sales
- Claiming tax treaty benefits under the Canada-US Tax Treaty
- Meeting state tax obligations in jurisdictions requiring a US tax ID

The ITIN is issued by the Internal Revenue Service and remains valid for five years of non-use before expiring (though recent IRS guidance has extended some expiration periods).

## How Form W-7 Applies Specifically to North Dakota Rental Property

### Federal Tax Obligations

Canadian landlords earning rental income from North Dakota property are classified as non-resident aliens for US federal tax purposes. You must file Form 1040-NR (US Nonresident Alien Income Tax Return) annually, reporting:

- Gross rental income
- Deductible expenses (mortgage interest, property taxes, maintenance, depreciation)
- Net rental income subject to US taxation

Without an ITIN, you cannot file this return or claim tax treaty benefits, which is critical because the Canada-US Tax Treaty allows Canadian residents to defer gains on principal residences and may reduce withholding obligations.

### North Dakota State Tax Obligations

North Dakota imposes a state income tax of **2.5% on rental income earned within the state**. Non-resident landlords must file Form ND-1 (North Dakota Individual Income Tax Return for Non-Residents and Part-Year Residents), reporting:

- Rental income sourced to North Dakota
- Allowable deductions allocated to ND income
- State tax liability calculated at the 2.5% flat rate

An ITIN is required by North Dakota to process your non-resident return and establish compliance records.

### FIRPTA Considerations

If you sell your North Dakota rental property, FIRPTA withholding rules apply. The buyer's closing attorney is required to withhold **15% of the gross sale proceeds** and remit this to the IRS, unless you:

1. Certify that you are a US citizen or resident alien
2. Provide an ITIN and certify that the property sale qualifies for an exemption (sales under $300,000 where the buyer intends to occupy as a residence)

Without an ITIN, the 15% withholding cannot be properly reported or credited against your final tax liability, resulting in unnecessary cash flow pressure.

## Who Must File Form W-7

You must apply for an ITIN if you are:

- A Canadian resident (non-resident alien under US tax law)
- Earning income from North Dakota rental property
- Unable to obtain a US Social Security Number
- Required to file a US tax return (1040-NR) or report FIRPTA transactions

You do **not** need an ITIN if:

- You are a US citizen or lawful permanent resident (green card holder)
- You hold a valid US Social Security Number
- You have no US-source income and no FIRPTA obligations

**Timing note:** You should apply for your ITIN **before** your first tax filing season with North Dakota rental income, or immediately upon entering a sale transaction subject to FIRPTA withholding.

## Step-by-Step Guide to Completing Form W-7

### Step 1: Gather Required Documentation

You will need:

- Your Canadian passport or other government-issued photo ID
- Documentation of US rental property ownership (deed, property tax statement, or title insurance policy)
- Proof of Canadian residence (utility bill, provincial health card, or residential lease)
- If applicable, Form 1040-NR (draft or completed) or documentation of FIRPTA transaction

### Step 2: Complete Form W-7 (Page 1)

**Part I – Personal Information:**

- Line 1: Enter your full legal name as shown on your Canadian passport
- Line 2: Enter your current mailing address (Canadian address is acceptable)
- Line 3: Select your country of birth (Canada)
- Line 4: Enter your date of birth and sex

**Part II – Type of Applicant:**

- Check the box for "Individual applying for an ITIN" (most common for landlords)
- Provide the basis for applying: select "Nonresident alien required to file a US tax return" or "Nonresident alien with US-source income"

### Step 3: Complete Form W-7 (Page 2)

**Part III – Identification:**

- Line 7: Leave blank (no US SSN yet)
- Line 8: Provide your Canadian Social Insurance Number (SIN) if available, or state N/A
- Line 9: Select your country of citizenship (Canada)

**Part IV – Residency:**

- Confirm your Canadian residence address
- Indicate your US visa status (most Canadian landlords have no visa or hold a visitor's status)

### Step 4: Attach Supporting Documents

You must include:

1. **Certified copy of your passport** (government-issued photo ID)
   - The IRS accepts color copies certified by a notary or public official
   - Alternatively, have your documents certified by a Canadian notary and apostilled (certified under the Hague Convention)

2. **Documentation of US tax requirement**
   - Original or copy of draft Form 1040-NR (Schedule E attached, showing North Dakota rental income)
   - Property tax statement from North Dakota County Assessor
   - Deed or title document

3. **Proof of Canadian residency** (utility bill, provincial health card)

### Step 5: Mail Your Application

**Address your Form W-7 to:**

IRS (depending on your mailing location):
- From Canada: **IRS, ITIN Operations, P.O. Box 447, Bensalem, PA 19020, USA**
- Processing time: 4–6 weeks (faster with Form 1040-NR filed simultaneously)

**Do not file electronically**—W-7 applications require original documentation and in-person verification or certified copies.

## North Dakota-Specific Considerations

### Property Tax Deduction Interaction

North Dakota's average effective property tax rate is **0.98%**. On a $300,000 rental property, expect annual property taxes of approximately $2,940. These property taxes are:

- **Fully deductible** on your US Form 1040-NR Schedule E
- **Fully deductible** on your North Dakota Form ND-1
- **Deductible on your Canadian T1 return** as a foreign tax credit (Form T776, Statement of Real Estate Rental Income)

Ensure your ITIN application references these deductions to demonstrate legitimate US tax filing obligations.

### Border State Considerations

As a Canadian resident operating rental property in a border state, you may be subject to enhanced IRS scrutiny regarding:

- Source of funds for property purchase (FATCA reporting by Canadian financial institutions)
- Intent to dispose of the property (capital gains treatment)
- Whether you meet the substantial presence test for residency

Apply for your ITIN proactively (before your first filing) to establish compliant, documented status.

### Coordinating with Canadian Tax Reporting

On your Canadian T1 return (personal income tax), you must:

1. Report gross North Dakota rental income on Form T776, Section 3
2. Claim deductions (property tax, mortgage interest, utilities, repairs, capital cost allowance)
3. Claim a foreign tax credit for US federal and state taxes paid (Schedule 1, Line 405)

Your ITIN ensures the IRS can properly track and coordinate your US filings with Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) information-sharing under the Canada-US Tax Treaty.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Applying too late:** Do not wait until the April 15 filing deadline. Apply 6–8 weeks before filing to allow processing time.

2. **Using photocopies without certification:** The IRS rejects uncertified passport copies. Use a notary or IRS Acceptance Agent.

3. **Incomplete documentation:** Missing property deeds or tax statements will delay processing. Include all supporting documents.

4. **Not claiming treaty benefits on the application:** If you are applying due to FIRPTA, explicitly state this on Part II—it affects processing and withholding exemption eligibility.
