# Form 1040-NR for Canadian Landlords: Rhode Island Rental Property Guide

## What Is Form 1040-NR?

Form 1040-NR (U.S. Nonresident Alien Income Tax Return) is the primary federal income tax return filed by non-resident aliens—including Canadian citizens—who have U.S.-source income subject to U.S. tax. For Canadian landlords owning rental property in the United States, this form becomes essential when you make the **Section 871(d) election** to treat rental income as effectively connected income (ECI).

Without this election, your U.S. rental income would be subject to a flat 30% withholding tax on gross rent with no deduction for expenses. By filing Form 1040-NR and making the Section 871(d) election, you can deduct legitimate rental expenses (mortgage interest, property taxes, maintenance, depreciation, etc.) and pay tax only on net income—a significantly more favorable treatment.

## How Form 1040-NR Applies to Rhode Island Rental Property Owners

Rhode Island presents a unique tax scenario for Canadian landlords:

**Federal Level**: You must report U.S.-source rental income and file Form 1040-NR to make the Section 871(d) election, allowing expense deductions.

**Rhode Island State Level**: Rhode Island imposes a state income tax of **5.99%** on non-resident alien income. As a Canadian landlord with Rhode Island rental property, you are not exempt from state tax and must file a **Rhode Island non-resident tax return** (Form RI-1040NR).

**Interaction with the Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty**: The Income and Agreement between Canada and the United States provides important relief. Under Article XIII (Real Property Income), Canada may tax your Rhode Island rental income. However, the treaty generally allows each country to tax real property income, and Article XXIV addresses relief from double taxation. Canada will typically allow a **foreign tax credit** on your Canadian T1 return for U.S. federal and state taxes paid, provided you meet certain conditions.

## Who Must File Form 1040-NR

You must file Form 1040-NR if:

- You are a Canadian citizen or permanent resident (non-resident alien for U.S. tax purposes)
- You own rental property in Rhode Island or elsewhere in the United States
- You have effectively connected income (ECI) from U.S. rental property
- Your gross income (including rental income) exceeds the filing threshold for your status

The filing requirement applies regardless of whether you have employees or independent contractors. However, if you have **no wages subject to U.S. withholding**, you may extend your deadline to June 15 instead of April 15.

## Step-by-Step Guide to Completing Form 1040-NR for Rhode Island Rental Income

### Step 1: Gather Your Documentation

Collect all rental-related documents:
- Rental income statements from property management company or tenant records
- Mortgage statements (for interest deduction)
- Rhode Island property tax assessments and payments
- Utility bills, insurance policies, maintenance invoices
- Depreciation schedule (Form 4562)
- Capital improvement receipts
- Form 1042-S (if withholding was taken on rental income)
- Canadian T776 calculation (if also filing in Canada)

### Step 2: Complete Header Information

Enter your name, address, and Individual Identification Number (ITIN) or Social Security Number (SSN). If you don't have an ITIN, you must apply using Form W-7 before filing Form 1040-NR.

Check the box for "Nonresident alien" and enter your country of citizenship (Canada).

### Step 3: Report Income on Schedule E

Attach **Schedule E (Supplemental Income or Loss)** to your Form 1040-NR. On Schedule E:

- **Part I**: Report rental real estate income and expenses for the Rhode Island property
- **Gross rent received**: Enter total rental income
- **Expenses**: List mortgage interest, property taxes, utilities, repairs, maintenance, insurance, property management fees, and depreciation
- **Net rental income or loss**: This is your taxable amount

Rhode Island property taxes are significant (average effective rate of 1.63%, among the highest in the nation). Ensure you accurately report all property tax payments made during the tax year.

### Step 4: Calculate Depreciation (Form 4562)

Attach **Form 4562 (Depreciation and Amortization)** to calculate annual depreciation on the building (not land). This is a critical deduction that reduces your net taxable income. Depreciation calculations require:

- Building cost basis (purchase price minus land value)
- Improvement date
- Depreciation method (generally 27.5 years for residential rental property)

### Step 5: Determine Your Filing Status and Deductions

As a non-resident alien, your standard deduction is **zero**. However, you are eligible for the same itemized deductions as U.S. residents if you itemize. For rental property owners, you typically use the expenses reported on Schedule E rather than itemizing.

### Step 6: Calculate Tax and Make the Section 871(d) Election

Your taxable income is the net rental income from Schedule E. Calculate your federal tax using the 2024 tax rates applicable to non-resident aliens (generally the same rates as U.S. citizens, but on ECI only).

**Critically**: To make the Section 871(d) election, you must file Form 8288 (U.S. Withholding Tax Return for Dispositions by Foreign Persons of U.S. Real Property Interests) and include it with your Form 1040-NR. This election ensures that:
- Your rental income is treated as effectively connected income
- You can deduct expenses
- You are taxed at regular rates on net income rather than 30% on gross rent

### Step 7: Calculate Rhode Island State Tax

On your **Rhode Island Form RI-1040NR**:

- Report the same net rental income from federal return
- Calculate state tax at **5.99%** on Rhode Island-source income
- Rhode Island allows the same federal deductions and credits

A portion of your income from the U.S. federal return may be apportionable to Rhode Island. Generally, rental income from property physically located in Rhode Island is entirely Rhode Island-source income.

### Step 8: File and Include Supporting Schedules

Submit Form 1040-NR with:
- Schedule E (Rental Real Estate Income and Loss)
- Form 4562 (Depreciation)
- Form 8288 (Election under Section 871(d))
- Schedule SE (if applicable for self-employment tax on rental real estate professionals)

## Rhode Island-Specific Considerations

**Property Tax Deduction**: Rhode Island's property tax rate (1.63% average) is substantial. Ensure your Form 1040-NR accurately captures all property taxes paid. These are fully deductible against rental income on Schedule E.

**No State Capital Gains Tax**: Rhode Island does not impose a separate capital gains tax, but ordinary state income tax applies to rental income.

**Non-Resident Return Requirements**: Rhode Island requires non-residents to file Form RI-1040NR if they have Rhode Island-source income. The deadline aligns with your federal deadline (April 15 or June 15).

**Treaty Considerations**: Under the Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty, Rhode Island cannot impose tax on real property gains (capital gains on sale), but it can tax rental income. Verify this distinction in your specific situation.

**Foreign Tax Credit**: On your Canadian T1 return, you'll report U.S. income and claim a foreign tax credit for U.S. federal and state taxes paid. Calculate this credit on **Schedule 1 (Federal Tax)** and **Schedule 11 (Federal Tuition and Education Amounts)** of your Canadian return, or use the detailed calculation on Form T776.

## Common Mistakes Rhode Island Landlords Make

1. **Failing to Make the Section 871(d) Election**: Many Canadian landlords file Form 1040-NR but don't attach Form 8288, forfeiting the election and remaining subject to 30% withholding on gross rent.

2. **Omitting Rhode Island State Return**: Some assume federal filing is sufficient; Rhode Island requires a separate non-resident state return.

3. **Miscalculating or Omitting Depreciation**: Depreciation is often overlooked but represents significant tax savings. Ensure Form 4562 is completed correctly.

4. **Not Claiming All Deductible Expenses**: Many landlords forget property management fees, insurance, utilities, maintenance, and repairs. Keep meticulous records.
