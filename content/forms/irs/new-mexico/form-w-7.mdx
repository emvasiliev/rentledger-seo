# Form W-7 Guide for Canadian Landlords with New Mexico Rental Property

## What Is Form W-7?

Form W-7, Application for IRS Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN), is the official IRS application to obtain a US Individual Taxpayer Identification Number. An ITIN is a nine-digit taxpayer identification number issued by the Internal Revenue Service to individuals who are required to have a US tax identification number but are not eligible for a Social Security Number (SSN).

For Canadian residents, an ITIN serves as your primary US tax identifier when filing federal returns and complying with US tax obligations related to US-source income. Unlike a Social Security Number—which is issued by the Social Security Administration to US citizens and permanent residents—an ITIN is specifically designed for non-residents and foreign nationals with US tax filing requirements.

## Why Canadian Landlords Need an ITIN

If you own rental property in New Mexico as a Canadian resident, you are classified as a **non-resident alien** for US federal tax purposes. The US taxes worldwide income of US citizens and residents, but non-residents are taxed only on US-source income. Rental income from New Mexico property is definitively US-source income.

You need an ITIN to:

- **File Form 1040-NR** (US Non-Resident Alien Income Tax Return)
- **Comply with FIRPTA withholding rules** when you eventually sell the property
- **Meet New Mexico state income tax requirements** (Form NR, Non-Resident Income Tax Return)
- **Satisfy treaty-based position disclosure requirements** if claiming Canada-US Tax Treaty benefits
- **Establish compliance records** with the IRS and New Mexico Department of Revenue

An ITIN is not a work authorization; it is strictly a tax identification number. Having an ITIN does not change your immigration status or grant you any work rights in the United States.

## How Form W-7 Applies in New Mexico

New Mexico has a particularly clear incentive structure for obtaining an ITIN as a rental property owner:

**Federal Tax Requirements:** You must file Form 1040-NR annually if you have rental income exceeding the filing threshold. For 2024, non-resident aliens generally must file if they have any US-source income subject to tax (rental income qualifies).

**New Mexico State Tax Requirements:** New Mexico imposes a **5.9% state income tax on rental income** earned within the state. Non-resident landlords must file **Form NR (Non-Resident Income Tax Return)** with the New Mexico Department of Revenue. The state requires a federal ITIN or SSN to process your return. Without an ITIN, you cannot file a compliant Form NR.

**Property Tax Considerations:** While New Mexico's property tax rate averages **0.8%**, this is levied by local counties and assessed on property value. Property tax is typically deductible on your federal return, and you'll need your ITIN established before claiming deductions on Form 1040-NR.

**FIRPTA Compliance:** When you eventually sell your New Mexico rental property, the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA) requires that 15% of the gross sale proceeds be withheld and remitted to the IRS. Your ITIN must be provided to your real estate agent, title company, and the IRS for this withholding to be processed correctly. Without an ITIN, the full 15% may be withheld with no attribution.

## Who Must File Form W-7

You must file Form W-7 if you:

- Are a non-resident alien with US-source rental income
- Do not have a US Social Security Number
- Are required to file a US federal tax return (Form 1040-NR)
- Need to comply with FIRPTA withholding on property sales
- Are claiming benefits under the Canada-US Tax Treaty (Article XXII may require treaty-based position disclosure using Form W-7 Schedule C)

You do **not** need to file Form W-7 if:

- You already have a valid US Social Security Number
- You have no US tax filing obligations
- You are a Canadian citizen with a Treaty-based position that exempts you from filing (rare for rental income scenarios)

## Step-by-Step Instructions for Completing Form W-7

### Step 1: Gather Required Documentation

Before completing Form W-7, assemble:

- **Valid Canadian passport** (color copy, both sides)
- **Proof of Canadian residence** (utility bill, lease agreement, or provincial identification with your name and address dated within 12 months)
- **IRS Form W-7** (current year version; obtain from IRS.gov)
- **Form 1040-NR** (draft or completed, to attach with your W-7 application)
- **New Mexico Form NR** (draft or completed)

### Step 2: Complete Form W-7 Accurately

**Part 1: Personal Identification**

- **Line 1a:** Enter your full legal name as it appears on your Canadian passport
- **Line 1b:** Enter your date of birth
- **Line 1c:** Enter your country of citizenship (Canada)
- **Line 2:** Enter your current mailing address in Canada
- **Line 3:** Enter your New Mexico rental property address

**Part 2: Application Type**

- Check **Box c: "I am a non-resident alien required to obtain an ITIN to comply with US tax laws"**
- This is the applicable box for rental property owners

**Part 3: Reason You Are Applying**

- Check the box corresponding to your primary reason. For rental property owners, this is typically **"Individual income tax return or Form 1040-NR"** and/or **"FIRPTA withholding/sale of US real property"**

**Part 4: Mailing Address**

- You may use a New Mexico address (property address or property manager's address) or your Canadian address
- Using a US address can sometimes expedite processing, but it is not required

### Step 3: Attach Required Attachments

Attach to your W-7 application:

1. **Certified copy of your Canadian passport** (front and back pages, color copy certified as a true copy by a notary or IRS Acceptance Agent)
2. **Proof of Canadian residence** (certified copy)
3. **Completed Form 1040-NR** (with all applicable New Mexico rental income schedules)
4. **IRS Form 8288-B** or **New Mexico Form NR** (if you have already sold the property, attach FIRPTA withholding documentation)

### Step 4: Sign and Date

- Sign and date the form personally; signatures must match your passport
- Do not sign before a notary (the IRS will reject the application if notarized before submission)

### Step 5: Submit Your Application

You have three options for submitting Form W-7:

**Option A: In-Person at an IRS Acceptance Agent (Recommended for Canadian Residents)**

The IRS Acceptance Agent program allows certain authorized representatives (banks, tax professionals, CPA firms) to verify your identity documents and submit your application directly to the IRS. This is the **fastest and most reliable method** for Canadian residents.

- Contact the **Albuquerque IRS office** (handles New Mexico) or find an Acceptance Agent in your area at IRS.gov
- Submit your original documents (uncertified copies of passport are acceptable to an Acceptance Agent)
- Processing time: 2–4 weeks

**Option B: By Mail to the IRS**

Send your completed Form W-7 with all required attachments to:

**Internal Revenue Service  
ITIN Unit  
Austin, TX 73301  
USA**

- Use registered mail with signature confirmation
- Processing time: 6–8 weeks
- Risk: Original documents sent by mail can be lost or delayed

**Option C: Through a US Tax Professional**

Engage a CPA or tax attorney in the US (or a Canadian cross-border tax firm) to submit Form W-7 on your behalf. They can use IRS e-services or in-person Acceptance Agent networks.

## New Mexico-Specific Considerations

### State Income Tax Nexus

New Mexico's Department of Revenue requires that all individuals with New Mexico-source income file a state return. Because you own rental property, you are automatically in New Mexico's tax system. Provide your ITIN (once issued) on your Form NR to avoid penalties for missing identification.

### Property Tax Deductions

New Mexico property taxes are deductible on both your federal Form 1040-NR (Schedule A) and your state Form NR. Your ITIN must be established and reported to the county assessor's office for your property records. Many New Mexico counties (Bernalillo, Santa Fe, Doña Ana) cross-check names and tax IDs.
