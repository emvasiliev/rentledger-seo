## US Rental Property Tax Guide for Northwest Territories Landlords

As a Northwest Territories resident with rental property in Arizona, you operate in one of Canada's most complex tax environments. Unlike Alberta and British Columbia landlords (who benefit from no Arizona state withholding on non-resident rental income), you must navigate dual filing obligations across three tax jurisdictions: the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA), the US Internal Revenue Service (IRS), and the Arizona Department of Revenue.

This guide walks you through the specific rules, forms, and deadlines that apply to your situation.

### Why This Combination Matters

Northwest Territories residents have no provincial income tax, which simplifies one layer. However, this advantage disappears when you earn US rental income—the IRS and Arizona treat you as a non-resident alien earning investment income, which triggers mandatory withholding, additional filing requirements, and exchange rate considerations.

Arizona specifically imposes a 2.5% state income tax on non-resident rental income (unlike Nevada, Texas, and Wyoming). Combined with US federal tax, Arizona property tax, and CRA obligations, your total tax burden can reach 40–50% if you don't plan strategically.

The key to managing this is understanding which forms to file, which elections to make, and when to file them.

## CRA Obligations for Northwest Territories Landlords

### Filing Form T776 (Statement of Real Estate Rentals)

You must report all Arizona rental income on your Canadian tax return, even if US withholding has already occurred. File **Form T776** annually with your T1 General (personal income tax return).

**What to include:**
- Gross rental income (converted to Canadian dollars using the Bank of Canada average annual rate)
- Mortgage interest (deductible)
- Property tax (deductible)
- Repairs and maintenance (deductible)
- Property management fees (deductible)
- Insurance (deductible)
- Utilities paid by you (deductible)
- Capital cost allowance (CCA) — optional claim on building only

**Currency conversion:** Use the Bank of Canada average annual exchange rate for the year of income. For 2024 income (filed in 2025), the average rate was approximately **1 USD = 1.36 CAD**. Do not use the daily rate at time of payment.

### Reporting US Tax Paid (Foreign Tax Credit)

When the IRS or Arizona withholds tax on your rental income, you can claim these amounts as a foreign tax credit on Schedule 1 (line 40400) of your T1 General.

**Calculate your foreign tax credit:**
- US federal withholding: 30% on gross rents (if Section 871(d) not elected)
- Arizona state withholding: 2.5% on net rental income
- Any estimated taxes you pay directly to the IRS

The CRA will allow a credit up to the amount of Canadian tax you would owe on that same income. If US tax paid exceeds Canadian tax, you cannot claim the excess—you simply lose it.

### Form T1135 (Foreign Income Verification Statement)

If your Arizona property has a fair market value exceeding **CAD $100,000**, you must file **Form T1135** with your T1 General. This is mandatory, even if the property generates a loss.

**What to report:**
- Description of the property (real estate, Arizona)
- Acquisition cost in Canadian dollars
- Fair market value at year-end in Canadian dollars
- Gross rental income
- Taxes paid

Failure to file T1135 triggers a **$25/day penalty** (minimum $500 per year).

## IRS Obligations: Filing as a Non-Resident Alien

### Obtaining an ITIN (Individual Taxpayer Identification Number)

If you don't have a US Social Security Number, you must apply for an **ITIN** using **Form W-7** before you can file a US tax return. Submit Form W-7 to the IRS along with:
- Certified copy of your Canadian passport
- Original or certified copy of a Canadian tax return or Notice of Assessment

Mail to the IRS at the address provided in Form W-7 instructions. Processing takes 4–6 weeks.

Once assigned, your ITIN remains valid for 5 years of non-use, then expires.

### Filing Form 1040-NR (US Non-Resident Alien Income Tax Return)

You must file **Form 1040-NR** with the IRS annually if:
- You have US rental income subject to tax, OR
- You made a Section 871(d) election (see below)

**Filing deadline:** June 15 (not April 15, if you're a Canadian resident). If June 15 falls on a weekend, the deadline extends to the next business day.

**What to include:**
- Schedule E (Supplemental Income and Loss) — report Arizona property rental income and expenses
- Calculation of taxable income: Gross rental income minus deductible expenses (mortgage interest, property tax, insurance, utilities, repairs, depreciation)

### Section 871(d) Election: Avoid 30% Withholding

By default, the IRS withholds **30% on the gross rents** paid to you—before any deductions. This is punitive and inefficient.

**Section 871(d) election** allows you to be taxed on net rental income instead, with withholding at ordinary rates (currently 10–37% based on your tax bracket).

**How to make the election:**
- Attach a statement to Form 1040-NR stating: "I elect under Section 871(d) to be taxed on net rental income from [property address], Arizona."
- File the return and election on time (June 15).
- Provide a copy to your US property manager or tenant via Form W-8IMY, so they withhold at the correct rate.

**Benefit:** If you have significant deductions (mortgage interest, property tax, repairs), Section 871(d) typically saves 10–15% compared to 30% gross withholding.

## Arizona State Tax Obligations

### Arizona Non-Resident Rental Income Tax

Arizona imposes **2.5% state income tax** on non-resident rental income. Unlike some states, Arizona does not allow non-resident landlords to claim standard deductions, but you *can* deduct ordinary business expenses (same list as the IRS).

### Filing Requirements

If you file a US federal return (Form 1040-NR), you should also file **Arizona Form 140-NR** (Nonresident Income Tax Return) or **Form 140** with Arizona's filing address.

**Key point:** Arizona allows you to credit federal tax paid against Arizona tax owing. In practice, federal tax will exceed Arizona tax, so you'll owe nothing to Arizona specifically—but you still must file Form 140-NR to report the income.

### Arizona Property Tax

Arizona's average effective property tax rate is **0.62%** of assessed property value (one of the lowest in the US). Property tax is fully deductible on your US Schedule E and counts as a deduction on your Canadian T776.

## Selling the Property: FIRPTA Basics

If you sell your Arizona rental property, **FIRPTA** (Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act) requires the buyer to withhold **15% of the sale price** unless:
- The property is your primary residence (not applicable for rentals), OR
- The sale price is under $300,000 and you provide an affidavit, OR
- You receive an IRS exemption certificate

**Your obligations:**
1. File **Form 8288** with the IRS within 10 days of closing to report the sale and withholding.
2. Report the sale on Form 1040-NR as a capital gain.
3. Report the capital gain on your Canadian T1 General (Schedule 3) as a capital gain, converted to CAD using the exchange rate on the closing date.
4. Claim the FIRPTA withholding as a foreign tax credit on Schedule 1.
