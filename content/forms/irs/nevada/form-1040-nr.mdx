# Form 1040-NR for Canadian Landlords: Nevada Rental Property Guide

## What Is Form 1040-NR?

Form 1040-NR (U.S. Income Tax Return for Nonresident Alien Individuals) is the primary federal income tax return filed by non-resident aliens—including Canadian citizens—who earn income that is effectively connected with a U.S. trade or business. For landlords, this typically means rental income from U.S. real estate.

Unlike U.S. citizens and permanent residents (who file Form 1040), non-resident aliens have significantly different rules regarding which income is taxable and how deductions are allowed. The 1040-NR is specifically designed to report only U.S.-source income and calculate tax on income that is "effectively connected income" (ECI)—a critical distinction for cross-border landlords.

## How Form 1040-NR Applies in Nevada

Nevada presents a unique tax advantage for Canadian landlords: **Nevada has no state income tax**. This simplification is significant and immediately reduces your filing complexity compared to owning rental property in states like California, Arizona, or Utah.

### Federal Tax Obligation

Even though Nevada imposes no state income tax, Canadian landlords remain **fully subject to federal U.S. income tax** on rental income from Nevada properties. The IRS treats rental income as effectively connected income (ECI) under Internal Revenue Code Section 871(d)—the critical election that allows you to deduct operating expenses (mortgage interest, property taxes, maintenance, utilities, property management fees) against your rental revenue.

Without making the Section 871(d) election, rental income would be subject to a flat 30% withholding rate with **no deduction for expenses**—a result that virtually no landlord accepts. Therefore, most Canadian landlords automatically file Form 1040-NR to claim this election and report net rental income.

### Nevada Property Tax Consideration

While Nevada has no income tax, it does impose property taxes. Nevada's average effective property tax rate is **0.59% of assessed property value**—among the lowest in the nation. This is crucial: property taxes are fully deductible on your Form 1040-NR, and you will also report them on your Canadian T1 return if you file one.

## Who Must File Form 1040-NR

### You Must File If:

- You are a **non-resident alien** (Canadian citizen without U.S. green card or resident alien status)
- You have **U.S.-source income** from Nevada rental property
- Your net rental income, **after deductions**, exceeds the filing threshold for your filing status
- You are **electing under Section 871(d)** to treat rental income as ECI

### Filing Threshold (2024 Tax Year)

For a single filer, the threshold is typically **$5,200** of net income. However, you should file even if below this threshold if you had **federal income tax withheld** on your rental income, as you may be entitled to a refund.

### Important Note on Residency Status

You are considered a non-resident alien for U.S. tax purposes if you fail the Substantial Presence Test (SPT). Most Canadian landlords automatically qualify as non-residents because they spend fewer than 183 days in the U.S. during the relevant period. Confirm your status carefully, as misclassification has serious consequences.

## Step-by-Step: Completing Form 1040-NR for Nevada Rental Income

### Step 1: Gather Documentation

Before you begin, collect:
- **Rental income records**: lease agreements, bank statements showing rent deposits, cancelled cheques
- **Expense documentation**: property tax statements, mortgage interest statements (Form 1098), insurance policies, maintenance receipts, property management invoices, utility bills
- **Nevada property deed or title** (to confirm the property address and ownership)
- **Identification information**: U.S. Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN) if you don't have a Social Security Number (SSN)

### Step 2: Obtain an ITIN (If Necessary)

Most Canadian landlords do not have a U.S. SSN. You will need to apply for an **Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN)** using **Form W-7** before you can file your 1040-NR. ITINs are issued specifically for tax purposes and are not immigration documents.

### Step 3: Complete Schedule NEC (Nonresident Alien Computation of Tax Income)

This is the core section for your Nevada rental income. You will report:

- **Gross rental income** from your Nevada property
- **Deductible rental expenses** (itemized):
  - Property taxes (Nevada assessment)
  - Mortgage interest
  - Property insurance
  - Repairs and maintenance
  - Property management fees
  - Utilities (if you pay them)
  - Depreciation (if depreciating the building)
  - HOA fees (if applicable)

**Net rental income** equals gross income minus deductions. This figure flows to your overall taxable income calculation.

### Step 4: Complete Schedule C or Schedule E

For rental property, you typically report on **Schedule E** (Supplemental Income and Loss). Complete Part 1 for your Nevada property:
- Property address
- Number of months rented
- Gross rental income
- All expense categories
- Depreciation calculation (if claiming)

### Step 5: Calculate Your Tax

Once Schedule E is complete, the net rental income flows to the 1040-NR tax calculation. The applicable **federal tax rate for non-resident aliens on rental income ranges from 10% to 37%** depending on your total taxable income and filing status.

### Step 6: Claim the Section 871(d) Election

File **Form 8288-B** (U.S. Income Tax Return for Nonresident Alien Individuals) or, more commonly, include the election statement with your 1040-NR. This election is crucial—without it, a 30% withholding rate applies to gross income.

## Nevada-Specific Considerations

### No Nevada State Return Required

This is the major advantage. You file **only the federal 1040-NR**. There is no Nevada state income tax return (no NV Form 1), no state withholding, and no additional state filing obligations. This clarity reduces compliance burden significantly.

### Property Tax Deduction on U.S. Return

Nevada property taxes are **fully deductible** on your Form 1040-NR. For the 2024 tax year, there is no federal limitation on deducting state and local property taxes (though SALT limitations apply in certain contexts for U.S. residents—these typically do not apply to non-resident aliens).

### Depreciation Considerations

If you depreciate your Nevada property building, you may claim **residential real property depreciation** over 27.5 years. However, understand that depreciation recapture upon sale creates a 25% capital gains tax rate on the depreciated amount. Many Canadian landlords underestimate this cross-border tax consequence.

### No Nevada Income Shelter

Unlike some states that provide income tax shelters for non-residents, Nevada's zero tax environment is straightforward: you owe federal tax only. Plan accordingly in your annual budgeting.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Forgetting to File Schedule SE (Self-Employment Tax)**
Rental income is *not* subject to U.S. self-employment tax (Social Security/Medicare). Do not file Schedule SE.

**2. Misreporting Gross vs. Net Income**
The Section 871(d) election allows deductions *against* rental income. Always report net income on the 1040-NR, not gross revenue.

**3. Overlooking the Canada-US Tax Treaty**
The Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty may provide relief from certain U.S. taxes. Article XIII addresses real property income. Confirm whether treaty provisions reduce your U.S. tax liability.

**4. Not Coordinating with Your Canadian T1 Return**
Your U.S. rental income must also be reported on your Canadian T1 return in Canadian dollars. Reconcile foreign exchange rates, and claim U.S. foreign tax credits on your Canadian return to avoid double taxation.

**5. Ignoring Withholding Requirements**
If your U.S. tenant or property manager withholds taxes, ensure they apply the correct rate under Section 1441/1442. Verify withholding receipts.
