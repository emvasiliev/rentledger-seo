# Form 1040-NR for Canadian Landlords: Your Virginia Rental Property Tax Guide

## What Is Form 1040-NR?

Form 1040-NR is the US income tax return filed by non-resident aliens (NRAs)—including Canadian citizens and permanent residents—who have US-source income subject to US taxation. For Canadian landlords with rental property in Virginia, this form is the cornerstone of your US tax filing obligations.

Unlike US citizens or residents who file Form 1040, non-resident aliens have limited personal exemptions and must file 1040-NR to report effectively connected income (ECI)—rental income that has a genuine business nexus to the United States. The form is paired with **Schedule NEC (Nonresident Aliens' Net Effectively Connected Income)** and, critically, requires the **Section 871(d) election** to deduct rental expenses and depreciation against your rental income.

Without this election, your Virginia rental income would be subject to a flat 30% withholding tax with no deductions allowed—a significantly worse outcome.

## How Form 1040-NR Applies to Virginia Landlords

Virginia presents a two-layer tax burden for non-resident alien landlords:

### Federal Level
Your rental income is taxed as effectively connected income under Section 871(d) of the Internal Revenue Code. The Canada-US Tax Treaty provides limited relief; while Article XIII generally permits the US to tax real property income, Article XXIV allows you to claim certain personal exemptions and apply tax credits similarly to US residents—but only if you make and maintain the Section 871(d) election.

### Virginia State Level
Virginia imposes a **5.75% state income tax** on all income of non-residents derived from Virginia sources. Virginia does **not** recognize the federal Section 871(d) election automatically; you must also file a **Virginia Form 540-NR (Non-Resident or Part-Year Resident Return)** and include rental income there.

Additionally, Virginia property tax on investment real estate averages **0.82% of assessed value annually** but is a local matter, not part of your state return.

## Who Must File Form 1040-NR

You must file Form 1040-NR if:

- You are a non-resident alien (Canadian citizen without US permanent residency or substantial presence status under the IRS "substantial presence test")
- You have US-source income from Virginia rental property
- Your income exceeds the filing threshold for non-resident aliens ($1,300 or more of US-source income in 2024, though this varies; consult current IRS guidance)
- You intend to claim the Section 871(d) election to deduct rental expenses

**Note on substantial presence:** If you spend ≥183 days in the US during the current year, or meet a weighted-day formula across three years, you may be classified as a US resident for tax purposes and file Form 1040 instead. The Canada-US Tax Treaty's "tie-breaker" rule (Article IV) may help you remain classified as a Canadian resident despite physical presence.

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form 1040-NR

### 1. Identify Your Filing Status
Non-resident aliens cannot use standard filing statuses (single, married filing jointly). You are simply "NRA." If married to a US citizen/resident, special rules apply; consult a cross-border accountant.

### 2. Report Income on Schedule NEC
Schedule NEC is your primary vehicle for reporting Virginia rental income. Complete:
- **Line 1a:** Gross rental income (rent received from Virginia property)
- **Line 2:** Deductions directly attributable to that income:
  - Mortgage interest
  - Property taxes
  - Insurance
  - Repairs and maintenance
  - Property management fees
  - Utilities (if you pay them)
  - HOA fees (if applicable)

### 3. Complete Schedule C for Depreciation (if applicable)
If you own the Virginia property (not merely leasing land), you may depreciate the building structure over 27.5 years using the straight-line method. Report depreciation on Schedule C; this flows into Schedule NEC.

### 4. File Schedule 8288-B
This schedule reports your Section 871(d) election. It confirms that you are electing to treat rental income as effectively connected income and claim deductions. Filing this schedule is **mandatory** to get the election benefit.

### 5. Calculate Federal Tax
Transfer your net rental income from Schedule NEC to Form 1040-NR, Line 21a. Apply the 2024 federal tax brackets (ordinary rates; NRAs do not access standard deductions). The tax owed is calculated on your net ECI.

### 6. Complete Schedule 3 for Withholding
If your Virginia property manager, tenant, or escrow agent has withheld federal tax from rental payments, report it here. Any overpayment can become a refund.

## Virginia-Specific Considerations

### Virginia Form 540-NR
You **must also file Virginia Form 540-NR** to report the same rental income. Virginia will not automatically recognize your federal 871(d) election. Key points:

- Virginia taxes your gross Virginia-source income at 5.75%
- Report Virginia rental income and the same deductions (mortgage interest, property tax, insurance, repairs)
- Virginia allows a **resident credit** for federal taxes paid on the same income (to prevent double taxation), but you must calculate this carefully as a non-resident
- **Filing deadline:** June 15 (same extension as federal) or April 15 (no extension); consult Virginia Department of Taxation

### Virginia Property Tax
Property taxes on your Virginia rental are a **local (county) responsibility**, not state. However, Virginia counties charge an average of 0.82% annually. Property tax is deductible on both Form 1040-NR and Form 540-NR, so ensure you have accurate records from your local assessor or property manager.

### Currency Conversion
If you receive rent in US dollars, no conversion is needed. If received in Canadian dollars, convert at the average exchange rate for the tax year (per IRS guidance). Maintain contemporaneous records.

### FIRPTA Withholding
If you sell the Virginia property, the buyer's closing agent must withhold 15% of the gross proceeds under the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA). This withholding is creditable against your tax liability in the year of sale.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Forgetting Schedule 8288-B:** Many Canadian landlords file 1040-NR but omit Schedule 8288-B, inadvertently failing to make the 871(d) election. The IRS may disallow deductions.

2. **Not Filing Virginia Form 540-NR:** Federal filing alone does not satisfy Virginia tax obligations. File both returns.

3. **Mixing Personal and Rental Deductions:** Only deductions directly tied to the rental activity (not personal use portions of mixed-use property) are deductible.

4. **Incorrect Currency Conversion:** Use IRS-approved exchange rates; do not use your personal banking rate.

5. **Overlooking Capital Gains:** If you sell the Virginia property, net capital gains are also subject to 1040-NR taxation and may trigger an additional federal capital gains tax (20% for NRAs on long-term gains in some cases). Plan accordingly.

6. **Missing the Virginia Credit for Federal Tax:** When filing Form 540-NR, apply the federal tax credit to avoid double taxation on the same rental income.

## Canadian Tax Return Implications

On your **Canadian Form T1 (Personal Tax Return)**, you must:

- Report the same Virginia rental income in Canadian dollars
- Claim a **foreign tax credit (FTC)** for US federal and Virginia state taxes paid
- Use the Bank of Canada average exchange rate for the year

The Canada-US Tax Treaty allows you to use the FTC to offset Canadian tax on US-source income, typically preventing full double taxation. However, if US tax paid exceeds Canadian tax on the same income, the excess cannot be carried back or forward under the treaty.
