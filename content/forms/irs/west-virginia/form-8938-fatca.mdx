# Form 8938 for Canadian Landlords with West Virginia Rental Property: A Complete Guide

## What is Form 8938?

Form 8938 (Statement of Specified Foreign Financial Assets) is a mandatory disclosure requirement under the Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA). This form requires US persons to report specified foreign financial assets that exceed certain thresholds to the Internal Revenue Service (IRS).

The form's primary purpose is to increase transparency regarding US persons' offshore financial holdings. Unlike FinCEN Form 114 (FBAR), which focuses on foreign financial accounts, Form 8938 captures a broader range of foreign financial assets, including investment accounts, real property in foreign countries, and certain foreign partnerships or corporations.

For Canadian landlords operating in the United States, Form 8938 creates an additional reporting layer beyond Canadian provincial requirements and US federal obligations.

## How Form 8938 Applies to West Virginia Landlords

As a Canadian landlord with rental property in West Virginia, you face a unique tax situation:

**US Federal Perspective**: You are likely classified as a "US person" under US tax law if you hold a green card or meet the substantial presence test (183+ days in the US in the current or preceding three years). This classification triggers FATCA reporting obligations.

**West Virginia State Perspective**: West Virginia imposes a 6.5% income tax rate on rental income derived from property located within the state. As a non-resident, you must file Form NR-1 (West Virginia Non-Resident Income Tax Return) to report rental income, depreciation deductions, and mortgage interest.

**The FATCA Intersection**: Your Canadian bank accounts, investment accounts, and registered accounts (RRSPs, TFSAs) that exceed the reporting threshold must be disclosed on Form 8938, *in addition to* your West Virginia rental property value and any Canadian-based income.

## Who Must File Form 8938

You must file Form 8938 if you meet two criteria:

1. **You are a US person** (US citizen, lawful permanent resident/green card holder, or resident alien under the substantial presence test)
2. **Your specified foreign financial assets exceed the reporting threshold**:
   - $50,000 on the last day of the tax year (or at any point during the year if you file a US return on April 15)
   - $100,000 for married couples filing jointly (same dates)
   - $25,000 for married individuals filing separately living with spouse
   - Higher thresholds apply if you are a bona fide resident of a US possession

**Canadian Assets That Must Be Reported**:
- Canadian bank accounts (personal or business)
- Canadian investment accounts (stocks, bonds, mutual funds)
- Registered accounts (RRSP contributions and accumulated value)
- Tax-Free Savings Accounts (TFSA) — reported as "other specified property"
- Canadian real estate held in personal name (valued at fair market value)

Note: Your West Virginia rental property itself is *not* reported on Form 8938, as it is US-situs property. However, any Canadian accounts funding that property (or held alongside it) *are* reportable if thresholds are met.

## Step-by-Step Completion Guide

### Part I: Summary of Specified Foreign Financial Assets

**Line 1a–1d**: Report the maximum value of each category of foreign financial assets during the tax year:
- Deposits and custodial accounts (e.g., Canadian savings accounts)
- Securities (Canadian stocks, bonds, ETFs)
- Other financial instruments and contracts
- Other property (including registered retirement accounts)

For each category, enter the *maximum* value in US dollars during the tax year, using the IRS published exchange rate for December 31 of the relevant year (or the rate on the date of valuation, if that date is used consistently).

### Part II: Detailed Information About Specified Foreign Financial Assets

For each asset exceeding $10,000 in value (or the aggregated threshold of $50,000/$100,000), provide:

- **Financial institution name and address** (e.g., Royal Bank of Canada, Toronto branch)
- **Account type and number** (or institution reference identifier)
- **Beginning and ending values in US dollars**
- **Entity type**: Individual account or held through a corporation/trust

**Example**: RRSP held at TD Bank, Toronto. Maximum value CAD $75,000 = USD ~$56,000 (at 2023 rates). Report separately from personal chequing account.

### Part III: Transactions with and Distributions from Foreign Financial Assets

Report any significant transactions during the year if the aggregate fair market value exceeded the reporting threshold, including:
- Deposits to foreign accounts
- Transfers between foreign accounts
- Withdrawals exceeding $10,000
- Distributions received

## West Virginia-Specific Considerations

### Property Tax Coordination

West Virginia's average effective property tax rate is 0.59%, significantly lower than the US national average. The annual property tax on your West Virginia rental property should be deducted on Schedule E (Form 1040) as a rental expense. This deduction *reduces* your federal taxable rental income and may lower your West Virginia Form NR-1 liability.

**Example**: A $400,000 rental property in Charleston, WV would generate approximately $2,360 in annual property taxes. This is fully deductible on Form 1040 Schedule E and carries forward to your West Virginia non-resident return calculation.

### Form NR-1 Integration

You must file both:
- **Form 8938** (if thresholds are met) with your US Form 1040 by April 15
- **West Virginia Form NR-1** (Non-Resident Income Tax Return) by April 15

Your rental income is taxed at West Virginia's 6.5% rate. However, foreign tax credits available under the US-Canada Tax Treaty (Article 24) may provide relief if you also report the same income to Canada. Report your West Virginia taxes paid on Form 1116 (Foreign Tax Credit) as an alternative to claiming the standard deduction, if beneficial.

### Canadian T1 Return Filing

Simultaneously, you must file a Canadian T1 return with the Canada Revenue Agency, reporting:
- Rental income from the West Virginia property
- Canadian source income
- All specified foreign assets (on Form T1135, if required)

The US-Canada Tax Treaty prevents double taxation. Coordinate Form 8938 timing with Canadian T1 filing (June 15 deadline for non-residents) to ensure consistent reporting.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Forgetting to convert to USD**: Report all values in US dollars at year-end exchange rates. The IRS publishes December 31 rates; using month-end rates or daily rates introduces errors.

2. **Excluding registered accounts**: Many Canadian landlords forget that RRSP and TFSA balances are "specified foreign financial assets" and must be reported at fair market value, not just contributions.

3. **Double-counting US property**: Your West Virginia rental building itself is not reported on Form 8938. Only foreign accounts and Canadian real estate in your personal name are reportable.

4. **Missing the filing deadline**: Form 8938 must be *attached* to your Form 1040/1040-NR. It is not filed separately. Late filing triggers penalties of $10,000+ per year.

5. **Ignoring Part III transactions**: If you made large deposits to Canadian accounts, transfers between accounts, or withdrew funds during the year, you must report these in Part III, even if overall balances didn't exceed thresholds.

6. **Inconsistent reporting across forms**: Ensure your RRSP/TFSA values match between Form 8938 and your Canadian T1135, if filed.
