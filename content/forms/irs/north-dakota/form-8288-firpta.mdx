# Form 8288: FIRPTA Withholding Tax for Canadian Landlords Selling North Dakota Property

## What Is Form 8288?

Form 8288 is the **U.S. Withholding Tax Return for Dispositions by Foreign Persons of U.S. Real Property Interests**, filed under the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA). When a Canadian resident sells U.S. real property, the buyer is required by U.S. federal law to withhold 15% of the gross sale price and remit it to the Internal Revenue Service (IRS).

This withholding is a federal requirement—not optional. The buyer faces penalties and personal liability if withholding is not completed and Form 8288 is not filed within 20 days of the property transfer closing date.

FIRPTA exists because the U.S. considers real property sales by foreign persons to be U.S.-source income, which is subject to U.S. federal taxation even though the seller is a non-resident. The withholding ensures the IRS captures tax from cross-border transactions.

## How FIRPTA and Form 8288 Apply in North Dakota

North Dakota is a property-rich border state with significant agricultural and residential real estate activity. For Canadian landlords—particularly those in Manitoba and Saskatchewan—North Dakota represents accessible real estate investment. However, this proximity creates FIRPTA obligations.

### The Withholding Scenario

You are a Canadian resident who owns a residential rental property in Fargo or Bismarck. After several years of collecting rental income, you decide to sell the property to a U.S. buyer for **$400,000**. The buyer's attorney or title company is responsible for ensuring 15% withholding occurs:

**Mandatory withholding = $400,000 × 15% = $60,000**

This $60,000 is withheld from your sale proceeds and remitted to the IRS via Form 8288, filed by the buyer's closing agent or attorney.

### North Dakota State Tax Implications

While FIRPTA is purely federal, North Dakota has its own income tax system. As a non-resident, you must file a **North Dakota Form ND-1 (North Dakota Income Tax Return for Non-Residents)** if you had net income from North Dakota sources during the tax year. 

North Dakota's top combined tax rate on ordinary income is approximately **5.84%**, and the state's rental income is taxed at your marginal rate. However, **capital gains from real property sales are generally not subject to North Dakota state income tax** (North Dakota has not implemented capital gains taxation). This is a significant advantage compared to other U.S. states.

The 15% federal FIRPTA withholding does not reduce North Dakota's rental income tax obligations for years prior to sale. If you operated the rental property for multiple years, you remain responsible for filing ND-1 returns for those years and paying the 2.5% tax on net rental income (after deductions for mortgage interest, repairs, depreciation, property taxes, etc.).

North Dakota's average effective property tax rate is **0.98%**, which factors into your rental income calculations and your adjusted basis for capital gains.

## Who Files Form 8288?

**The buyer or the buyer's legal representative files Form 8288**, not the Canadian seller. This is critical: the buyer's closing attorney, title company, or escrow officer is the party responsible for:

1. Calculating the 15% withholding amount
2. Collecting the withheld funds
3. Preparing Form 8288
4. Remitting the funds to the IRS within 20 days of closing
5. Providing the seller with a copy

**You, as the Canadian seller, are the "foreign person" identified on Form 8288**, but your direct filing obligation is limited. However, you may file Form 8288 if you are **requesting a reduced withholding certificate** from the IRS under IRC Section 1445(b)(4).

## Reducing Withholding: The Withholding Certificate Route

The default 15% withholding may exceed your actual U.S. tax liability. If you believe this is the case, you can apply for a **Certificate of Withholding Tax on Dispositions of U.S. Real Property Interests** by submitting:

- **Form 8288-B** (Application for Reduced Withholding Certificate) to the IRS
- Supporting documentation proving expected U.S. tax liability
- Proof of Canadian residence

The IRS may issue a certificate allowing reduced withholding (e.g., 10% or even lower if your expected liability is minimal).

**Timeline**: Apply at least 20 days before closing to allow the IRS time to issue the certificate. Processing typically takes 10-15 days.

## Step-by-Step: Completing Form 8288

While the buyer's representative completes Form 8288, understanding its sections helps you verify accuracy:

### Part I: Transferor Information
- Your name as it appears on the deed
- Your Canadian address
- Your U.S. taxpayer identification number (if obtained) or description

### Part II: Transferee Information
- Buyer's name, address, and U.S. taxpayer identification number (SSN or EIN)
- Date of transfer (the closing date)

### Part III: Property Description
- Street address of the North Dakota property
- Property description (legal description or parcel number)
- Type of property (residential rental property, agricultural, etc.)

### Part IV: Transfer Information
- Date of transfer
- Sales price (gross amount, including liens assumed)
- Amount of withholding (15% of gross price)
- Entity type (individual, partnership, trust, etc.)

### Part V: Withholding Agent Information
- Title company, attorney, or other closing agent information
- Amount withheld and date of remittance

## North Dakota-Specific Considerations

### 1. Agricultural Property Exemptions
If your North Dakota property includes active agricultural operations, special rules may apply. The Agricultural Foreign Investment Disclosure Act (AFIDA) requires disclosure of foreign ownership of U.S. agricultural land. This does **not** exempt FIRPTA withholding, but it affects reporting requirements and potential state compliance obligations.

### 2. Currency and Exchange Rates
The sale price on Form 8288 must be in U.S. dollars. If your purchase price was in Canadian dollars, the withholding is calculated on the actual U.S. dollar sale price. There is no special currency adjustment for FIRPTA purposes.

### 3. Mortgage Assumptions
If the buyer assumes a mortgage, the **gross amount** of the sale price (including the assumed mortgage debt) is subject to the 15% withholding calculation. The net proceeds to you are reduced by both the assumed mortgage and the withholding.

### 4. Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty Coordination
The Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty (Article XIII) addresses real property taxation. While it does not eliminate FIRPTA withholding, it may provide relief under Article 22 for double taxation if you can demonstrate that treaty benefits apply. You claim a **foreign tax credit** on your Canadian T1 return for the FIRPTA withholding.

## Reporting on Your Canadian Return

You must report the FIRPTA withholding on your Canadian personal income tax return (Form T1):

1. **Capital gains or losses**: Report the net proceeds and adjusted basis to calculate the capital gain
2. **Foreign tax credit**: Claim the 15% FIRPTA withholding (or reduced amount) on Schedule 1, line 40500, to offset Canadian tax on the gain
3. **U.S. rental income**: If you reported rental income in prior years on a T776, include those years' schedules with your return

CRA will allow a foreign tax credit for U.S. withholding taxes, provided the property sale qualifies as taxable in Canada (which it does for a North Dakota rental property).

## Common Mistakes

1. **Assuming the buyer will handle everything**: While the buyer files Form 8288, verify that the closing statement correctly calculates 15% of the gross (not net) sale price.

2. **Forgetting to apply for reduced withholding early**: If your expected U.S. tax liability is low (e.g., the property appreciated modestly), file Form 8288-B at least 20 days before closing.

3. **Not tracking adjusted basis**: Keep records of purchase price, improvements, depreciation claimed on rental returns, and property taxes. These reduce your taxable gain.

4. **Ignoring prior-year rental income reporting**: If you failed to file North Dakota non-resident returns for rental income in prior years, resolve this before selling to avoid audit complications.

5. **Overlooking recapture of depreciation**: Even though capital gains are not subject to North Dakota state tax, the IRS taxes depreciation recapture at 25%. Factor this into your U.S. tax planning.
