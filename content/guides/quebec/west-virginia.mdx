## Cross-Border Rental Property Tax Guide: Quebec Residents Owning in West Virginia

Owning rental property across the Canada-US border creates a unique tax situation. As a Quebec resident, you're subject to taxation from three tax authorities: the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA), the US Internal Revenue Service (IRS), and the West Virginia Department of Tax and Revenue. Understanding the rules in each jurisdiction—and how they interact—is essential to avoid penalties and minimize double taxation.

This guide walks you through the specific obligations, timelines, and strategies for managing rental income from West Virginia property as a Quebec resident.

## Why This Combination Matters

Quebec residents who own US rental property face overlapping tax jurisdictions. The US taxes you on your worldwide income as a non-resident alien, West Virginia levies state income tax on rental income sourced in the state, and the CRA taxes your worldwide income as a Canadian resident. Without proper planning, you could pay tax three times on the same dollar of rental income.

The good news: tax treaties, foreign tax credits, and elections exist to prevent full triple taxation. However, filing requirements are strict, and missed deadlines trigger penalties automatically.

## CRA Obligations for Quebec Landlords

### Reporting Rental Income

You must report all gross rental income from your West Virginia property on your Canadian tax return, regardless of whether you've already paid US taxes on it.

**Form T776** (Statement of Real Estate Rentals) is filed with your personal tax return. Report:
- Gross rent collected
- Operating expenses (property tax, insurance, utilities, repairs, mortgage interest)
- Capital cost allowance (CCA) claims, if applicable

Convert all US dollar amounts to Canadian dollars using the Bank of Canada annual average rate. For 2025, use **1 USD = 1.36 CAD** (or the actual annual average published by the Bank of Canada).

### Form T1135 (Foreign Property)

If your West Virginia property's fair market value exceeds **$100,000 CAD** at any time during the tax year, you must file Form T1135. This form reports:
- Property location and description
- Fair market value in Canadian dollars
- Rental income earned

**Failure to file T1135 triggers a $2,500 penalty** for the first year of non-compliance, plus $100 per day of continued non-compliance (up to $12,000 annually). The CRA treats this as a strict-liability penalty, meaning intent doesn't matter.

### Foreign Tax Credit (FTC)

The foreign tax credit prevents double taxation on the same income. On your Canadian return, you claim a credit for:
- **US federal income tax** paid on rental income
- **West Virginia state income tax** paid on rental income
- **Part XIII withholding** paid to the CRA (if you filed Form NR6—see below)

Calculate your FTC on **Schedule 1** of your T1 return. You can claim the credit only up to the Canadian tax you owe on that foreign income. Unused credits cannot be carried forward to other years under current rules.

## IRS Obligations for Non-Resident Aliens

### Obtain an ITIN

To file US tax returns and avoid excessive withholding, you must obtain an **Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN)** from the IRS. Apply using:
- **Form W-7** (Application for IRS Individual Taxpayer Identification Number)
- Mail to the IRS with supporting documents (Canadian passport copy + rental property documentation)
- Processing time: 4–6 weeks

Without an ITIN, rental withholding defaults to **30% of gross rents** under IRC Section 1441, even if you don't owe that much tax.

### File Form 1040-NR

Non-resident aliens file the **1040-NR** (U.S. Tax Return for Nonresident Alien Individuals), not the standard 1040 form. This is mandatory if:
- You have US-source rental income, OR
- You had US tax withheld during the year

**Filing deadline:** June 15, 2025 (for 2024 tax year). If filing electronically via an authorized e-file provider, the deadline extends to October 15, 2025, but you should aim for June 15 to reclaim overpaid withholding.

### Schedule E and Rental Income Reporting

On the 1040-NR, attach **Schedule E** (Supplemental Income or Loss) and report:
- Property address and description
- Rental income (gross)
- Deductible expenses: property tax, insurance, repairs, maintenance, utilities, mortgage interest, HOA fees (if applicable), advertising, property management fees
- Depreciation (if claiming CCA equivalent on the US side)

**Do not claim a deduction for CCA/depreciation** on the US return if you don't claim it on the Canadian return. Coordinate CCA strategy between both returns to avoid conflicts.

### Section 871(d) Election

This is critical. Under **Section 871(d) of the Internal Revenue Code**, a non-resident alien can elect to treat rental real estate income as if it were "effectively connected income" (ECI). This allows:
- Filing a full 1040-NR with deductions
- Applying graduated tax rates (up to 37% federal) instead of a flat 30% withholding
- Usually results in a **lower effective tax rate** than the default 30% withholding

To make the election:
- Attach a statement to your 1040-NR indicating you elect Section 871(d) treatment
- Include the statement in your tax return filed before the deadline
- The election applies to all rental real estate income for that tax year

For most Quebec residents with modest rental income, the Section 871(d) election reduces tax liability significantly. Example: If your rental income is $25,000 USD and your deductible expenses are $8,000 USD, your taxable income is $17,000. Federal tax at graduated rates (~12%) is roughly $2,040, far less than 30% of $25,000 ($7,500).

### Part XIII Withholding and Form NR6

If you don't file a US tax return, the CRA can withhold up to **25% of gross rents** under Part XIII withholding rules. To avoid this, your US property manager or tenant must file **Form NR6** (Undertaking to File an Income Tax Return by a Non-Resident of Canada) with the CRA. This confirms you'll file a US return and prevents the default CRA withholding.

Many US property managers are unfamiliar with Form NR6. Provide them with a copy and ensure it's filed by **June 15** of the tax year.

## West Virginia State Tax Obligations

### State Income Tax Filing

West Virginia taxes non-resident rental income at a **flat 6.5% rate** (as of 2024; rates can change—verify current rates at the West Virginia Department of Tax and Revenue website).

Non-residents must file:
- **Form NR-2** (West Virginia Nonresident Tax Return), or
- The equivalent schedule attached to the federal 1040-NR

**Filing deadline:** Same as federal (June 15 / October 15 for e-file).

Report the same rental income and deductions used on Schedule E (1040-NR).

### Property Tax

West Virginia property tax averages **0.59% of fair market value** annually, though rates vary by county. This is deductible on both your US and Canadian returns.

Calculate annual property tax from your WV property tax bill or the assessor's office website, convert to CAD, and deduct on Form T776 (Canada) and Schedule E (US).

## Selling the Property: FIRPTA Basics

If you sell your West Virginia property, the **Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA)** applies. Here's the mechanics:

- The **buyer must withhold 15% of the sale proceeds** and remit to the IRS
- You file a **Form 8288** (U.S. Withholding Tax Return for Disposition by Foreign Persons) with the IRS within 10 days of closing
- You report the gain/loss on a supplemental 1040-NR filed in the year of sale
- You claim the 15% withholding as a credit against your US tax liability

For capital gains treatment in Canada: calculate adjusted cost basis in CAD, subtract proceeds in CAD, and report the capital gain on your Canadian return.
