# Tax Guide for Northwest Territories Landlords with Alaska Rental Property

## Overview: Why This Combination Matters

As a Northwest Territories resident owning rental property in Alaska, you operate in a unique tax environment. You're subject to Canadian federal and territorial taxation on your worldwide income, including US rental revenue—but Alaska offers a significant advantage: it has no state income tax. This means your US tax liability is limited to federal taxes only, reducing your overall tax burden compared to landlords in other US states.

However, this advantage comes with compliance obligations in both countries. The Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) treats US rental income as taxable Canadian income and requires detailed reporting. The US Internal Revenue Service (IRS) requires non-resident aliens (which includes Canadian citizens) to file US tax returns and pay federal income tax on US-source rental income. Navigating these obligations correctly prevents penalties, withholding issues, and double taxation.

This guide covers your filing requirements, tax rates, key forms, and strategic elections available to minimize your tax burden.

## Canadian Tax Obligations: CRA Requirements

### Reporting Rental Income on Your Canadian Return

All rental income from your Alaska property must be reported on your Canadian personal tax return. The primary form is the **T776 (Statement of Real Estate Rentals)**, which is submitted with your annual T1 personal income tax return to the CRA.

On the T776, you must report:
- **Gross rental income** in Canadian dollars (converted at the exchange rate applicable to the date received)
- All **rental expenses**: property taxes, insurance, utilities, repairs, advertising, property management fees, condo fees (if applicable), and mortgage interest
- **Capital cost allowance (CCA)** if you elect to claim depreciation

For 2025, use the Bank of Canada annual average exchange rate of **1 USD = 1.36 CAD** for the year's rental income and expenses, unless you elect to use a different reasonable method consistently.

Your net rental income (or loss) is added to your other Canadian income and taxed at your marginal rate in the Northwest Territories. NT marginal tax rates for 2024 range from 5.05% (lowest bracket) to 43.05% (highest bracket). Combined federal and territorial rates reach approximately **53.5%** at the top bracket.

### Form T1135: Foreign Property Reporting

If the fair market value of your Alaska rental property exceeds **CAD $100,000** at any time during the year, you must file Form **T1135 (Foreign Income Verification Statement)** with your Canadian tax return. This form requires disclosure of:
- Property location and description
- Fair market value in Canadian dollars
- Type of income earned (rental income)
- Country where tax was paid

Failure to file T1135 when required results in a penalty of **$25 per day** (maximum $2,500 per year for individuals).

### Foreign Tax Credit (FTC)

You can claim a **Foreign Tax Credit** on your Canadian return for US federal income tax paid on your Alaska rental income. This prevents double taxation.

Use **Form T2209 (Federal Foreign Tax Credit)** to calculate your FTC. The credit is limited to the lesser of:
- US federal tax actually paid on rental income, or
- Canadian federal tax that would be payable on the same income

The FTC reduces your Canadian federal tax dollar-for-dollar (up to the limit). Any excess US tax paid cannot be refunded but may be carried back three years or forward ten years.

## US Tax Obligations: IRS Requirements

### Obtaining an ITIN

As a Canadian citizen without a US Social Security Number (SSN), you must obtain an **Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN)** from the IRS. An ITIN allows you to file US tax returns and is required to claim treaty benefits.

Apply for an ITIN using:
- **Form W-7 (Application for IRS Individual Taxpayer Identification Number)**, or
- **Form W-7EZ** (simpler version if you meet eligibility requirements)

Submit the application with a certified copy of your passport. Processing typically takes 4–6 weeks. Your ITIN begins with "9" and is formatted like an SSN (###-##-####).

### Filing Form 1040-NR: US Non-Resident Alien Return

You must file a **Form 1040-NR (U.S. Income Tax Return for Nonresident Aliens)** with the IRS annually to report your Alaska rental income and claim the **Section 871(d) election** (discussed below).

**Filing deadline**: June 15, 2026 (for 2025 tax year). The deadline for non-residents is automatically extended to June 15; no extension request is required.

**Where to file**: Internal Revenue Service, Austin, TX 73301, USA (use the address specified in the current Form 1040-NR instructions for your country of residence).

On Form 1040-NR, you will report:
- Gross rental income (in USD)
- All deductible rental expenses
- Net rental income or loss

### Schedule E: Reporting Rental Property Details

Attach **Schedule E (Supplemental Income or Loss)** to your Form 1040-NR. Schedule E requires:
- Property address (Alaska rental)
- Number of days rented at fair rental value
- Days of personal use
- Gross rental income
- Itemized rental expenses

Proper completion of Schedule E ensures the IRS correctly attributes income to the property and allows full deduction of expenses.

### Section 871(d) Election: The Key Tax Strategy

The **Section 871(d) election** is critical for reducing your US tax burden. Without this election, the default withholding rate on your rental income is **30%** of gross rents. With the election, you are taxed instead on **net rental income** (gross income minus expenses) at regular graduated US federal tax rates.

**Why this matters**: For most Canadian landlords, net income after expenses is significantly lower than gross income, resulting in lower overall US federal tax.

**How to make the election**:
1. Complete Form 1040-NR showing net rental income
2. Attach a statement titled "Section 871(d) Election" specifying:
   - The property address in Alaska
   - Your ITIN
   - A declaration that you elect to be taxed on net income rather than gross income
3. File Form 1040-NR with your Section 871(d) statement by the June 15 deadline

Once filed, the election applies to that property for the year and future years until revoked.

**US federal tax rates (2025)** on net income range from **10%** (lowest bracket) to **37%** (highest bracket) for single filers.

## Alaska's Tax Advantage: No State Income Tax

Alaska imposes **zero state income tax** on individuals. This is a substantial advantage compared to other US states where you would owe state income tax in addition to federal tax.

However, Alaska does impose property taxes:
- **Average effective property tax rate**: **1.19%** of assessed value
- **Assessment ratios vary by municipality**: typically 10–20% of fair market value
- Property taxes are deductible on your US Form 1040-NR and your Canadian T776

Example: A CAD $400,000 Alaska property assessed at 15% of value (USD $294,000 CAD) would incur approximately USD $3,500 in annual property tax (1.19% × $294,000), which is deductible.

## Selling the Property: FIRPTA Basics

If you sell your Alaska rental property, US federal tax applies under the **Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA)**. 

Key points:
- The sale proceeds are subject to US federal capital gains tax
- FIRPTA requires the buyer (or buyer's attorney) to withhold **15%** of the net sale proceeds and remit to the IRS
- You must file a US tax return (Form 1040-NR) reporting the sale and capital gain
- You can claim a Foreign Tax Credit on your Canadian return for US tax paid

Report the capital gain on your Canadian return (Form T776 and Schedule 3 of your T1) and claim an FTC to avoid double taxation.
