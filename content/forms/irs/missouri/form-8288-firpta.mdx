# Form 8288: FIRPTA Withholding for Canadian Landlords Selling Missouri Property

## What Is Form 8288?

Form 8288 (US Withholding Tax Return for Dispositions by Foreign Persons of US Real Property Interests) is the IRS mechanism for collecting and remitting tax when a foreign national—including a Canadian citizen—sells US real estate. It's part of the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA), enacted to ensure the US government captures tax revenue on property dispositions by non-residents.

When you as a Canadian landlord sell rental property in Missouri, the buyer's closing agent is legally obligated to withhold 15% of the **gross sale price** and remit it to the IRS using Form 8288. This withholding is not optional; it's a statutory requirement that protects the US Treasury.

The good news: you can apply for a **withholding certificate** (Form 8288-B) to reduce or eliminate this withholding if your tax liability is projected to be lower than 15% of the sale price.

## How FIRPTA Applies Specifically in Missouri

Missouri presents a particular scenario for Canadian landlords because it combines federal withholding obligations with state-level tax considerations.

**Federal Level:**
- 15% withholding on gross proceeds applies uniformly across all US states
- This withholding is credited against your federal income tax liability for the year of sale

**Missouri State Level:**
- Missouri imposes a 4.95% state income tax on rental income from Missouri property
- As a non-resident, you must file a Missouri state return (Form MO-1040) reporting the sale and claiming any available deductions
- Missouri does **not** impose withholding on real estate sales at the state level, but you remain liable for state income tax on any gain
- Missouri's average effective property tax rate is 1.01%, which may apply during your ownership period but not on the sale itself

**Example:**
If you sell Missouri rental property for $200,000:
- Federal withholding: $30,000 (15% × $200,000)
- Your actual federal tax liability on the gain depends on your adjusted basis, depreciation recapture, and other factors
- You'll also owe Missouri state tax on your gain
- These withholdings and taxes are separate obligations

## Who Files Form 8288?

Two parties can file Form 8288:

1. **The Buyer's Agent or Closing Agent** (Most Common)
   - Responsible for withholding 15% of gross proceeds at closing
   - Must file Form 8288 with the IRS within 20 days of the transfer date
   - Must provide you with a copy of Form 8288 after filing

2. **The Foreign Seller** (You, if seeking reduced withholding)
   - You can file Form 8288 to apply for a withholding certificate
   - This allows you to reduce withholding if you can demonstrate lower tax liability
   - Must be filed before the transfer date or you risk full 15% withholding

As a Canadian landlord, you typically won't file the initial Form 8288—the buyer's agent does. However, you may file Form 8288 **preemptively** if you want reduced withholding.

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form 8288

If you're applying for a withholding certificate to reduce withholding:

**Part I: Return Information**
- Enter your name, address, and US TIN (Individual Taxpayer Number) or ITIN
- Provide the property address in Missouri
- Enter the date of transfer
- Report the gross sales price

**Part II: Deductions and Basis**
- Schedule your adjusted basis (original purchase price plus improvements, minus depreciation claimed)
- Calculate your realized gain (sale price minus adjusted basis)
- List deductions (selling expenses, real estate commissions, etc.)
- Show your estimated taxable gain

**Part III: Tax Calculation**
- Apply your estimated tax rate (likely 15% federal + 4.95% Missouri state tax = approximately 20% combined, depending on depreciation recapture rates)
- Calculate the tax you expect to owe
- If this is less than 15% of gross proceeds, explain why reduced withholding is appropriate
- Request a specific withholding percentage

**Part IV: Certification**
- Sign and date the form
- If you have a US tax professional or attorney, they may sign as your representative

## Missouri-Specific Considerations

**1. Depreciation Recapture**

If you held your Missouri rental property for business purposes and claimed depreciation deductions on your US federal returns (and likely on your Canadian T1 return via foreign property rules), the sale triggers Section 1250 recapture. This portion is taxed at ordinary rates rather than capital gains rates.

For example:
- Original basis: $150,000
- Depreciation claimed: $30,000 (over holding period)
- Adjusted basis: $120,000
- Sale price: $200,000
- Recognized gain: $80,000
- Of which ~$30,000 may be recapture (taxed at ordinary rates, up to 25%)

This recapture is factored into your withholding certificate calculation.

**2. State Return Filing Requirement**

Even if the buyer withholds 15% of proceeds, you remain liable for Missouri income tax on your gain. You must file a Missouri Form MO-1040 (non-resident return) for the year of sale. Any state tax paid can be claimed on your US federal return, and the federal withholding can be credited against your federal liability.

**3. Property Tax Considerations**

Missouri's 1.01% average effective property tax rate means your annual carrying costs were likely higher than in lower-tax states. Ensure you've accounted for all deductible property taxes and expenses when calculating your adjusted basis and gain.

**4. Canada-US Tax Treaty**

Under Article XIII of the Canada-US Tax Treaty, the US has the right to tax real property gains. However, the treaty contains provisions allowing Canadian residents to claim foreign tax credits. The 15% federal withholding and Missouri state taxes paid are creditable against your Canadian income tax liability on the same gain when reported on your Canadian T1 return.

You'll report the US source income and foreign taxes paid on your Canadian return using Schedule 11 (Foreign Tax Credit), which helps prevent double taxation.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Assuming the Buyer Will Handle Everything**

Don't assume the closing agent will correctly identify you as a foreign person or properly withhold. Communicate your non-resident status clearly. Provide a copy of Form W-8BEN (Certificate of Foreign Status) so the buyer's agent understands their withholding obligation.

**2. Forgetting to File Form 8288-B Preemptively**

The 20-day filing deadline is tight. If you want reduced withholding and don't file Form 8288-B before closing, the buyer must withhold 15%. You'll have to wait for a refund, which can take months. File early.

**3. Not Accounting for Depreciation Recapture**

Many sellers underestimate their tax liability because they forget that depreciation claimed reduces basis and creates ordinary income recapture. This inflates your apparent tax liability and may prevent withholding reduction.

**4. Ignoring Missouri State Tax Obligations**

Don't assume federal withholding satisfies all tax duties. Missouri state tax is separate. Failure to file a Missouri non-resident return can result in penalties and interest.

**5. Miscalculating the Gross Proceeds**

For FIRPTA purposes, "gross proceeds" means the total sale price, not net proceeds after real estate commissions. The 15% is withheld on the full amount before commission deductions are made.

## Key Deadlines

- **Before Transfer:** File Form 8288-B for a withholding certificate to reduce withholding
- **At Closing:** Ensure the buyer withholds and provides you with Form 8288 documentation
- **20 Days After Transfer:** Buyer must file Form 8288 with the IRS
- **April 15 (Following Tax Year):** File your US federal return (Form 1040) claiming the withholding as a credit
- **April 15 (Following Tax Year):** File Missouri Form MO-1040 non-resident return
- **June 15 (Following Tax Year):** File your Canadian T1 return, reporting the US source income and claiming foreign tax credits

## Key Takeaways for Missouri Landlords

- **The 15% federal withholding is mandatory unless you obtain a Form 8288-B certificate beforehand.** Acting early prevents cash flow disruption at closing and speeds up refunds.

- **Missouri state income tax (4.95%) on your gain is separate from federal withholding.** You must file a Missouri non-resident return and account for state tax liability
