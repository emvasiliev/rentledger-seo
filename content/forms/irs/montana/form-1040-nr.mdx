# Form 1040-NR for Canadian Landlords with Montana Rental Property

## What Is Form 1040-NR?

Form 1040-NR (U.S. Income Tax Return for Nonresident Alien Individuals) is the primary federal income tax return filed by non-resident aliens—including Canadian citizens—who have U.S.-source income subject to U.S. taxation. For Canadian landlords owning rental property in Montana, this form becomes essential when you elect to treat your rental income as "effectively connected income" (ECI) under Section 871(d) of the Internal Revenue Code.

Without this election, your Montana rental income would be subject to a flat 30% withholding tax under Section 871(a). By filing Form 1040-NR and making the Section 871(d) election, you report actual net rental income (gross rents minus deductible expenses) and pay tax at graduated rates—often resulting in significant tax savings.

## How Form 1040-NR Applies to Montana Landlords

### Federal Tax Treatment

When you own rental property in Montana, the IRS treats you as having "effectively connected income" with a U.S. trade or business. Form 1040-NR allows you to:

- Report gross Montana rental income
- Deduct mortgage interest, property taxes, insurance, repairs, and depreciation
- Pay tax only on net rental income at applicable federal rates (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, or 37% for 2024)
- Claim the standard deduction (if eligible) or itemize deductions

The Section 871(d) election itself is made by filing Form 1040-NR with a statement attached to your return indicating your intent to be taxed as a U.S. resident on your rental income.

### Montana State Tax Considerations

**Montana imposes a separate state income tax on rental income of non-residents at a flat rate of 6.75%.** This is in addition to your federal liability. You must file **Montana Form NR (Nonresident Income Tax Return)** concurrently with your federal return.

Montana's approach differs from some states: it taxes non-residents on income "sourced" to Montana, which clearly includes rental property income. Montana does not recognize the federal Section 871(d) election, so you cannot avoid the 6.75% state tax by filing Form 1040-NR alone.

**Example:** If your Montana rental property generates $50,000 in net rental income:
- Federal tax (assuming 22% bracket): ~$11,000
- Montana state tax (6.75%): $3,375
- Combined: ~$14,375

Montana's property tax rate averages **0.84% annually**, which you can deduct on both your federal and Montana returns, providing some offset.

## Who Must File Form 1040-NR

You must file Form 1040-NR if you meet all of these criteria:

1. You are a non-resident alien for U.S. tax purposes (not a U.S. citizen or green card holder)
2. You have Montana rental property generating income
3. You intend to claim deductions against that rental income (making the Section 871(d) election)
4. Your U.S.-source income exceeds the filing threshold

**Canadian resident status:** If you are a Canadian citizen and resident in Canada, you are generally classified as a non-resident alien by the IRS, even if you have a Social Insurance Number (SIN). The Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty (Article IV) defines "resident" for treaty purposes, but the IRS applies its own residency rules for Form 1040-NR purposes.

If you have taken U.S. substantial presence (more than 183 days in the U.S. during the testing period), consult a cross-border specialist—you may be classified as a U.S. resident alien and must file Form 1040 instead.

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form 1040-NR for Montana Rental Income

### Step 1: Gather Required Information

- Your Canadian Social Insurance Number (SIN) and U.S. Individual Identification Number (ITIN) or Employer Identification Number (EIN) if applicable
- Montana property address and legal description
- Gross rental income for the tax year
- Mortgage statement(s) showing interest paid
- Property tax assessment and payment records
- Insurance premium receipts
- Records of repairs, maintenance, utilities, and other expenses
- Depreciation schedule from your initial property acquisition

### Step 2: Calculate Net Rental Income

Complete **Schedule E (Supplemental Income or Loss)**, Part I:

- **Line 3:** Enter gross Montana rental income
- **Lines 5–18:** Deduct allowable expenses:
  - Mortgage interest (not principal)
  - Property taxes (~0.84% of assessed value in Montana)
  - Insurance premiums
  - Utilities (if you pay them)
  - Repairs and maintenance
  - Depreciation (typically 27.5 years for residential property)
  - Advertising and management fees

The **net amount on Schedule E becomes your taxable rental income.**

### Step 3: Complete Form 1040-NR, Part II (Income)

- **Line 8c:** Enter net rental income from Schedule E
- **Line 10:** Total income

Form 1040-NR differs from the standard Form 1040 in that it has separate sections for residents versus non-residents and limits the deductions available.

### Step 4: Make the Section 871(d) Election

**Attach a statement to Form 1040-NR** stating: "Under Section 871(d) of the Internal Revenue Code, I elect to be treated as engaged in a U.S. trade or business with respect to rental income from real property located in Montana during the tax year."

This election allows you to deduct expenses against your gross income rather than paying the 30% withholding on gross rent.

### Step 5: File Montana Form NR

Simultaneously, complete **Montana Department of Revenue Form NR (Nonresident Income Tax Return)**:

- Report the same net rental income from your federal Schedule E
- Montana allows deductions similar to federal (mortgage interest, property taxes, depreciation)
- Calculate Montana tax at the flat 6.75% rate on your net income

Montana Form NR is due **April 15** or **June 15** (if you have an extension).

### Step 6: Calculate Foreign Tax Credit (if applicable)

As a Canadian resident, you will also file a **Canadian T1 return** reporting the same U.S. rental income in Canadian dollars. Canada taxes worldwide income, including U.S. rental property income.

To avoid double taxation, you can claim a **Foreign Tax Credit (FTC)** on your Canadian return for the U.S. federal and state taxes paid. Conversely, you may be able to claim a credit on Form 1040-NR for Canadian taxes paid, though this is less common and requires careful planning.

**Important:** The Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty (Article XXIV) includes provisions to prevent double taxation. Coordinate your filings with a cross-border accountant to maximize treaty benefits.

## Montana-Specific Considerations

### Property Tax Deduction

Montana's 0.84% average property tax is fully deductible on both federal Form 1040-NR and Montana Form NR. If your property is in an area with higher-than-average assessments, ensure your property tax statement clearly shows the assessed value and tax paid.

### No Montana Withholding Requirement

Unlike some states, Montana does not require rental income withholding for non-residents. However, you remain responsible for filing Form NR and paying the 6.75% state tax by April 15. Failure to do so may result in penalties and interest.

### Rental Agent and Management

If you use a Montana property management company, ensure they provide a year-end statement itemizing gross rent, vacancies, and expenses paid on your behalf. Some management companies issue **Form 1098 (Mortgage Interest Statement)** if they collect mortgage payments; others provide a standard management statement.

### FIRPTA Considerations

When you eventually sell the Montana property, the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA) will apply. Non-resident aliens typically must have 15% of the gross sale price withheld by the buyer/closing agent. This is separate from the annual rental income filing but affects your long-term planning.
