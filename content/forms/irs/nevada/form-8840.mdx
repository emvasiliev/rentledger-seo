# Form 8840: Closer Connection Exception for Canadian Landlords with Nevada Property

## What Is Form 8840?

Form 8840 (Closer Connection Exception Statement for Aliens) is an IRS form that allows Canadian citizens to claim they maintain a closer connection to Canada despite meeting the US Substantial Presence Test (SPT). Without Form 8840, you could be classified as a US resident for tax purposes—triggering worldwide income reporting obligations, estate tax exposure, and additional compliance requirements.

The form essentially tells the IRS: "Yes, I was physically present in the US enough to trigger the SPT, but my tax home and permanent residence remain in Canada."

## Understanding the Substantial Presence Test

The SPT counts:
- **Full days in the US**: All days you're physically present (including partial days of arrival/departure)
- **Formula**: Current year days + (⅓ of days in prior year) + (⅙ of days in second prior year)
- **Threshold**: 183 cumulative weighted days = presumed US resident

For Canadian snowbird landlords managing Nevada rental property, even brief management visits compound quickly. A landlord who spends 120 days in Nevada annually, plus visits in prior years, can easily exceed 183 weighted days.

## Why Form 8840 Matters for Nevada Landlords

Nevada presents a unique scenario for Canadian real estate investors:

**No state income tax advantage**: Nevada has no state income tax (unlike California, which taxes residents at up to 13.3%). This makes Nevada attractive for US investment. However, this advantage *disappears* if you're classified as a US federal resident—you'll still owe federal income tax on worldwide income, plus potentially estate tax.

**Primary concern**: Federal classification as a "US resident alien" under the SPT, not Nevada state tax (which doesn't exist).

The Canada-US Tax Treaty (Article IV) defines tax residence based on where you have a permanent home available and, secondarily, where your center of vital interests lies. Form 8840 documents this treaty protection.

## Who Must File Form 8840

You must file if **all** these conditions apply:

1. You are a Canadian citizen (not a US citizen or green card holder)
2. You meet the Substantial Presence Test in the calendar year
3. You were not a US resident in either of the two preceding years
4. You did not apply for a green card during the year
5. You maintain a closer connection to Canada

**Critical**: Filing Form 8840 is *not* optional if you meet these criteria. Failure to file when required can result in:
- Reclassification as a US resident for the entire tax year
- Liability for back taxes on worldwide income
- FIRPTA compliance issues on Nevada property sales
- Estate tax exposure on worldwide assets (40% federal tax on estates over ~$13.61 million USD in 2024)

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form 8840

### Part I: Identification
- Enter your name, Canadian SIN (as foreign tax ID number), and Canadian address
- Use your Canadian mailing address—this reinforces closer connection

### Part II: Substantial Presence Test Calculation
Show your day count:
- Current year: Count full and partial days in the US
- Prior year: Divide total days by 3
- Second prior year: Divide total days by 6
- **Total weighted days**: Must equal 183 or more to justify filing

*Example*: 120 days in current year + 90/3 (30) + 60/6 (10) = 160 days. Below threshold? You may not need Form 8840. At or above? You do.

### Part III: Closer Connection Factors
Check boxes indicating your closer connection to Canada:

- **Permanent home**: Your principal residence is in Canada (this is typically the strongest factor)
- **Family/dependents**: Spouse, children, or parents reside in Canada
- **Employment**: Primary employment is in Canada
- **Professional licenses/memberships**: Valid Canadian professional designation (accountant, lawyer, physician)
- **Bank accounts/financial ties**: Significant financial accounts in Canada
- **Social/cultural ties**: Clubs, religious organizations, community involvement in Canada
- **Business interests**: Significant business operations in Canada

### Part IV: Tax Home Location
Declare your US tax home location (the city in Nevada where you stay, if applicable) and confirm your **abode** is in Canada.

## Nevada-Specific Considerations

### Property Tax and Rental Income Documentation

Nevada's average effective property tax rate is **0.59%**—among the lowest in the US. This attractive rate drives BC and Alberta landlords to invest, but the rental income itself triggers Form 8840 filing obligations.

**Documentation you'll need**:
- **Deed and proof of ownership** (Clark County Recorder if Las Vegas/Henderson area)
- **Nevada rental property address and legal description**
- **Rental income statements** for the current year (Nevada has no state income tax, but federal Schedule E applies)
- **Days present at the property** (keep a calendar; be conservative and count any partial day)

### No State Income Tax ≠ No Federal Compliance

A common misconception: "Nevada has no income tax, so I don't need to file." This is **false**. Even though Nevada doesn't tax residents, the federal government does. If classified as a US resident, you owe:
- Federal income tax on worldwide income (including Canadian employment, pensions, investment income)
- Self-employment tax if applicable
- Potential FIRPTA withholding when you sell the Nevada property

### Clark County Property Management Visits

If you hire a Clark County or Washoe County property manager, your trips to Nevada may decrease, strengthening your Form 8840 claim. Document that you rely on professional management for day-to-day operations, reinforcing that your permanent home is Canada.

## Connecting Form 8840 to Your Canadian Tax Return

On your **Canadian T1 return**, you must:

1. **Report Nevada rental income**: Schedule 11 (Canadian residents reporting US rental income)
2. **Claim foreign tax credits**: Form T776 and Schedule 8 (for US federal income tax and property tax paid)
3. **File Form 8840 with the IRS** (not CRA)—attach a copy to your T1 for your records

The Canada-US Tax Treaty provides relief:
- **Article X**: US can tax Nevada real property rental income
- **Article XXIII**: Foreign tax credit allows you to offset US federal tax against Canadian tax
- **Article IV**: Closer connection (Form 8840) establishes Canadian residency for treaty purposes

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Miscounting days under the SPT**
- Include arrival and departure days as full days
- Count partial days (e.g., a morning flight out counts as a day present)
- Don't exclude days you're at the Nevada property but in Nevada for other reasons

**2. Filing Form 8840 late or not at all**
- Deadline: June 15 of the following year (not April 15)
- Late filing = loss of closer connection protection for that year
- Must file annually to maintain protection

**3. Overstating closer connection factors**
- The IRS scrutinizes frequency and nature of ties
- "Visits Canada once yearly" is weaker than "maintains primary home, spouse, and employment in Canada"
- Be honest; exaggeration invites audit

**4. Ignoring FIRPTA on property sales**
- If you sell Nevada rental property and are classified as a US resident, FIRPTA requires 15% withholding
- If you file Form 8840, you may claim exemption, but documentation must be crystal clear

**5. Not coordinating US and Canadian filings**
- Form 8840 to IRS, T1 to CRA, rental income reported on both
- Mismatches trigger audit flags
- Keep copies of Form 8840 with Canadian tax records

## Key Takeaways for Nevada Landlords

- **Form 8840 is required, not optional**: If you meet the Substantial Presence Test and maintain a closer connection to Canada, filing is mandatory. Failure to file results in US resident classification and worldwide income reporting obligations—eliminating any state tax advantage Nevada offers.

- **Nevada's lack of state income tax doesn't exempt you from federal compliance**: The real risk is federal residency status, not state tax. Form 8840 protects your Canadian tax residency status for federal purposes, preserving treaty benefits and preventing double taxation on Nevada rental income.
