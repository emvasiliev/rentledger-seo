# Form 4562 for Canadian Landlords: West Virginia Rental Property Guide

## What is Form 4562?

Form 4562 (Depreciation and Amortization) is a US Internal Revenue Service (IRS) form that allows property owners to claim depreciation deductions on real property and personal property used in a trade or business. For Canadian landlords owning residential rental property in the United States, Form 4562 is the mechanism through which you claim depreciation on your West Virginia rental assets.

Depreciation is a non-cash deduction that reduces your taxable rental income by spreading the cost of depreciable property over its useful life. In the US, residential rental property qualifies for depreciation using the Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System (MACRS), which mandates a 27.5-year straight-line depreciation period.

## How Form 4562 Applies to West Virginia Rental Property

West Virginia does not impose a state income tax on out-of-state rental income for non-residents in most cases; however, this exemption is complex and depends on your specific circumstances. As a Canadian landlord, you are considered a non-resident of West Virginia for tax purposes. The state's income tax rate of 6.5% applies to West Virginia residents with income sourced within the state. Non-residents are generally not subject to West Virginia income tax on rental income, but you may still be required to file a West Virginia non-resident return (Form WV-1NR) if you had West Virginia-source income during the tax year.

The federal depreciation deduction you claim on Form 4562 is reported on your US Schedule E (Rental Income and Loss) and carried to your Form 1040-NR (US Nonresident Alien Individual Income Tax Return). Since West Virginia does not typically tax non-residents on rental income, your depreciation deduction reduces your federal taxable income on Form 1040-NR only.

West Virginia's average effective property tax rate is 0.59%, which is among the lowest in the nation. While property taxes are deductible on Schedule E as a rental expense (distinct from depreciation), this favorable rate means your overall carrying costs in West Virginia remain relatively low compared to other states.

## Who Must File Form 4562

Any landlord—Canadian resident or otherwise—who owns rental property in the United States and wishes to claim depreciation must file Form 4562. As a Canadian non-resident, you are required to file Form 1040-NR (Nonresident Alien Individual Income Tax Return) with the IRS, and Form 4562 attaches to this return.

You must file Form 4562 if:

- You own residential rental property in West Virginia and want to claim depreciation
- You acquired the property after 1986 (when MACRS began)
- You have not previously claimed depreciation on this specific property
- You are continuing to depreciate property acquired in prior years

## Step-by-Step: Completing Form 4562 for West Virginia Rental Property

### Part I: Election to Expense and Other Provisions

Part I allows you to claim "Section 179" expensing (immediate deduction of property costs) and bonus depreciation. For most Canadian landlords, these provisions do not apply to residential rental property, as they are limited to business property and certain improvements. Leave Part I blank unless you have made qualifying improvements (e.g., appliances, carpeting, or structural components) that you are expensing under Section 179.

### Part II: General Depreciation System (GDS)

This is where you report depreciation on your West Virginia residential rental property.

**Column (a) – Description of Property:** Describe your property clearly (e.g., "Residential rental property located at [address], Charleston, WV").

**Column (b) – Date Placed in Service:** Enter the month and year you first rented the property or held it available for rent. This is not the purchase date, but the date the property became income-producing.

**Column (c) – Basis for Depreciation:** This is the cost basis of the building only, not the land. Land is not depreciable. Calculate this by:
- Taking your total purchase price
- Subtracting the value allocable to land (typically 20–30% of the purchase price, depending on local assessments)
- Adding capitalized improvements (roof replacement, HVAC system, etc.)

**Column (d) – Recovery Period:** Enter "27.5 years" for residential rental property.

**Column (e) – Convention:** Enter "S/L" (straight-line method). The US tax code requires residential rental property to use the straight-line convention.

**Column (f) – Depreciation Deduction:** The IRS provides tables for calculating depreciation. For straight-line depreciation over 27.5 years:
- Annual depreciation = Depreciable basis ÷ 27.5

If the property was placed in service mid-year, use the mid-month convention (depreciation begins in the month placed in service).

### Part III: Summary

Sum all depreciation deductions from Part II. This total flows to Schedule E, line 18 (Depreciation Expense).

## West Virginia-Specific Considerations

### Non-Resident State Tax Filing

Although West Virginia generally does not tax non-residents on rental income, verify your filing obligation with West Virginia State Tax Department (phone: 1-833-WVA-TAXE). If required to file WV-1NR, report your rental income but note that depreciation is a federal deduction only; West Virginia may not allow it.

### Property Tax Deduction

West Virginia's low 0.59% property tax rate means your rental property taxes are modest. These are deductible on Schedule E as a rental expense (separate from depreciation). For a property worth $200,000, annual property taxes would average approximately $1,180, significantly lower than many other states.

### Land Allocation Challenge

When claiming depreciation, accurately allocating purchase price between building and land is critical. West Virginia property assessments are public records. Use the county assessor's valuation as a reference to justify your land allocation to the IRS.

### Canada-US Tax Treaty Implications

Under Article XXII of the Canada-US Tax Treaty, you may be able to claim a foreign tax credit on your Canadian T1 return for US taxes paid. Form 4562 depreciation reduces your US taxable income, which may reduce or eliminate US tax liability. Any remaining US tax (after depreciation deduction) can be credited against your Canadian tax on the same income.

When reporting the West Virginia rental property on your Canadian T1:

- Report the gross rental income in Canadian dollars (converted at the exchange rate on the receipt date)
- Deduct US rental expenses (including depreciation) on a separate Canadian statement
- Report the net rental income
- Claim a foreign tax credit for US taxes paid using Form T2036

### Recapture on Sale

Depreciation claimed on Form 4562 must be recaptured when you sell the property. The IRS taxes recaptured depreciation at a flat 25% rate, separate from capital gains tax. As a Canadian, you'll report the gain on your T1 return; the recapture portion may create a mismatch between US and Canadian tax treatment, necessitating careful calculation and disclosure.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Including Land in Depreciable Basis:** Land cannot be depreciated. Failing to properly exclude land value is one of the most frequent errors. Use a professional appraisal or assessor data to support your allocation.

2. **Depreciating Property You Don't Own:** Ensure you have legal ownership and that the property is held for income-production. Personal residences do not qualify.

3. **Incorrect Date Placed in Service:** Use the date the property was first rented or available for rent, not the purchase date. Mistakes here affect depreciation start date and calculation.

4. **Mixing US and Canadian Depreciation Methods:** Canada allows capital cost allowance (CCA) at variable rates. The US requires 27.5-year straight-line. File Form 4562 using US rules; reconcile differences on your T1 return.

5. **Failing to Carry Depreciation to Schedule E:** Form 4562 calculates the deduction, but it must be reported on Schedule E (line 18). Missing this step means the deduction does not reduce income.

6. **Not Filing Form 4562 at All:** If you own a rental property, you must file Form 4562 to claim depreciation. Omitting it forfeits the deduction that year and complicates future calculations.
