# Form 1040-NR for Canadian Landlords with Kentucky Rental Property

## What is Form 1040-NR?

Form 1040-NR (U.S. Income Tax Return for Nonresident Alien Individuals) is the primary federal income tax return filed by non-resident aliens—including Canadian citizens—who have U.S. source income subject to taxation. For Canadian landlords owning rental property in Kentucky, this form becomes essential when you elect to treat your rental income as "effectively connected income" (ECI) under Internal Revenue Code Section 871(d).

Unlike the standard Form 1040 filed by U.S. residents, the 1040-NR is specifically designed to report income that is connected to a U.S. trade or business and to claim deductions and credits available only to non-resident aliens. The election under Section 871(d) is particularly valuable because it allows you to deduct rental expenses (mortgage interest, property tax, repairs, utilities, depreciation, and management fees) directly against your rental income, rather than paying a flat 30% withholding tax on gross rental income under Section 871(a).

## How Form 1040-NR Applies in Kentucky

Kentucky presents a unique tax environment for Canadian landlords. The state imposes both a personal income tax (4.5% on net rental income) and property tax (average effective rate of 0.86%). Understanding the interplay between federal Form 1040-NR requirements and Kentucky state obligations is critical for compliance and tax planning.

**Federal Taxation Structure**

When you own rental property in Kentucky, the IRS treats your rental income as effectively connected income (ECI). Under the Section 871(d) election, you report this income on Form 1040-NR and calculate your federal tax liability using the standard U.S. tax rate schedule. This is significantly more favorable than the default 30% withholding rate applied to non-resident aliens under Section 871(a).

**Kentucky State Tax Implications**

Kentucky requires non-resident aliens with Kentucky-source income to file Form K-1, Kentucky Nonresident Return, in addition to the federal Form 1040-NR. Kentucky's 4.5% state income tax rate applies to your net rental income (after deducting mortgage interest, property tax, utilities, repairs, insurance, and depreciation). This state return is due on the same date as your federal return: April 15th.

**Property Tax Considerations**

While property tax is not a state income tax, Kentucky's 0.86% average effective rate is paid to county assessors and is fully deductible on both your federal Form 1040-NR (as part of your rental expense deductions) and on your Kentucky nonresident return. This double deduction benefit significantly reduces your overall tax liability.

**Canada-US Tax Treaty Benefits**

The Canada-U.S. Income and Estate Tax Treaty (Article XXII) provides relief from double taxation. Canadian landlords may claim a foreign tax credit on their Canadian T1 return for federal and state taxes paid to the U.S. Proper documentation of Form 1040-NR and Kentucky tax payments is essential for substantiating these credits with Canada Revenue Agency (CRA).

## Who Must File Form 1040-NR in Kentucky

You must file Form 1040-NR if you are:

- A Canadian citizen or permanent resident without U.S. citizenship
- Deriving income from Kentucky rental property that qualifies as effectively connected income
- Making a Section 871(d) election to deduct expenses against rental income
- Not subject to U.S. federal income tax withholding (or subject to withholding below your actual tax liability)

You must file even if your net rental income is zero or negative, provided you have made the Section 871(d) election. The election itself is made by attaching a statement to your Form 1040-NR in the year you first claim deductions against the rental income.

## Step-by-Step: Completing Form 1040-NR

### Part I: Identification and Filing Information

Enter your name, Canadian address, and Identification Number (ITIN). If you do not have an ITIN, you must apply for one using Form W-7 before filing your return. Include your individual identification number from Canada (Social Insurance Number, for reference only—do not submit).

Check the box for "Nonresident alien" and the box for "Single" or your applicable filing status under U.S. law (married status is not recognized for non-resident aliens unless you elect to file jointly with a U.S. resident spouse).

### Part II: Income Section

**Line 8 (Rent Income)**

Report the gross rental income received from your Kentucky property in U.S. dollars. Convert Canadian receipts at the average exchange rate for the tax year (found on CRA's historical rates or the IRS's approved rates).

Enter only your Kentucky rental income here. If you own property in multiple U.S. states, report each state's income separately and aggregate on the total line.

### Part III: Deductions

This is where the Section 871(d) election provides tremendous value. You may deduct all ordinary and necessary business expenses related to the Kentucky property:

- Mortgage interest (not principal)
- Property tax (Kentucky average 0.86%)
- Utilities and maintenance
- Repairs (not capital improvements)
- Property management fees
- Insurance premiums
- Depreciation (calculated on Form 4562)
- HOA fees (if applicable)

The total of these deductions reduces your taxable income from Kentucky rentals.

### Part IV: Tax Calculation

Calculate your federal tax using the appropriate non-resident alien tax rate schedule (provided with Form 1040-NR instructions). Apply the standard graduated rates, noting that your income is combined with any other U.S.-source income you may have.

### Part V: Credits

Claim the standard deduction (not available to non-resident aliens for most U.S.-source income, with limited exceptions). You may claim certain credits including the foreign tax credit for Kentucky taxes paid.

### Part VI: Estimated Payments and Withholding

If your rental property management company has withheld U.S. federal income tax (rare for non-resident landlords), it will be reported here. Include any estimated tax payments you made directly to the IRS.

## Kentucky-Specific Considerations

**Coordination with Kentucky Form K-1**

File both Form 1040-NR federally and Kentucky Form K-1 simultaneously. The Kentucky return mirrors the federal return but applies Kentucky's 4.5% rate. Kentucky allows deduction of the same expenses as the federal return, plus an additional deduction for federal income taxes paid (though this may trigger alternative minimum tax considerations).

**Property Tax Assessment Appeals**

Kentucky property tax assessments are occasionally favorable to non-resident landlords in certain counties. Before your first filing, review your county assessor's valuation. If the assessment appears inflated relative to comparable properties, file an appeal; this directly reduces your deductible property tax expense and your taxable rental income.

**No Local Income Tax in Kentucky**

Unlike some U.S. states, Kentucky has no local (city or county) income tax. This simplifies your filing obligations and reduces your overall state tax burden compared to owning property in states like New York or Tennessee.

## Common Mistakes Canadian Landlords Make

**Mistake 1: Failing to Make the Section 871(d) Election**

Many Canadian landlords mistakenly assume they cannot deduct expenses and simply pay 30% withholding on gross income. File Form 1040-NR with the election statement in year one and save substantially on tax.

**Mistake 2: Mixing USD and CAD Without Proper Conversion**

Always convert rental income and expenses to USD using the CRA's historical monthly average rates. Mixing currencies creates audit risk and incorrect reported income.

**Mistake 3: Overlooking Foreign Tax Credit Coordination**

When filing your Canadian T1 return, claim the full foreign tax credit for both federal and Kentucky taxes paid. Failure to do so results in double taxation on the same income.

**Mistake 4: Deducting Capital Improvements as Repairs**

Replacing a roof or replacing all windows is a capital improvement (depreciated over years), not a current-year expense. Incorrectly classifying these inflates deductions and triggers IRS challenges.

## Key Deadlines for Kentucky Landlords

- **April 15**: Form 1040-NR and Kentucky Form K-1 are both due (or June 15 if no U.S. wages subject to withholding and you file electronically)
- **January 31**: Rental income statements and property tax summaries from your U.S. property manager must reach you
- **June 15** (Canada): Canadian T1 return due (if you have filed for a federal U.S. extension, CRA typically grants a matching extension)
