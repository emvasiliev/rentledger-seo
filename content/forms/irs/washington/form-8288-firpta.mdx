# Form 8288: FIRPTA Withholding for Canadian Landlords Selling Washington Rental Property

## What Is Form 8288?

Form 8288 is the **US Withholding Tax Return for Dispositions by Foreign Persons of US Real Property Interests**. Under the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA), when a foreign person (including a Canadian citizen or Canadian corporation) sells US real property, the **buyer is required to withhold 15% of the gross sale price** and remit this amount to the IRS using Form 8288.

For Canadian landlords, this represents a significant cash flow consideration. If you're selling a Washington rental property valued at $500,000, the buyer must withhold $75,000—funds you won't receive at closing until FIRPTA obligations are satisfied.

The 15% withholding rate applies unless the seller obtains a **withholding certificate** (Form 8288-B) reducing the rate based on actual US tax liability.

---

## FIRPTA Withholding Applies in Washington—Despite No State Income Tax

A critical point for Washington property owners: **even though Washington has no state income tax, federal FIRPTA withholding still applies at 15%**. The absence of state income tax does not reduce the federal withholding obligation.

This makes Washington particularly attractive for Canadian investors—you avoid Washington state income tax entirely—but you remain subject to federal FIRPTA. The withholding is a federal requirement, not a state one.

The 15% withholding is calculated on the **gross sale price**, not net proceeds. If your property sells for $600,000, the withholding is $90,000, regardless of your actual mortgage balance, selling costs, or adjusted basis.

## Who Files Form 8288?

### Primary Filer: The Buyer
**The buyer or buyer's agent must file Form 8288** within **20 days after the date of transfer** (closing). This is the buyer's legal obligation, not the seller's. However, as a Canadian seller, you should coordinate with the buyer's closing agent or title company to ensure compliance.

### Secondary Filer: The Seller (For Withholding Certificate)
If you expect the 15% withholding to exceed your actual US federal income tax liability, **you can apply for a withholding certificate (Form 8288-B) to reduce the withholding rate**. The IRS will issue a certificate specifying a lower withholding percentage based on your calculated liability.

## Step-by-Step: How Form 8288 Is Completed

### Part I: Property Information
- **Property address**: Your Washington rental property's full address
- **Legal description or parcel number**: Washington County Assessor information
- **Date of transfer**: Closing date
- **Gross sales price**: Total consideration (purchase price + any seller financing)

### Part II: Transferor Information
The buyer completes this section identifying you (the foreign transferor):
- Name, address, and country of residence
- US taxpayer identification number (ITIN) or SSN if you have one
- Canadian address

### Part III: Transferee Information
The buyer's complete information.

### Part IV: Withholding and Tax Calculation
- **15% of gross sales price** is automatically entered unless a withholding certificate reduces it
- If a withholding certificate was issued, the reduced amount is entered
- Total withholding amount to be remitted to the IRS

### Part V: Certification
The buyer or their representative signs and certifies the information is correct.

## How to Obtain a Withholding Certificate (Form 8288-B)

To reduce the 15% withholding, you must apply to the **Internal Revenue Service** for a withholding certificate **before closing**. 

### Filing Requirements for Form 8288-B:
1. **Submit IRS Form 8288-B** to the IRS Service Center in Philadelphia
2. **Include calculations** showing your expected US tax liability on the gain
3. **File at least 10 days before closing** to allow IRS processing time
4. The IRS will issue a certificate specifying the allowable withholding rate

### Calculate Your Expected Liability:
- **Gain on sale** = Sale price minus adjusted basis
- **Federal tax rate** = 15%, 21%, or higher depending on the gain amount (as a non-resident alien, long-term capital gains rates typically apply)
- **Example**: If your gain is $200,000, your federal tax is approximately $30,000–$42,000, justifying a withholding certificate for less than $75,000 (15% × $500,000)

**Note**: The IRS may request additional documentation, including proof of basis, property improvement records, and depreciation schedules. Work with a US tax professional to prepare this application.

## Washington-Specific Considerations for Form 8288

### No Washington State Income Tax
Unlike many US states, Washington imposes **no state income tax** on residents or non-residents. This eliminates a second layer of withholding on your sale. You are only subject to **federal withholding under FIRPTA**, not any state FIRPTA equivalent.

However, Washington **does impose property taxes** (averaging 1.03% of assessed value). These are paid by the property owner, not through FIRPTA withholding, and are deductible against your US rental income.

### FIRPTA Applies to Residential and Commercial Property
Washington FIRPTA withholding applies equally to:
- Single-family rental homes
- Multifamily apartments
- Commercial properties
- Land

No exemption exists for residential property.

### Currency Consideration for Canadian Sellers
The gross sale price is stated in USD. If you financed the purchase in CAD, exchange rate fluctuations between purchase and sale affect your actual gain and tax liability. When preparing your withholding certificate application, ensure all figures are in USD.

## Reporting the Withholding on Your Canadian Return

### Step 1: Report US Income on Form T1
As a Canadian resident, you report US rental income and the capital gain on your **Canadian T1 tax return**:
- **Line 10400**: Canadian portion of rental income
- **Line 12700**: Foreign investment income (US rental income net of US expenses)
- **Schedule 3, Section 2**: Capital gain on the property sale

### Step 2: Claim Foreign Tax Credit (Form T776 and Schedule 1)
The 15% FIRPTA withholding (or reduced withholding amount) is a **US federal tax payment** and qualifies for the **Foreign Tax Credit** on your Canadian return:
- Report the withholding amount on **Schedule 1, Line 40500** (Non-business income tax paid to other countries)
- The Canada-US Tax Treaty (Article 24) allows you to claim this credit

The foreign tax credit prevents double taxation: you claim the US withholding against your Canadian tax liability on the same income.

## Common Mistakes Canadian Sellers Make

### 1. Not Applying for a Withholding Certificate
Many Canadian sellers accept the 15% withholding without exploring reduction options. If your actual tax liability is lower, you're over-withholding. Filing Form 8288-B typically saves $10,000–$30,000.

### 2. Assuming Washington Property Tax Reduces FIRPTA Withholding
Property taxes do not reduce the 15% withholding amount. The withholding is based on gross sales price, not net proceeds. Property taxes are accounted for separately as a deduction on your US and Canadian returns.

### 3. Failing to Report the Sale on Your Canadian Return
Some sellers file Form 8288 with the buyer but don't report the capital gain on their Canadian T1. This invites CRA scrutiny. Always report the full transaction, including the gain and the foreign tax credit for withholding paid.

### 4. Underestimating Your Basis
For the withholding certificate, you must calculate your adjusted basis (original cost + improvements − depreciation). Underestimating basis inflates your gain, resulting in unnecessary withholding. Keep all purchase documents, renovation receipts, and depreciation records.

### 5. Missing the 20-Day Filing Deadline
If the buyer doesn't file Form 8288 within 20 days of closing, the IRS can assess penalties. As the seller, ensure your closing agent confirms filing before releasing final documents.
