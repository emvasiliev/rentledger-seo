# Form 8288: FIRPTA Withholding for Canadian Landlords Selling Illinois Rental Property

## What is Form 8288?

Form 8288 is the **US Withholding Tax Return for Dispositions by Foreign Persons of US Real Property Interests**. It's the mechanism through which buyers of US real property must withhold and remit taxes when purchasing from foreign sellers—including Canadian residents.

Under the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA), when a Canadian sells US real estate, the buyer is legally obligated to withhold **15% of the gross sale price** and remit this amount to the US Internal Revenue Service (IRS). Form 8288 is the official return used to document and report this withholding.

For a Canadian landlord disposing of rental property in Illinois, understanding Form 8288 is critical because:

1. The withholding directly affects your net proceeds from the sale
2. You may be able to reduce the withholding rate through an IRS withholding certificate
3. The withheld amount becomes a credit against your US tax liability
4. Improper handling creates compliance issues in both the US and Canada

## How FIRPTA Withholding Applies in Illinois

Illinois presents specific circumstances that make Form 8288 particularly relevant for Canadian landlords.

### The Illinois Context

Illinois imposes a flat **4.95% state income tax** on rental income. As a non-resident foreign person, you must file an Illinois state return (Form IL-1040-NR or IL-1040) to report rental income and claim any credits or deductions. However, the 15% federal withholding under FIRPTA is separate from and in addition to Illinois state taxes.

When you sell Illinois rental property:

- **Federal withholding**: 15% of gross sale price (FIRPTA requirement)
- **Illinois state withholding**: Generally not required on the disposition itself, but you remain liable for Illinois income tax on gains if you're considered an Illinois resident for tax purposes
- **Property taxes**: Illinois's effective property tax rate averages **2.27%** annually, but this is paid by the seller before closing (or negotiated between buyer and seller)

### The Withholding Obligation Chain

1. **Buyer** receives Form 8288 instructions from their title company or legal counsel
2. **Buyer** withholds 15% from the purchase price at closing
3. **Buyer** (or their agent) files Form 8288 with the IRS within 20 days of the transfer date
4. **You (the Canadian seller)** receive a Form 8288-B (Statement of Withholding Credit) as proof of withholding

The IRS matches this return against your US tax return to credit the withheld amount.

## Who Files Form 8288?

### Primary Filer: The Buyer

In most Illinois property transactions, **the buyer of the property files Form 8288**. However, the buyer typically delegates this responsibility to:

- Real estate attorneys
- Title insurance companies
- Escrow agents
- Real estate agents

### Secondary Filing: The Seller (You)

As the Canadian seller, you may also file Form 8288 or Form 8288-B if:

1. You apply for an **IRS Withholding Certificate** (Form 8288-B) to reduce withholding below 15%
2. You need to document or dispute the withholding amount
3. The buyer failed to file Form 8288 properly

Requesting a withholding certificate is strategically important if you expect your actual US tax liability to be less than 15% of the gross sale price—which is common when there are significant selling expenses, mortgage payoff, or other adjustments.

## How to Complete Form 8288: Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Gather Information

Before beginning, collect:

- Sale date and property address (Illinois address)
- Purchase price (full gross amount, not adjusted)
- Canadian seller name, address, and US Tax Identification Number (TIN) or Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN)
- Buyer name and address
- Form of ownership (individual, corporation, partnership)
- Certificate of non-foreign status (if applicable—typically filed by buyer's agent)

### Step 2: Complete Part I – Transferor Information

- **Line 1a–1d**: Enter your full legal name, address (Canadian mailing address is acceptable), and your US TIN or ITIN
- **Line 2**: Enter your country of citizenship (Canada)
- **Line 3**: Note whether you have a permanent residence visa (generally no, as a Canadian resident)

### Step 3: Complete Part II – Transferee Information

- **Lines 4–7**: Enter the buyer's name, address, and TIN
- **Line 8**: Identify the buyer's status (individual, corporation, etc.)

### Step 4: Complete Part III – Property Information

- **Line 9**: Enter the Illinois property address in full detail
- **Line 10**: Enter the legal description if available (this appears on your deed)
- **Line 11**: Specify property type (residential rental property)
- **Line 12**: Enter the date of transfer (the closing/settlement date)

### Step 5: Complete Part IV – Computation of Withholding

- **Line 13**: Enter the full gross amount realized from the sale (sales price, not net)
- **Line 14**: Multiply by 15% to calculate the standard withholding
- **Line 15**: Enter any reduced withholding rate (if you've obtained an IRS withholding certificate)
- **Line 16**: Calculate withholding due based on the applicable rate
- **Line 17**: Enter amount already withheld
- **Line 18**: Indicate if additional withholding or refund is due

### Step 6: Sign and File

Form 8288 must be signed and filed with:

**IRS Philadelphia Service Center**  
FIRPTA Unit  
2970 Market Street  
Philadelphia, PA 19104-3306

File electronically via EFTPS (Electronic Federal Tax Payment System) if withholding exceeds $2,500, or by paper check if under that threshold.

## Illinois-Specific Considerations

### Non-Resident Income Tax Filing

After the property sale, you must file an **Illinois non-resident return** (Form IL-1040-NR) to report the gain. Illinois taxes the entire net gain at 4.95%. This is filed separately from your US federal return, typically by the same April 15 deadline.

**Important**: The federal withholding on Form 8288 does NOT satisfy Illinois state tax obligations. You must still file and potentially pay additional Illinois tax.

### Coordination with US Federal Return

On your **US Form 1040**, you'll report:

- The full gain on Schedule D (Capital Gains and Losses)
- The sale under Section 1031 exchange rules (if applicable)
- The 15% federal withholding as a credit on Form 1040, line 24

### Reduced Withholding Certificate Strategy

If your actual tax liability (federal + state combined) is significantly less than 15%, consider requesting a **reduced withholding certificate** by filing Form 8288-B with the IRS **before closing**. The IRS will issue a certificate allowing the buyer to withhold a lower percentage (or potentially 0% in limited cases).

For Illinois sellers, this is particularly relevant if:

- The property has significant depreciation recapture
- You have substantial selling expenses
- Your income places you in a lower federal bracket
- You qualify for capital gains exclusions

### Canadian Tax Credit Implications

As a Canadian resident, the 15% federal withholding can be claimed as a **foreign tax credit** on your Canadian T1 return. Report the withholding on **Form T2209 (Federal Foreign Tax Credits)** in the year of the sale.

However, the US-Canada Tax Treaty may provide relief. Under Article XXII (Consultation) and the Mutual Agreement Procedure, you may request the IRS and Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) coordinate to avoid double taxation.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Forgetting Illinois State Tax**: Many Canadian sellers assume the 15% federal withholding covers all US taxes. It doesn't—Illinois state income tax remains your separate obligation.

2. **Not Requesting a Withholding Certificate**: If you expect a refund after filing your US return, applying for reduced withholding before closing recovers cash at closing rather than waiting for a refund.

3. **Incorrect Form of Ownership**: If you hold the property in a corporation or partnership, different rules apply. Form 8288 instructions vary significantly for entity-owned properties.

4. **Missing the 20-Day Filing Deadline**: The buyer must file Form 8288 within 20 days of transfer. Delays can trigger IRS penalties. Verify with your real estate attorney that the buyer understands this requirement.
