# Form W-7 Guide for Canadian Landlords with North Carolina Rental Property

## What is Form W-7?

Form W-7 (Application for IRS Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) is an IRS application that assigns a unique nine-digit ITIN to non-US citizens and non-resident aliens who must file US tax returns or comply with US tax withholding requirements. An ITIN functions similarly to a Social Security Number (SSN) for tax identification purposes but does not authorize work in the United States.

For Canadian landlords, the ITIN serves as your US tax identifier when filing Form 1040-NR (US Nonresident Alien Income Tax Return) and for FIRPTA (Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act) compliance when selling US rental property.

## How Form W-7 Applies to North Carolina Landlords

As a Canadian landlord owning rental property in North Carolina, you are classified as a non-resident alien for US federal tax purposes. North Carolina, like most US states, requires non-residents earning income from state sources to file a state income tax return.

**Federal Requirements:**
- You must file Form 1040-NR to report rental income, expenses, and claim deductions
- You need an ITIN to file this return and receive proper tax credits
- Your ITIN must be provided on all US tax filings and FIRPTA documentation

**North Carolina State Requirements:**
- North Carolina imposes a flat 4.5% state income tax on net rental income earned within the state
- Non-residents must file Form NC-1040 (North Carolina Individual Income Tax Return) to report this rental income
- Your ITIN can be used on the NC-1040, or you may be required to apply separately for a North Carolina taxpayer identification number, though the federal ITIN typically satisfies state requirements

**Property Tax Context:**
- North Carolina's effective property tax rate averages 0.8% of property value, assessed locally
- This is separate from income tax but relevant to your total tax burden on the property

**FIRPTA Implications:**
- When you eventually sell the North Carolina property, the buyer or their agent must withhold 15% of the net sale price (or 21% of gross sale price under FIRPTA rules)
- You must provide your ITIN to claim an FIRPTA withholding certificate (Form 8288-B) to reduce or eliminate this withholding
- Without an ITIN, the full withholding applies, creating cash flow complications

## Who Must File Form W-7

You must file Form W-7 if you are:
- A Canadian citizen or permanent resident without a US Social Security Number
- Earning income from US sources (rental property in North Carolina qualifies)
- Required to file a US federal tax return (Form 1040-NR for non-residents)
- Subject to FIRPTA withholding on property sales

Spouses who co-own the property must each obtain individual ITINs if both are Canadian residents without SSNs.

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form W-7

### Step 1: Gather Required Documentation

Before completing Form W-7, prepare:
- Valid passport (Canadian passport is acceptable)
- Proof of North Carolina rental income (lease agreements, property management statements, bank deposits)
- Your Canadian Social Insurance Number (SIN)
- A US mailing address (use your property address, property manager's address, or US tax representative's address)

### Step 2: Complete Form W-7

**Part I – Personal Identification:**
- Enter your full legal name exactly as it appears on your Canadian passport
- Provide your date of birth
- List your country of citizenship (Canada)
- Enter your Canadian SIN in the "Foreign tax ID number" field (if applicable)

**Part II – Type of ITIN Applicant:**
- Check the box for "Nonresident alien individual" or "Nonresident alien engaged in US trade or business"
- Since you earn rental income, you are engaged in US trade or business, making the rental property business line the appropriate selection

**Part III – Reason for Applying:**
- Select "Filing US tax return" (Form 1040-NR for rental income)
- If applicable, also note "FIRPTA withholding certificate" if you anticipate a property sale

**Part IV – US Residency Status:**
- Confirm your status as a non-resident alien
- Provide your Canadian address

**Part V – Declaration:**
- Sign and date the form
- Consider having a US tax representative (CPA or tax attorney) sign as authorized representative if you have one

### Step 3: Certify Your Passport

You must submit certified copies of your identification. Options include:
- Submitting original documents and having the IRS return them
- Having documents certified by a US consulate, embassy, or notary public
- Using an IRS-approved certification process through a tax professional

For Canadian applicants, the most practical approach is having your Canadian passport notarized by a US notary or certified by a US consulate.

### Step 4: File the Application

**By Mail:**
- Send Form W-7 and supporting documents to the IRS address specified for your state (typically the Philadelphia IRS Service Center for North Carolina)
- Use certified mail with return receipt requested
- Processing typically takes 4–6 weeks

**With Your Tax Return:**
- Attach Form W-7 to your Form 1040-NR when filing initially
- This accelerates ITIN assignment and ensures coordination with your return
- Recommended approach for Canadian landlords filing their first return

### Step 5: Receive Your ITIN

The IRS will mail your ITIN assignment notice (IRS Notice 1) to your US address. This notice serves as proof of your ITIN and should be retained for all future filings.

## North Carolina-Specific Considerations

### State Tax Return Filing

Once you receive your ITIN, you must file Form NC-1040 (North Carolina Individual Income Tax Return) reporting:
- Gross rental income from the North Carolina property
- Allowable deductions (property taxes, mortgage interest, repairs, utilities, depreciation, property management fees)
- Net income subject to the 4.5% state tax rate

**Example Calculation:**
If your North Carolina rental generates $30,000 gross income with $15,000 in deductible expenses, your taxable income is $15,000. North Carolina state tax would be $675 (15,000 × 4.5%).

### Federal-State Coordination

North Carolina allows a foreign tax credit under certain circumstances:
- US federal taxes paid may reduce your North Carolina liability if properly documented
- However, since North Carolina imposes only state income tax (not a separate withholding tax on non-residents), the credit is typically limited
- File Schedule S-1 (Foreign Income Exclusion and Credit) with your NC-1040 if claiming this credit

### Canada-US Tax Treaty Benefit

The Canada-US Income and Gains Tax Treaty (Article XIV) may reduce US taxation on your rental income in specific circumstances:
- Independent personal services and business profits may qualify for reduced rates
- You can claim a "tie-breaker" exemption if you maintain permanent residence in Canada
- File Form 8833 (Treaty-Based Return Position Disclosure) with your Form 1040-NR if claiming treaty benefits

You will report US rental income on your Canadian T1 return (Line 12100 for rental income). Canadian tax on this income is offset by US Foreign Tax Credits (FTC) claimed on your Form 1040-NR, Schedule 3 (Form 1116). This prevents double taxation.

### Property Tax Considerations

North Carolina property taxes (averaging 0.8% of assessed value) are deductible on both your US Form 1040-NR (Schedule E) and your Canadian T1 return (under investment expenses), reducing your net taxable income in both jurisdictions.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Delaying ITIN Application**: Applying for your ITIN only when filing your first return slows processing. File Form W-7 immediately upon acquiring the property or establishing rental income.

2. **Misidentifying Your Tax Status**: Incorrectly selecting "resident alien" instead of "nonresident alien" on Form W-7 results in rejection and delays.

3. **Submitting Uncertified Passport Copies**: The IRS requires certified copies. Photocopies alone are insufficient.

4. **Forgetting to Update Address**: If you move before receiving your ITIN notice, inform the IRS immediately to avoid mail delivery delays.

5. **Not Filing State Returns**: Many Canadian landlords file the federal 1040-NR but neglect North Carolina's NC-1040, resulting in state penalties and interest.

6. **Confusing ITIN with FIRPTA Withholding Certificate**: An ITIN is your identification number. A FIRPTA withholding certificate (Form 8288-B) is a separate document filed at property
