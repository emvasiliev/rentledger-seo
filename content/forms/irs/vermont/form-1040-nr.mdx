# Form 1040-NR for Canadian Landlords: Complete Vermont Rental Property Guide

## What Is Form 1040-NR?

Form 1040-NR (U.S. Income Tax Return for Nonresident Alien Individuals) is the primary federal income tax return filed by non-resident aliens—including Canadian citizens—who have U.S.-source income. For Canadian landlords owning rental property in Vermont, this form is essential when electing under **Section 871(d)** of the Internal Revenue Code to treat rental income as effectively connected income (ECI). This election allows you to deduct rental expenses (mortgage interest, property tax, repairs, management fees, depreciation) against your rental income, rather than having the gross rental income taxed at a flat 30% withholding rate under Section 871(c).

## How Section 871(d) Changes Your Tax Picture in Vermont

Without a Section 871(d) election, the IRS would impose a flat 30% withholding tax on your Vermont rental income with minimal deduction opportunities. By making the Section 871(d) election on Form 1040-NR, you:

- **Report net rental income** after legitimate business deductions
- **Potentially owe less tax** if expenses reduce your net income to break-even or loss
- **Qualify for deductions** unavailable to non-electing non-resident aliens, including depreciation
- **Must file Form 1040-NR annually** for all years the election is in effect

The election is made by attaching a statement to Form 1040-NR for the first year and remains in effect for subsequent tax years unless revoked.

## Vermont-Specific Tax Considerations

### State Income Tax Obligation

Vermont imposes a **8.75% state income tax** on net rental income earned within the state. As a non-resident owner, you must file **Vermont Form BI-471** (Vermont Nonresident Income Tax Return) in addition to your federal 1040-NR. Vermont taxes rental income on a net basis (gross income less ordinary and necessary business expenses), which aligns favorably with federal reporting.

### Property Tax Burden

Vermont's average effective property tax rate is **1.9% of property value**, higher than many U.S. states. This is a significant deductible expense. For example, on a $500,000 rental property, annual property taxes could exceed $9,500—a substantial deduction on your 1040-NR Schedule E.

### Why Vermont Attracts Quebec Landlords

Vermont's proximity to Quebec and cultural ties make it particularly popular with Montreal-area investors. The short distance reduces management complexity, and the Canada-US Tax Treaty provides relief from double taxation on the same income.

## Who Must File Form 1040-NR: Eligibility

You must file Form 1040-NR if you are:

1. **A non-resident alien** (not a U.S. citizen, green card holder, or substantial presence test filer)
2. **With U.S.-source income** subject to tax, including rental income from Vermont real estate
3. **Making the Section 871(d) election** to treat rental income as effectively connected income

Canadian citizens are considered non-resident aliens for U.S. tax purposes unless they hold a valid green card or meet the substantial presence test (physical presence in the U.S. for 183+ days in the current year or averaged over three years).

### When You Don't Need Form 1040-NR

If you do not elect under Section 871(d), the IRS will apply withholding at the source (typically via Form 1042-S reporting to you). You would not file Form 1040-NR but would still report the income on your Canadian T1 personal tax return.

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form 1040-NR

### Step 1: Determine Your Tax Residency Status

Confirm your non-resident alien status. Complete the **Nonresident Alien Worksheet** (attached to 1040-NR instructions) to verify you don't meet the substantial presence test. Canadian citizens physically residing in Canada typically qualify as non-resident aliens.

### Step 2: Gather Documentation

Collect for the 2023 tax year (example):

- **Rental property details**: Address, acquisition date, depreciable basis
- **Income records**: Rent collected, other income
- **Expense receipts**: Property tax statements, mortgage interest statements (Form 1098), repairs, insurance, property management fees, utilities (if you pay), HOA fees
- **Depreciation schedule**: Calculate or retrieve from prior year
- **Canadian tax information**: Your Canadian address and social insurance number (SIN)

### Step 3: Complete Schedule E (Supplemental Income and Loss)

Schedule E is the core of your 1040-NR for rental reporting:

- **Part I, Line 1**: Enter the Vermont property address
- **Lines 3–20**: Report gross rental income and all eligible deductions
- **Deductible items** include:
  - Advertising (vacancy listings)
  - Auto/travel (property management trips—capped by Canadian tax law reciprocally)
  - Cleaning/maintenance
  - Commissions (property manager fees)
  - Insurance
  - Interest (mortgage and loan interest)
  - Legal/professional fees (tax prep, legal advice)
  - Management fees (often 8–10% of gross rent in Vermont)
  - Mortgage principal repayment **(not deductible)**
  - Property tax (Vermont taxes)
  - Repairs (not improvements)
  - Utilities (if you cover them)
  - Depreciation (27.5-year residential property)

### Step 4: Calculate Depreciation

Determine your depreciable basis (property cost minus land value, typically 20–30% land in Vermont). Divide the building value by 27.5 years. Report this on Form 4562 (Depreciation and Amortization), then transfer to Schedule E.

**Example**: $400,000 building ÷ 27.5 years = **$14,545 annual depreciation** (subject to recapture on future sale).

### Step 5: Make the Section 871(d) Election

Attach a **statement to Form 1040-NR** (for the first year only) titled:

> "ELECTION UNDER SECTION 871(d) AND SECTION 882(d): I hereby elect to treat all U.S. real property rental income for the tax year as effectively connected income under Section 871(d) of the Internal Revenue Code."

Include the property address and a brief description. Once filed, this election remains active unless formally revoked on IRS Form 8288-B.

### Step 6: File the Return

Form 1040-NR is filed with the IRS (not state-specific):

- **Address**: Use the current IRS filing address in the form instructions (typically Ogden, UT or Philadelphia, PA depending on state)
- **If filing electronically**: Use an IRS-authorized e-filer (highly recommended for accuracy)
- **If filing by mail**: Include all schedules, elections, and supporting documentation

### Step 7: File Vermont Form BI-471

Simultaneously file Vermont's nonresident state income tax return:

- Vermont requires net rental income calculation
- Vermont allows the same deductions as federal, reducing your Vermont tax base
- Vermont Form BI-471 typically due **April 15** (same federal deadline)
- File with: *Vermont Department of Taxes, P.O. Box 547, Montpelier, VT 05601-0547*

## Vermont-Specific Considerations

### Coordination with Canadian Tax Return

You must report the same Vermont rental income on your **Canadian Form T1 General (Personal Income Tax Return)** as a foreign source income:

- Include the Vermont property in **Schedule 1, Lines 10400–10700** (rental income and expenses)
- List the property in **Schedule 11 (Rental Income)**
- Report U.S. income in **Canadian dollars** at the applicable year-end exchange rate
- **Claim a foreign tax credit** on Form 1116 (U.S.) and Schedule 1 (Canada) for Vermont state income taxes paid to avoid double taxation

### Quebec Landlord Advantage

If you reside in Quebec, you have an additional filing requirement: **Revenu Québec Form TP-80-V** (Declaration of Foreign Source Income). However, the Canada-Quebec tax treaty provisions generally prevent double taxation.

### Withholding and Estimated Tax

Once you file Form 1040-NR with the Section 871(d) election, you may be required to pay **estimated tax** quarterly (Form 1040-ES-NR) if you expect to owe more than $1,000. Vermont also requires estimated payments if your net rent exceeds certain thresholds—check current requirements with Vermont Department of Taxes.
