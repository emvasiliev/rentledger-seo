# Form W-7: ITIN Guide for Canadian Landlords with Alabama Rental Property

## What Is Form W-7?

Form W-7, Application for IRS Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN), is the official IRS form used by non-US citizens and non-resident aliens to request an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number. An ITIN is a nine-digit identification number formatted like a Social Security Number (SSN) but designated exclusively for individuals who are not eligible for an SSN but must file US tax returns or comply with US tax obligations.

For Canadian landlords, obtaining an ITIN is not optional—it is a fundamental requirement for:

- Filing Form 1040-NR (US Nonresident Alien Income Tax Return)
- Complying with FIRPTA (Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act) withholding rules
- Reporting US-source rental income to the IRS
- Establishing proper documentation for Alabama state tax compliance

The ITIN serves as your "US tax identity" and remains valid as long as you file a federal return at least once every three consecutive calendar years.

## How Form W-7 Applies to Canadian Landlords in Alabama

### Federal Requirements

As a Canadian resident owning rental property in Alabama, you are classified as a "non-resident alien" for US federal tax purposes. This status triggers three critical obligations:

1. **Filing requirement**: You must file Form 1040-NR annually to report US-source income, including Alabama rental income
2. **FIRPTA compliance**: If you sell the property, the buyer's closing agent must withhold 15% of the sale price under FIRPTA rules (unless a FIRPTA withholding certificate exempts you)
3. **Tax identification**: You cannot file either form without a US taxpayer identification number—your ITIN

The Canada-US Tax Treaty (Article XXIII) provides limited relief for certain Canadian residents, but this relief does not eliminate the filing requirement or the need for an ITIN. In fact, claiming treaty benefits on Form 1040-NR typically requires an ITIN to substantiate your Canadian residency status.

### Alabama State Requirements

Alabama imposes a **5% state income tax on rental income** earned within the state. As a non-resident, you must file an Alabama Individual Income Tax Return (Form INC 40) even though you do not reside in Alabama. This state return is due by the same federal deadline (typically April 15).

Alabama does not grant an automatic exemption to non-residents, though the Canada-US Tax Treaty may provide some relief. Critically, Alabama's Department of Revenue uses your ITIN (or your permanent resident card number) to track compliance. Without an ITIN, you cannot properly file Alabama returns, and the state may assess penalties and interest.

Additionally, Alabama property taxes (averaging 0.41% of assessed value) are not claimed as itemized deductions on the federal 1040-NR for non-residents, but they may reduce your net rental income calculation on Alabama Form INC 40.

## Who Files Form W-7?

You must file Form W-7 if:

- You are a Canadian citizen or permanent resident without a US Social Security Number
- You own taxable US real property (such as your Alabama rental)
- You are required to file a US tax return or have a filing requirement triggered by FIRPTA
- You intend to claim treaty benefits on Form 1040-NR

You should **not** file Form W-7 if:

- You have already obtained an ITIN
- You are eligible for and have been issued an SSN (rare for Canadian residents)
- You have no US tax filing obligation and no FIRPTA event

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form W-7

### Step 1: Gather Required Documentation

Before submitting Form W-7, assemble:

- A completed Form W-7 (download from IRS.gov)
- Your original or certified copy of your Canadian passport or birth certificate
- Proof of Canadian tax residency (a recent Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) Notice of Assessment or residency letter)
- A completed Form 1040-NR (even if you have not yet filed; this is typically submitted alongside W-7)
- Form 8288-B (FIRPTA Statement of Withholding) if you are selling the property
- Photocopies of the above (originals may be requested by the IRS)

### Step 2: Complete Form W-7 Accurately

**Part I: Personal Information**

Enter your full legal name as it appears on your Canadian passport. Use your Canadian mailing address (NOT your Alabama property address). The IRS will communicate with you in Canada.

**Part II: Type of Applicant**

Select box **c** (Individual with US rental real property income). This is the correct category for landlords.

**Part III: Country of Citizenship and Residence**

Enter "Canada" in both fields.

**Part IV: Mailing Address**

Provide your Canadian home address where the IRS should send your ITIN documentation.

### Step 3: Submit Form W-7 with Required Attachments

**Option A: Submit with Your 1040-NR**

The most straightforward approach is to file Form W-7 together with Form 1040-NR for the tax year in which you first have a filing obligation. Include all supporting documents. Mail to:

**Internal Revenue Service**  
Philadelphia IRS Campus  
Attn: ITIN Unit  
2970 Market Street  
Philadelphia, PA 19104  
USA

File this package by the April 15 deadline (or October 15 if you obtain an automatic extension via Form 4868).

**Option B: Apply by IRS Video Ident**

If you are a Canadian resident, you may apply for an ITIN through the IRS Video Ident process, which eliminates the need to certify document copies. Schedule an appointment through an IRS partner institution (certain international tax professionals and banks offer this service). This process typically takes 4–6 weeks.

### Step 4: Await ITIN Issuance

The IRS typically issues ITINs within:

- **6–8 weeks** if submitting by mail with proper documentation
- **2–4 weeks** if using the Video Ident process

You will receive a notice (IRS Letter 4CH or Letter 1198-C) with your 9-digit ITIN. **Do not file your 1040-NR until you have received your ITIN.**

## Alabama-Specific Considerations

### Coordination with Alabama Returns

Once you obtain your ITIN, you must include it on every Alabama Form INC 40 return you file. Alabama's tax software systems cross-reference ITINs with federal filings, so consistency is critical.

Alabama does not recognize certain Canada-US Treaty provisions the way the federal IRS does. If you claim "non-resident student" status or other treaty relief on your federal return, you may still owe full Alabama income tax. File Alabama Form INC 40 conservatively, claiming only treaty provisions that Alabama specifically honors (typically limited to the physical presence test under Article XV of the treaty).

### Alabama Property Tax Records

The Alabama Department of Revenue and county property assessors maintain property records under your ITIN. Using a consistent ITIN across all filings prevents duplicate assessments and audit triggers.

### FIRPTA and Alabama Implications

If you sell your Alabama rental property, the buyer's title company must withhold 15% of the sale price under FIRPTA rules. Your ITIN is required to file Form 8288 (US Withholding Tax Return for Disposition of US Real Property Interests) with the IRS. However, Alabama **does not** require a separate state FIRPTA withholding. The federal FIRPTA withholding satisfies both obligations, though you must still report the sale on Alabama Form INC 40.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Using Your Canadian Social Insurance Number (SIN)

Never use your SIN as a substitute for an ITIN on US forms. The IRS will reject your filing. The IRS specifically requires an ITIN for foreign nationals.

### Mistake 2: Submitting Uncertified Document Copies

The IRS requires either:

- Original documents, or
- Photocopies certified by a notary public (US or Canadian notary both acceptable)

Simply photocopying your passport and mailing it without certification will delay processing or result in rejection.

### Mistake 3: Filing Form 1040-NR Before Receiving Your ITIN

If you file a 1040-NR without an ITIN, the IRS will reject it and delay your filing. Always wait to receive your ITIN letter before submitting Form 1040-NR.

### Mistake 4: Forgetting to Claim Canadian Foreign Tax Credits
