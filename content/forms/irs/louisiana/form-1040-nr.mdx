# Form 1040-NR for Canadian Landlords: Louisiana Rental Property Tax Guide

## What Is Form 1040-NR?

Form 1040-NR (U.S. Income Tax Return for Nonresident Aliens) is the primary federal income tax return filed by non-resident aliens, including Canadian citizens, who have U.S.-source income subject to taxation. For Canadian landlords with rental properties in Louisiana, this form becomes essential when you elect to treat your rental income as "effectively connected income" (ECI) under **Section 871(d) of the Internal Revenue Code**.

Without this election, rental income is typically subject to a flat 30% withholding tax with no ability to deduct operating expenses—a far more punitive treatment. By filing Form 1040-NR and making the Section 871(d) election, you report rental income net of deductible expenses and pay tax at graduated federal rates, often resulting in significant tax savings.

The Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty (Article XIII) generally permits Canada and the United States to tax real property income. However, the treaty also provides relief mechanisms and foreign tax credit provisions that interact with your U.S. filing obligations.

## How Form 1040-NR Applies to Louisiana Landlords

### Federal Taxation of Rental Income

When you own rental property in Louisiana, the **net rental income** (after eligible deductions) is considered U.S.-source income and is subject to federal tax. Form 1040-NR allows you to report this income and claim deductions such as:

- Mortgage interest
- Property taxes
- Insurance
- Utilities and maintenance
- Depreciation
- Property management fees
- Repairs (but not capital improvements)

The tax is calculated at graduated federal rates. For 2024, non-resident alien rates follow the same brackets as single filers, ranging from 10% to 37% at the top marginal rate.

### Louisiana State Income Tax

Louisiana imposes a **4.25% state income tax** on rental income earned within the state. This is a direct state-level obligation separate from federal tax. Non-resident landlords must file Louisiana Form IT-540B (Individual Income Tax Return for Nonresidents) or the equivalent long form to report Louisiana-source income.

Louisiana does not recognize the Section 871(d) election at the state level in the same manner as the federal government, meaning you cannot reduce Louisiana taxable income dollar-for-dollar by expenses in all cases. However, Louisiana law generally permits deduction of ordinary and necessary business expenses for rental activity, similar to federal rules.

### Property Tax Considerations

Louisiana's property tax system is administered by parish assessors. The average effective property tax rate statewide is **0.56%**, though rates vary by parish. For example, Orleans Parish may have different assessments than rural parishes. Property taxes paid are fully deductible against both federal and Louisiana rental income.

## Who Must File Form 1040-NR?

You must file Form 1040-NR if:

1. You are a **non-resident alien** for U.S. tax purposes (a Canadian citizen without U.S. permanent residency or green card status typically qualifies)
2. You have **U.S.-source income** that is not exempt under treaty or statute
3. You make the **Section 871(d) election** to treat rental income as effectively connected income, OR your rental income exceeds the threshold requiring return filing

Canadian permanent residents of Louisiana or those holding U.S. green cards file Form 1040 (the standard U.S. return for residents and citizens) instead.

**Treaty Residency:** Under the Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty, your tax residency is determined by a "tie-breaker" test. If you are considered a resident of Canada under Canadian tax law and a non-resident of the U.S., you generally file Form 1040-NR (unless you hold a green card).

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form 1040-NR

### Step 1: Gather Documentation

Collect the following before beginning:

- Rental property address and legal description
- Gross rental income received during the tax year
- Records of all deductible expenses (invoices, receipts, bank statements)
- Mortgage statement(s) showing interest paid
- Property tax assessment and payment records
- Insurance policy documentation
- Depreciation schedules (if claiming depreciation)
- Louisiana state income tax information
- Social Security Number or ITIN

### Step 2: Determine Your Filing Status

Non-resident aliens typically file as **Single**. You cannot use Married Filing Jointly or Head of Household unless specific conditions are met (consult a tax professional).

### Step 3: Complete Personal Information

- Line 1: Enter your name, address, and ITIN (Individual Taxpayer Identification Number). Canadian citizens without a U.S. SSN must have an ITIN assigned by the IRS before filing.
- Line 2: Enter your country of citizenship (Canada).

### Step 4: Report Rental Income and Expenses

**Schedule E (Supplemental Income or Loss)** is attached to Form 1040-NR to report rental property details:

- **Part I:** Identify the Louisiana property address
- **Gross rental income:** Enter all rent received
- **Expenses:** List property tax, mortgage interest, insurance, utilities, repairs, depreciation, and other allowable deductions
- **Net income or loss:** Calculate the bottom-line result

Depreciation is calculated using **Form 4562**. The building itself (not land) is depreciated over 27.5 years for residential property.

### Step 5: Make the Section 871(d) Election

The Section 871(d) election must be made **explicitly on Form 1040-NR**. Typically, this is done by:

- Filing Form 1040-NR rather than allowing withholding
- Attaching a statement explicitly electing Section 871(d) treatment, or
- Including the election language in the filing

Consult your tax advisor to ensure proper documentation, as improper election can result in the default 30% withholding treatment.

### Step 6: Calculate Federal Tax

Transfer net rental income to the appropriate line on Form 1040-NR. Federal tax is calculated on this income using 2024 single filer rates. The filing may include other U.S.-source income (if applicable).

### Step 7: Account for Louisiana State Tax

Calculate Louisiana income tax separately. File **Louisiana Form IT-540B** (Nonresident Return) and report Louisiana-source income. Louisiana state tax is typically **4.25%** of Louisiana taxable income.

### Step 8: Foreign Tax Credits

On **Form 1116** (Foreign Tax Credit), you may claim a credit for Louisiana state income tax paid. This reduces your federal liability dollar-for-dollar (subject to limitations). This foreign tax credit mechanism is crucial for managing double taxation.

## Louisiana-Specific Considerations

### Parish-Level Variations

Property tax rates and assessments vary significantly by parish. New Orleans (Orleans Parish) has different assessment practices than rural parishes. Always obtain the specific property tax bill from the assessor's office to ensure accurate reporting.

### No State-Level Section 871(d) Equivalent

Louisiana does not have a direct state-level equivalent to the federal Section 871(d) election. However, Louisiana does allow deduction of business expenses against rental income for non-resident taxpayers. Ensure your Louisiana return (IT-540B) includes all deductible expenses to minimize Louisiana taxable income.

### Withholding on Rental Payments

If your Louisiana property management company or tenants are required to withhold federal taxes, ensure those withholdings are properly reported and credited on your 1040-NR. Coordinate with your property manager to ensure withholding compliance.

### Canada-U.S. Treaty Benefits

Article XIII of the Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty permits the U.S. to tax real property income. However, Article XXIII provides a foreign tax credit mechanism. Louisiana state tax paid can offset federal tax via Form 1116, and the reverse applies in Canada (U.S. federal tax can offset Canadian provincial tax). File your Canadian T1 return concurrently and claim the foreign tax credit for U.S. taxes paid.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Failing to File Form 1040-NR Altogether:** Some Canadian landlords assume they can use withholding-only compliance. Filing 1040-NR with the Section 871(d) election is often more tax-efficient.

2. **Not Electing Section 871(d) Explicitly:** Merely filing Form 1040-NR does not constitute the election. Be explicit in your filing or risk the default 30% withholding rate.

3. **Forgetting Louisiana State Return:** Completing only the federal 1040-NR while ignoring Louisiana Form IT-540B results in Louisiana penalty and interest. File both.

4. **Miscalculating Depreciation:** Depreciation must be calculated on the building value
