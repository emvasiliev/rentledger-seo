# Form 8288: FIRPTA Withholding for Canadian Landlords Selling Kansas Rental Property

## What is Form 8288?

Form 8288 is the **US Withholding Tax Return for Dispositions by Foreign Persons of US Real Property Interests**. It's the mechanism through which the IRS enforces the **Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA)**, enacted to ensure that foreign sellers of US real estate pay their share of US income tax.

When you, as a Canadian resident, sell rental property located in Kansas, the buyer's closing agent or legal representative is **required by federal law** to withhold 15% of the gross sale price and remit it to the IRS using Form 8288. This withholding is separate from any state-level withholding and is calculated on the full purchase price—not just the gain.

## How FIRPTA Withholding Works in Kansas

### The 15% Withholding Rule

Under **IRC Section 1445**, when a foreign person disposes of a US real property interest, the transferee (buyer) must withhold 15% of the "amount realized" on the sale. For practical purposes, this is 15% of the gross sale price.

**Example:** You sell a rental property in Topeka, Kansas for $300,000. The buyer's closing agent must withhold $45,000 (15% × $300,000) and remit this to the IRS via Form 8288 within 20 days of closing.

### Kansas Context

Kansas does not impose a separate state-level FIRPTA withholding. However, as a non-resident seller, you'll also be subject to:

- **Kansas state income tax** of 5.7% on rental income you earned during your ownership period (filed via **Kansas Form K-40**, Schedule NR for non-residents)
- **Kansas property tax** during your holding period, at an average effective rate of 1.41%

Critically, the 15% federal FIRPTA withholding does **not** satisfy Kansas state tax obligations. You may owe additional Kansas income tax on the sale gain itself, depending on your total taxable income and available deductions.

## Who Files Form 8288?

### The Buyer's Responsibility

The **buyer** (transferee) is primarily responsible for filing Form 8288. Specifically:

- The buyer's real estate attorney, title company, or closing agent typically prepares and files the form
- The buyer must file Form 8288 with the IRS **within 20 days of the date of transfer**
- The buyer must also provide a copy to you (the seller) and to the Kansas Department of Revenue

In Kansas, the closing process typically involves a title company that coordinates this withholding. However, **you should verify with your real estate attorney or closing agent** that they are aware of your foreign status and will handle Form 8288 filing.

### When You (the Seller) File Form 8288

You may file Form 8288 in a different capacity if you're applying for a **withholding certificate** (Form 8288-B) to reduce or eliminate the 15% withholding. See the section below on reduced withholding applications.

## Applying for Reduced Withholding: Form 8288-B

### When Withholding May Be Reduced

You don't have to accept the automatic 15% withholding. You can apply to the IRS for a **withholding certificate (Form 8288-B)** if:

1. Your actual US tax liability on the sale is less than 15% of the sale price
2. You file the application **before closing**, allowing time for IRS review (typically 30–60 days, sometimes longer)
3. The IRS agrees that reduced withholding is appropriate

### Example of Reduced Withholding

Suppose you sell the Kansas property for $300,000 with an adjusted basis (cost) of $280,000. Your capital gain is $20,000. After accounting for depreciation recapture and other factors, your estimated tax liability might be $4,000 rather than the full $45,000 (15% withholding).

By filing Form 8288-B, you can request that the buyer withhold only $4,000 instead. This requires:

- Calculating your estimated US tax liability on the sale
- Including documentation of your cost basis and deductions
- Submitting the application to the IRS **before the closing date**

**Important:** The buyer is not obligated to accept reduced withholding unless you have an IRS-issued certificate. Never tell the buyer to withhold less without the certificate in place.

## How to Complete Form 8288 (as the Buyer or Your Representative)

Form 8288 consists of five main parts:

### Part I: Transferor Information
- Your name and Canadian address
- Your Canadian Social Insurance Number (SIN) — this functions as your ITIN for this purpose
- Your status as a foreign person

### Part II: Transferee Information
- Buyer's name and US address
- Buyer's federal Employer Identification Number (EIN) or Social Security Number (SSN)
- Date of transfer (closing date)

### Part III: Property Information
- Address of the Kansas property (full street address, city, county, and legal description if available)
- Description of property interest (e.g., "residential rental property")

### Part IV: Calculation of Withholding
- Gross sale price (amount realized)
- Multiply by 15% (or the reduced percentage if a Form 8288-B certificate was issued)
- Enter total amount to be withheld

### Part V: Certification
- Buyer or representative certifies accuracy
- Signs and dates the form

## Step-by-Step Process: Your Role as the Canadian Seller

1. **Notify your closing agent of foreign status**: Inform the buyer's attorney or title company **immediately upon entering into a purchase agreement** that you are a Canadian resident (foreign person for FIRPTA purposes).

2. **Decide on withholding certificate**: Determine whether to apply for Form 8288-B reduced withholding. Consult a US cross-border tax professional to calculate your likely tax liability.

3. **Request Form 8288-B if applicable**: Submit your application to the IRS at least 45 days before closing. Include:
   - Estimated calculation of gain and tax liability
   - Proof of your Canadian residency
   - Documentation of adjusted basis

4. **Provide IRS certificate to buyer**: If approved, furnish the Form 8288-B certificate to the closing agent so the buyer knows the correct withholding amount.

5. **Cooperate with closing**: Ensure the buyer completes Form 8288 accurately with your correct name, SIN, and property details.

6. **Receive IRS and Kansas copies**: The buyer should provide you with a copy of the filed Form 8288 after closing.

7. **Report on your US tax return**: Report the sale on **Form 8949** (Sales of Capital Assets) and **Schedule D** (Capital Gains and Losses) when you file your US Form 1040-NR.

## Kansas-Specific Considerations

### Non-Resident Income Tax Return Requirements

After selling Kansas property, you must file a **Kansas non-resident income tax return (Form K-40, Schedule NR)** if you realized a taxable gain. Kansas taxes **non-resident persons on income derived from sources within Kansas**, including:

- The capital gain on the property sale
- Any rental income earned during your holding period

Kansas's state income tax rate of **5.7%** applies to this income. The good news: Kansas does **not** impose an additional gains tax or sales tax on real estate transactions. However, any state income tax you owe to Kansas is **separate from** the 15% federal FIRPTA withholding.

### Federal-State Coordination

The 15% federal FIRPTA withholding may be more or less than your actual combined federal and Kansas tax liability:

- **Excess withholding**: You'll recover this as a refund when you file your US federal return (Form 1040-NR) and your Kansas non-resident return.
- **Shortfall**: If the federal + state tax exceeds 15%, you'll owe additional tax when you file.

### Canada-US Tax Treaty Implications

The **Canada-US Tax Treaty** (Article XIII) generally allows the US to tax gains on the disposition of real property. However, the treaty does provide relief in certain circumstances:

- If the property was a **principal residence** (owner-occupied), you may claim a reduced tax rate or exemption under Canadian rules, but the US still taxes non-residents.
- You may claim a **foreign tax credit** on your Canadian T1 return (line 40500, Schedule 1) for any US income tax paid on the sale, reducing your Canadian tax liability.
