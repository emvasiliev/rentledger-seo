# Form W-7: ITIN for Canadian Landlords with Delaware Rental Property

## What Is Form W-7 and Why You Need It

Form W-7 (Application for IRS Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) is the IRS application used to obtain an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN). An ITIN is a nine-digit tax identification number that the IRS issues to non-residents and foreign nationals who have US tax obligations but do not qualify for a Social Security Number.

As a Canadian landlord earning rental income from Delaware property, you are classified as a non-resident alien for US tax purposes. The IRS requires you to have either a Social Security Number or an ITIN to file Form 1040-NR (Non-resident Alien Income Tax Return) and to satisfy FIRPTA (Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act) withholding obligations when you eventually sell your property.

Without an ITIN, you cannot:
- File a US federal tax return for rental income
- Comply with FIRPTA withholding requirements on property disposition
- Claim tax treaty benefits under the Canada-US Tax Treaty
- Obtain a Delaware rental property tax identification number

## How the ITIN Requirement Applies to Delaware Landlords

Delaware presents a unique tax situation for Canadian rental property owners. While Delaware has no sales tax and minimal corporate taxes, it does impose a **6.6% state income tax on rental income** earned within the state. This applies equally to non-residents.

Here's the Delaware-specific filing framework:

**Federal Level**: You must file Form 1040-NR with the IRS by June 15 (for non-residents) using your ITIN.

**Delaware State Level**: You must file a Delaware Nonresident Income Tax Return (Form 1040A or equivalent) by April 15 of the following year, also using your ITIN. Delaware's 6.6% flat tax rate applies to net rental income less allowable deductions (mortgage interest, property tax, repairs, management fees, utilities, insurance, depreciation).

**Property Tax**: Delaware's effective property tax rate averages 0.57%, assessed at the county level. This is deductible against both federal and state taxable income.

**FIRPTA Considerations**: If you sell your Delaware property, the buyer's agent or title company must withhold 15% of the gross sale price and remit it to the IRS. Your ITIN is essential to claim this as a credit or obtain a refund.

## Who Must File Form W-7

You must file Form W-7 if you are:

- A Canadian citizen or permanent resident with no US Social Security Number
- Earning rental income from Delaware residential or commercial property
- Subject to US tax filing obligations for that rental income
- Required to satisfy FIRPTA withholding requirements
- Claiming treaty benefits under Article IV(g) of the Canada-US Tax Treaty (which may exempt certain types of income or allow reduced withholding)

You do **not** need to file W-7 if you already hold a valid Social Security Number or ITIN.

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form W-7

### Step 1: Gather Required Documents

Before submitting Form W-7, collect:

- **Proof of Canadian citizenship**: Valid Canadian passport or provincial ID (certified copy or original)
- **Proof of foreign status**: Your Certificate of Canadian Citizenship or Permanent Residency document
- **Tax return documentation**: A copy of your most recent Canadian T1 General (personal income tax return), which demonstrates your tax filing history
- **Lease agreements or property title**: Documentation showing your ownership interest in the Delaware property
- **IRS Form 8288-B**: If applicable, evidence that FIRPTA withholding was already collected on a property sale

### Step 2: Complete Form W-7 Accurately

**Part I – Personal Information**:
- Enter your full legal name as it appears on your Canadian passport (first name, middle initial, last name)
- Provide your date of birth in MM/DD/YYYY format
- Enter your Canadian mailing address or US address (if you have one)

**Part II – Country of Citizenship and Residency**:
- Enter Canada as country of citizenship
- Enter Canada as country of residency

**Part III – Type of Applicant**:
- Check box **"Other"** and write: "Non-resident alien deriving US-source rental income from Delaware real property"

**Part IV – Intended Use of ITIN**:
- Check **"Federal tax return filing"** (mandatory)
- Check **"FIRPTA"** (if you own property subject to potential sale)

**Part V – Declaration**:
- Sign and date the form under penalty of perjury

### Step 3: Submit with Supporting Documents

The IRS requires original or certified copies of identity documents. **Do not send originals** to the IRS if possible; instead:

- Mail Form W-7 with certified photocopies to the appropriate IRS ITIN Unit (in Philadelphia for Delaware applicants)
- Include a cover letter explaining that you are a Canadian landlord deriving rental income from Delaware property
- Attach a copy of the Canada-US Tax Treaty (Article IV, Article XIII) to establish non-resident status
- Include a copy of your most recent T1 return with the Canadian Revenue Agency

### Step 4: Processing Timeline

- **Processing time**: 4–6 weeks if submitted by mail with complete documentation
- **ITIN validity**: Valid for five years if not used; must renew if it expires
- **Follow-up**: You will receive IRS Notice CP565 (ITIN Assignment Notice) confirming your number

## Delaware-Specific Considerations

### 1. **Property Tax Deductibility**

Delaware's 0.57% average effective property tax rate is fully deductible against Delaware taxable income on your state return (Form 1040A). Ensure you claim the actual county-assessed property tax amount; real estate transfer tax (2.5%) on purchase is typically not deductible as an operating expense.

### 2. **Delaware's Employer Withholding Requirement**

If your Delaware rental property is managed by a US agent or property management company, Delaware law (Title 30, Section 1701) may require them to withhold 10% of net rental income if you do not provide your ITIN and tax certification. Obtaining your ITIN eliminates this withholding.

### 3. **Canada-US Tax Treaty Implications**

Under Article XIII of the Canada-US Tax Treaty, rental income is taxable in both countries. However, Article XXIV(4) allows Canadian residents to claim a foreign tax credit on their Canadian T1 return for Delaware state income taxes paid. You should:

- File Form 8288 (FIRPTA withholding statement) to report state taxes withheld
- Report the same Delaware income on your Canadian T1 return (Line 12699)
- Claim a foreign tax credit (Schedule 1, Line 40426) for Delaware state and local taxes paid

This prevents double taxation.

### 4. **Franchise Tax on Corporations (Not Applicable)**

If you operate through a Delaware LLC or corporation, different rules apply. For individual landlords, only the income tax applies.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Submitting an Unfiled W-7**
Never submit Form W-7 without attaching a tax return. The IRS will reject it. Submit W-7 with your first 1040-NR or as part of the FIRPTA documentation.

**2. Incorrect Applicant Type**
Selecting the wrong category in Part III causes delays. Clearly indicate "Non-resident alien" status.

**3. Using an Expired Canadian Passport**
The passport must be valid on the date you sign Form W-7. Renew your passport before submitting.

**4. Forgetting FIRPTA Withholding Documentation**
If you previously sold Delaware property and withholding was collected, include Form 8288-B or the settlement statement proving withholding occurred.

**5. Incorrect Mailing Address**
The Philadelphia IRS ITIN Unit handles Delaware. Sending Form W-7 to a general IRS address delays processing by 8–12 weeks.
