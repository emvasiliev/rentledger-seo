# Form 1040-NR for Canadian Landlords with Nebraska Rental Property

## What Is Form 1040-NR?

Form 1040-NR is the United States income tax return filed by non-resident aliens who have US-source income subject to US federal taxation. For Canadian landlords, this form becomes essential when you own rental property in the United States and elect to treat that rental income as "effectively connected income" (ECI) under Section 871(d) of the Internal Revenue Code.

Unlike the standard Form 1040 used by US citizens and residents, Form 1040-NR is specifically designed to calculate US tax liability for individuals who are not US tax residents. The form allows you to report your US rental income and claim deductions against that income—a significant advantage over the alternative 30% withholding tax that would apply without this election.

## How Form 1040-NR Applies to Nebraska Landlords

Nebraska presents a unique cross-border tax situation for Canadian landlords. When you own rental property in Nebraska, you face both federal US taxation and Nebraska state income taxation. Understanding how these layers interact is critical to your overall tax planning.

**Federal Level:** By filing Form 1040-NR with the Section 871(d) election, you report your Nebraska rental income at the federal level and deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses (mortgage interest, property taxes, insurance, repairs, depreciation, utilities paid by you, and property management fees). This election converts what would otherwise be passive investment income subject to 30% withholding into effectively connected income, allowing you to pay tax only on net rental income at graduated rates.

**Nebraska State Level:** Nebraska imposes a 5.84% state income tax on rental income earned within the state by non-resident individuals. Because you are a Canadian resident and non-resident of Nebraska, you must file Form 1040-NR federally *and* file Nebraska Form 1040N (Nebraska Nonresident and Part-Year Resident Individual Income Tax Return) with the Nebraska Department of Revenue. Nebraska does not recognize the Section 871(d) election for state purposes in the same way, so you'll report rental income on the state form as well.

**Property Tax Context:** Nebraska's average effective property tax rate of 1.73% is higher than the national average. These property taxes are deductible on your federal 1040-NR return, which provides substantial tax relief.

## Who Must File Form 1040-NR?

You must file Form 1040-NR if you are:

- A non-resident alien (which includes Canadian citizens and permanent residents of Canada who are not US tax residents)
- Have US-source income that is effectively connected with a US trade or business
- Have elected under Section 871(d) to treat rental income as effectively connected income
- Have gross income from your Nebraska rental property that exceeds the filing threshold (generally $12,550 for 2024, though this can vary)

The IRS will not automatically know you want to make the Section 871(d) election; you must affirmatively elect it on your tax return by filing Form 1040-NR. Without this election, rental income paid to a non-resident would be subject to a flat 30% withholding tax (unless reduced by the Canada-US Tax Treaty).

**Canada-US Tax Treaty Consideration:** Under Article XIII of the Canada-US Income and Gains Tax Treaty, you may be entitled to a reduced withholding rate (typically 15% instead of 30% on rental income). However, the Section 871(d) election is generally more advantageous because it allows you to pay tax only on *net* income after deductions.

## Step-by-Step Guide to Completing Form 1040-NR

### Part I: Personal Information and Residency Status

Complete your name, Canadian address, and Individual Identification Number (typically your US tax identification number—ITIN or SSN, or your Canadian Social Insurance Number if you don't have a US number). You must obtain an ITIN from the IRS before filing if you don't already have one.

In the residency status section, confirm that you are a non-resident alien for the entire tax year. Because Canada and the US have a tax treaty, you may claim treaty-based position disclosure on Form 8833 if your non-resident status is based on that treaty.

### Part II: Income and Deductions

Report your gross rental income from your Nebraska property on the appropriate line. This is where your 1098 forms from your mortgage lender and your actual rent receipts are reconciled.

Then claim your rental expenses:

- Mortgage interest (not principal)
- Real estate property taxes (deductible, crucial in Nebraska given the 1.73% rate)
- Insurance premiums
- Repairs and maintenance
- Depreciation (calculated on Form 4562)
- Property management fees (if applicable)
- Utilities you paid
- Advertising for tenants
- Professional fees (accounting, legal, tax preparation)

The net rental income figure is critical because this is the amount on which you'll pay US federal income tax.

### Part III: Tax Credits and Withholding

If your mortgage lender or tenant withholding agent submitted Form 8288-A or similar withholding, report that amount. You will claim credit for any withholding tax paid.

### Part IV: Making the Section 871(d) Election

You must clearly elect on your return that you are choosing to treat your rental income as effectively connected income. This is typically done by attaching a statement with your 1040-NR that reads: "The taxpayer elects under Section 871(d) of the Internal Revenue Code to be taxed on a net basis with respect to rental income derived from real property located in the United States."

## Nebraska-Specific Considerations

### State Income Tax Filing

You must also file Nebraska Form 1040N to report your rental income to Nebraska. The state will tax it at 5.84% on your net income (after federal deductions). The good news: you can claim your Nebraska state income taxes paid as a deduction on your federal Form 1040-NR, reducing your federal taxable income.

### Property Tax Deduction

Nebraska's 1.73% effective property tax rate is a meaningful expense. Ensure these taxes are properly documented and deducted. Property tax assessments are public record in Nebraska counties, which the IRS may cross-reference.

### Estimated Tax Payments

If you expect to owe more than $1,000 in federal and Nebraska combined taxes, you may need to make quarterly estimated tax payments. The deadline for Nebraska estimated taxes aligns with federal deadlines: April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15.

### Filing Location

Federal Form 1040-NR is filed with the IRS (depending on whether you have a US address; typically to the Philadelphia service center). Nebraska Form 1040N is filed with the Nebraska Department of Revenue.

## How to Report on Your Canadian Tax Return

As a Canadian resident, you must also report worldwide income to the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) on your T1 return. Report your US rental income in Canadian dollars on Form T776 (Statement of Real Estate Rentals). The key advantage: you can claim a foreign tax credit for US federal and Nebraska state income taxes paid, which offsets your Canadian tax liability. This prevents double taxation.

**Currency:** Convert your income and expenses to Canadian dollars using the Bank of Canada average exchange rate for the year, or use the average monthly rates if that better reflects your situation.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Forgetting to Make the Section 871(d) Election:** Many Canadian landlords file 1040-NR without formally electing to treat income as ECI, which results in a 30% withholding instead of graduated tax rates. Always include the election statement.

2. **Not Filing Nebraska Form 1040N:** Failure to file the Nebraska state return can result in penalties and interest, even if your federal return is correct.

3. **Misclassifying Expenses:** Repairs are deductible; capital improvements are not. Mortgage principal is not deductible (only interest). These distinctions significantly affect your net income calculation.

4. **Ignoring the Property Tax Deduction:** Nebraska's property taxes are a substantial deduction; failing to claim them overstates your taxable income.

5. **Not Converting Currency Properly:** Using an incorrect exchange rate can trigger CRA adjustments on your Canadian return.

## Key Deadlines

- **April 15:** Standard federal and Nebraska estimated tax payment deadline (if filing without extension)
- **June 15:** Automatic extension deadline for non-resident aliens without wages subject to US withholding
- **October 15:** Extended deadline if Form 4868 (extension request) is filed by April 15
