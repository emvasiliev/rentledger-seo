# Form W-7: ITIN Application Guide for Canadian Landlords with DC Rental Property

## What Is Form W-7?

Form W-7 (Application for IRS Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) is the IRS application used to obtain an **Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN)**—a nine-digit tax identification number for individuals who are not eligible for a US Social Security Number but have a US tax filing requirement.

An ITIN serves the same identification function as a Social Security Number (SSN) for tax administration purposes. For Canadian landlords owning rental property in the United States, an ITIN is **essential** to:

- File Form 1040-NR (US Non-Resident Alien Income Tax Return)
- Report rental income and deductions
- Comply with FIRPTA (Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act) withholding obligations
- Obtain an EIN (Employer Identification Number) if the property is held in a business entity

Without an ITIN, you cannot legally file US tax returns or establish US tax compliance, even if you believe you owe no tax. The IRS will not process returns submitted without a valid taxpayer identification number.

## How an ITIN Applies to District of Columbia Rental Property Owners

District of Columbia presents a **unique tax situation** for Canadian landlords because DC functions as both a federal jurisdiction and a municipality with its own income tax system.

### DC State Income Tax Obligation

Non-resident owners of DC rental property must file **Form D-40 (DC Non-Resident Income Tax Return)** and pay DC income tax at a flat rate of **10.75%** on all rental income sourced to DC. This is one of the highest state income tax rates in the US.

**Example**: If your DC rental property generates $50,000 in annual net rental income, you owe approximately $5,375 in DC income tax alone, *before* federal tax.

### Federal FIRPTA Withholding

When you eventually sell your DC rental property, FIRPTA requires the buyer's closing attorney to withhold **15% of the gross sale price** and remit it to the IRS. To comply with FIRPTA and provide the buyer with proper documentation, you **must have an ITIN** before the sale occurs.

### The ITIN-FIRPTA Connection

FIRPTA withholding is calculated and reported using your ITIN. Without an ITIN on file with the IRS *before closing*, the withholding agent cannot properly process the transaction, and you may face:

- Delays in closing
- Excess withholding (potentially 31% instead of 15%)
- Difficulty claiming refunds of over-withheld amounts

### Property Tax Considerations

While DC's real property tax rate averages **0.56%** annually, this is *separate* from income tax and does not require ITIN (property taxes are paid to the DC Assessor). However, your rental income derived from DC property is subject to the 10.75% income tax rate, which does require ITIN filing.

## Who Must File Form W-7

You must file Form W-7 if you are:

1. **A Canadian citizen or permanent resident** with no US Social Security Number
2. **Required to file a US tax return** (1040-NR for rental income)
3. **A non-resident alien** for US tax purposes (not a US citizen or green card holder)
4. **Deriving US-source income** (rental income from DC property qualifies)
5. **Subject to FIRPTA** (when selling property)

You do **not** need an ITIN if:

- You hold US rental property through a Canadian corporation (the corporation needs an EIN, not your personal ITIN)
- You have no US tax filing obligation (unlikely if you own DC rental property)
- You already possess a valid SSN

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form W-7

### Step 1: Gather Required Documents

Before submitting Form W-7, assemble:

- **Completed Form W-7** (download from IRS.gov)
- **Valid passport** (photocopy of identification page and any visa pages)
- **Proof of status** (Canadian passport, birth certificate, or permanent residency certificate)
- **IRS Form 1040-NR** (draft or completed) showing your US tax filing requirement
- **Proof of DC rental property ownership** (deed, property tax assessment, purchase documents)

### Step 2: Complete Form W-7 Accurately

**Section 1: Personal Information**
- Enter your legal name *exactly as it appears on your passport*
- Provide your Canadian address (not a temporary US address)
- Country of citizenship: **Canada**

**Section 2: Type of ITIN Applicant**
- Check box **c**: "Nonresident alien individual required to file a US income tax return"

**Section 3: Reason for Applying for ITIN**
- Check: "Applying for the ITIN to file a US income tax return"
- List Form 1040-NR as the primary form

**Section 4: Declaration**
- Sign and date the form
- Signature must match your passport signature

### Step 3: Attach Form 1040-NR or Schedule NEC

Include either:
- A **draft or actual Form 1040-NR** showing your DC rental income (even if unfiled)
- **Schedule NEC (Non-Resident Earnings from US Sources)** explaining your rental income

Do **not** file the actual 1040-NR with W-7; only provide it as supporting documentation of your tax filing requirement.

### Step 4: Submit Correctly

**Valid submission methods:**

1. **IRS Acceptance Agent** (recommended): Visit an authorized acceptance agent in Canada (some Canadian banks, law firms, and tax services offer this). This provides same-day processing.

2. **Mail to IRS**:
   ```
   Internal Revenue Service
   ITIN Operation
   P.O. Box 149342
   Austin, TX 73714-9342
   USA
   ```
   Processing time: 4–6 weeks

3. **US Embassy/Consulate in Canada**: Schedule an appointment at the nearest US Embassy or Consulate. Bring original passport and completed Form W-7 with supporting documents.

### Step 5: Receive Your ITIN

The IRS will issue your ITIN via mail within 4–6 weeks (or same-day if submitted through an acceptance agent). Your ITIN letter will be valid indefinitely *unless* you fail to file a US tax return for three consecutive years.

## District of Columbia-Specific Considerations

### 1. DC-40 Non-Resident Return Requirement

Your ITIN is necessary to file **Form D-40 (District of Columbia Non-Resident Income Tax Return)**. This return is due by **June 15** (three months after the federal deadline of April 15) and must report all rental income at the 10.75% rate.

The DC Office of the Chief Financial Officer (OCFO) requires an ITIN or SSN for processing. Without an ITIN, your D-40 cannot be filed or accepted.

### 2. DC Estimated Tax Payments

As a non-resident earning DC rental income, you may be required to make **quarterly DC estimated tax payments**. These are due:
- April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15
- Calculate: (Estimated annual rental income × 10.75%) ÷ 4

Your ITIN must appear on all estimated payment vouchers.

### 3. Canada-US Tax Treaty Relief

The **Canada-US Tax Treaty** (Article XXII) allows Canadian residents to claim a foreign tax credit on their Canadian T1 return (Line 40526) for DC income tax paid. This prevents double taxation.

**How this works**:
- File Form 1040-NR reporting DC rental income
- Pay DC income tax (10.75%)
- File Canadian T1 return reporting the *same* DC rental income
- Claim the DC tax paid as a foreign tax credit on Line 40526
- **Result**: You pay tax at the higher of the two rates, not both

### 4. DC Property Transfer Tax

When selling DC rental property, in addition to FIRPTA withholding (15%), you must also pay DC's **transfer and recordation tax** (approximately 1.1% to 1.5%). This is paid by the seller at closing and is *separate* from FIRPTA withholding.
