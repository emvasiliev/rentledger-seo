# Form 8288: FIRPTA Withholding Guide for Canadian Landlords Selling Kentucky Rental Property

## What Is Form 8288?

Form 8288 is the **U.S. Withholding Tax Return for Dispositions by Foreign Persons of U.S. Real Property Interests**, required under the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA). When you, as a Canadian resident, sell rental property located in the United States, the buyer must withhold 15% of the gross sale price and remit it to the IRS. Form 8288 is the mechanism through which this withholding is reported and submitted.

FIRPTA exists to ensure the U.S. government collects tax on gains realized by foreign investors selling American real property. Without this withholding requirement, foreign sellers could potentially leave the country without satisfying U.S. tax obligations on property disposition gains.

## How FIRPTA and Form 8288 Apply in Kentucky

Kentucky presents specific considerations for Canadian landlords selling rental properties:

**Kentucky State Tax Implications**

Kentucky imposes a state income tax of 4.5% on all income, including capital gains. When you sell Kentucky rental property, you'll face both federal FIRPTA withholding (15%) and potential Kentucky state-level tax obligations. Unlike some states that don't impose income tax on non-residents, Kentucky requires non-resident sellers to file a Kentucky state income tax return (Form 740-NR) for the year of sale to report the gain on the property disposition.

The 15% federal withholding under FIRPTA is separate from Kentucky state withholding. You may face additional Kentucky tax liability beyond the federal 15% withheld, depending on your actual capital gain and applicable deductions. This is crucial: the federal withholding is not necessarily sufficient to cover your total U.S. tax liability.

**Kentucky Property Tax Context**

Kentucky's effective property tax rate averages 0.86%, among the lowest in the nation. While property taxes during your ownership period are separate from the sale transaction, understanding Kentucky's tax environment helps explain your total investment costs and the capital gain calculation when you eventually sell.

## Who Files Form 8288?

**Primary Filers: The Buyer**

In most cases, the **buyer of your Kentucky rental property** is responsible for filing Form 8288 with the IRS within 20 days of the transfer. The buyer must withhold 15% of the gross sales price and submit Form 8288 along with the withholding payment (typically via EFTPS—Electronic Federal Tax Payment System).

**Secondary Role: The Seller**

As the Canadian seller, you don't typically file Form 8288 yourself. However, you can proactively request a **Withholding Certificate** (Certificate of Withholding—also called an IRC Section 1445(b) certificate) before the sale closes. This certificate, obtained from the IRS, can reduce or eliminate the 15% withholding requirement if you can demonstrate:

- Your sale price is less than $300,000 and the property will be your personal residence
- You can prove reduced tax liability on the gain
- Special circumstances warrant reduced withholding

To request a withholding certificate, you file **Form 8288-B** with the IRS Service Center for your state.

## Step-by-Step: How Form 8288 Is Completed

While the buyer files this form, understanding its components helps you prepare for sale proceeds:

**Section 1: Property Information**
- Legal description of the Kentucky property
- Street address and parcel/account number
- Date of transfer/closing date

**Section 2: Seller Information**
- Your name, Canadian address, and tax identification number
- If you have a U.S. Individual Identification Number (ITIN) or Social Security Number, this must be included

**Section 3: Buyer Information**
- Purchaser's name, address, and taxpayer identification number
- Entity type (individual, corporation, trust, etc.)

**Section 4: Withholding Computation**
- Amount realized from the sale (gross sale price)
- Withholding calculation: typically 15% of amount realized
- Actual withholding paid

**Section 5: Transmittal**
- Verification that withholding was submitted to the IRS

The buyer files this form with Form 8288-A (Statement of Withholding to Foreign Persons) provided to you for your records.

## Kentucky-Specific Considerations for Canadian Sellers

**Coordinating Federal and State Withholding**

Kentucky does not have a separate FIRPTA-equivalent withholding requirement at the state level. However, you will still owe Kentucky income tax on the gain. The 15% federal withholding does **not** cover Kentucky's 4.5% state tax. This means:

- Federal withholding satisfies part of your federal obligation
- Kentucky withholding does not automatically occur at closing
- You must file Form 740-NR (Kentucky Nonresident Return) to settle any additional state tax due

**Coordinating with Canadian Tax Obligations**

As a Canadian resident, you must also report this U.S. property disposition on your Canadian T1 General Return. Under Canadian tax law, the adjusted cost basis (typically your purchase price plus capital improvements) is subtracted from the sale proceeds to calculate the capital gain. Only 50% of the capital gain is taxable in Canada (the inclusion rate as of 2024).

The Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty (Article 6) addresses real property income and gains. Generally, gains from disposing of real property are taxable in the country where the property is located—meaning your Kentucky property gain is taxed by the U.S. first. Canada typically allows a foreign tax credit on your Canadian return for U.S. federal tax paid, reducing Canadian tax liability on the same gain.

**Claiming Foreign Tax Credits**

File Form T2209 (Federal Foreign Tax Credits) with your Canadian tax return to claim a credit for U.S. federal tax paid on the Kentucky property sale. Keep all documentation: the Form 8288-A received from the buyer, your Form 1040-NR filing (if required), and proof of tax paid.

## Common Mistakes Canadian Sellers Make

**Mistake #1: Assuming 15% Withholding Covers All Liability**

The 15% federal withholding is a **deposit**, not your final tax bill. If your capital gain is substantial, you may owe more than 15% in federal tax alone, plus 4.5% Kentucky state tax. Budget accordingly and engage a cross-border accountant.

**Mistake #2: Forgetting Kentucky State Return Filing**

Many Canadian sellers overlook Kentucky Form 740-NR. The state expects nonresident sellers to report Kentucky-source gains. Failing to file can result in penalties and interest, even if your gain is small.

**Mistake #3: Not Requesting a Withholding Certificate When Eligible**

If your sale qualifies for reduced withholding under IRC Section 1445(b), file Form 8288-B early—well before closing. This reduces the cash tied up at closing and simplifies your tax settlement.

**Mistake #4: Overlooking Capital Improvements**

Canadian sellers sometimes forget to document capital improvements made to the Kentucky rental property. These reduce your capital gain. Keep receipts and photographs; claim these deductions on Form 1040-NR.

## Key Takeaways for Canadian Kentucky Landlords

- **15% federal withholding under FIRPTA is a deposit, not your final U.S. tax liability.** You'll likely owe additional federal tax plus Kentucky's 4.5% state tax, requiring Form 1040-NR and Form 740-NR filings to settle the full amount and claim any refund.

- **Request a withholding certificate (Form 8288-B) early if your sale qualifies for reduced withholding.** This can significantly improve your cash position at closing and reduces administrative burden post-sale.

- **Coordinate U.S. and Canadian tax filings to claim foreign tax credits on your Canadian T1 return.** The Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty permits credits for U.S. federal tax paid, minimizing double taxation on the same Kentucky property
