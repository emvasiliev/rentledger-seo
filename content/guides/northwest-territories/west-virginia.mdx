## US Rental Property Ownership: A Tax Guide for Northwest Territories Landlords

Owning rental property in West Virginia as a Northwest Territories resident places you at the intersection of three tax jurisdictions: Canada (federal and territorial), the United States (federal), and West Virginia state. Without a clear understanding of your obligations in each jurisdiction, you risk non-compliance penalties, excess withholdings, and missed deductions. This guide walks you through the filing requirements, tax rates, and deadlines you need to know.

## Overview: Why This Combination Matters

As a non-resident of both the US and West Virginia, you are subject to:

- **Canadian taxation** on your worldwide income (including US rental income)
- **US federal taxation** as a non-resident alien earning US-source income
- **West Virginia state taxation** on income from property located in the state
- **Withholding taxes** at multiple levels unless proper elections are made

The key to managing this structure is understanding that income is taxed in multiple jurisdictions, but foreign tax credits (and careful filing) can prevent double taxation. West Virginia's 6.5% state income tax and 0.59% average effective property tax rate add meaningful costs to your rental operation that must be tracked separately for Canadian tax purposes.

## Canadian Tax Obligations

### Reporting Rental Income on Your T776

You must report all rental income and expenses related to your West Virginia property on your Canadian tax return, in Canadian dollars.

**Income conversion:** Convert all US dollars to Canadian dollars using the Bank of Canada exchange rate for the year the income was earned. For 2025, use an average rate of 1 USD = 1.36 CAD. If you earned US$10,000 in rent during 2025, you report CAD$13,600 on your T776.

**Form T776 (Statement of Real Estate Rentals)** must be filed with your personal tax return if you have a net loss or if the CRA requests it. Many landlords file it proactively to establish a clear record. Report:

- Gross rental income (converted to CAD)
- Mortgage interest paid (converted to CAD)
- Property tax (converted to CAD)
- Insurance, utilities, maintenance, and repairs (all converted to CAD)
- Capital cost allowance (CCA), if claimed
- West Virginia state income tax paid (converted to CAD) — this is deductible

### Foreign Property Reporting: Form T1135

If the fair market value of your West Virginia property exceeds CAD$100,000 at any time during the tax year, you must file **Form T1135 (Foreign Income Verification Statement)**.

Most rental properties in West Virginia will exceed this threshold, so you will likely file this form annually. Report the cost basis and fair market value of the property in Canadian dollars. Failure to file T1135 when required results in a penalty of CAD$2,500 for individuals.

### Foreign Tax Credit

You will pay income tax in three places: Canada (federal/territorial), the US (federal), and West Virginia (state). Canada allows a foreign tax credit to offset some of the US and state taxes paid.

**How it works:** Calculate your Canadian tax liability on the US rental income, then claim a credit for US federal and West Virginia state income taxes actually paid. You cannot claim a credit for US property taxes—instead, deduct them as an expense on your T776.

The foreign tax credit is limited to the lesser of:

1. Foreign taxes actually paid (US federal + West Virginia state income tax)
2. Canadian tax rate multiplied by the foreign income

File **Form T2209 (Federal Foreign Tax Credits)** with your personal tax return to claim this credit. This is essential—without it, you will pay full Canadian tax plus full US tax on the same income.

**Territorial consideration:** Northwest Territories has a top marginal tax rate of approximately 40.05% (combined federal-territorial, 2025). When combined with US federal and state taxes, your total tax burden can exceed 50% without careful planning.

## US Federal Tax Obligations

### Obtain an ITIN

You cannot use your Social Insurance Number (SIN) to file US tax returns. Instead, apply for an **Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN)**.

File **Form W-7 (Application for IRS Individual Taxpayer Identification Number)** with the IRS. You can file it with your first US tax return (Form 1040-NR) or submit it separately. The ITIN is valid indefinitely if your US tax filing obligations continue. Processing typically takes 4–6 weeks.

### File Form 1040-NR: US Individual Income Tax Return for Nonresidents Aliens

**Filing deadline:** June 15, 2025 (for tax year 2024 income). The April 15 deadline that applies to US residents does not apply to non-residents; you get an automatic two-month extension.

**File with:** IRS, Philadelphia Service Center, if you file on paper.

**What to report:**
- Schedule E (Supplemental Income and Loss): Report gross rental income, expenses, and net rental income
- Convert all amounts from USD to CAD using the exchange rate on the day received, or use the average rate for the year (IRS allows either method—choose one and be consistent)

### Schedule E and Deductible Expenses

You can deduct all ordinary and necessary expenses on Schedule E:

- Mortgage interest
- Property taxes
- Insurance
- Repairs and maintenance
- Property management fees
- Utilities (if you pay them)
- Depreciation (not available if you make a Section 871(d) election—see below)

**Do not deduct** Canadian taxes (GST/HST, income tax). These are deducted on your Canadian T776, not on your US return.

### Section 871(d) Election: Treat Rental Income as Effectively Connected Income

This is the most important election for cross-border landlords.

**The problem:** By default, non-resident aliens are subject to a **30% gross withholding tax** on US-source rental income (without deductions allowed). This means if you earn US$10,000 in rent, US$3,000 is withheld, and you cannot deduct expenses to reduce your taxable income.

**The solution:** File **Form 8288-B (U.S. Real Property Interest Disposition by Foreign Persons)** to elect under Section 871(d). This election allows you to:

- Deduct all rental expenses (mortgage interest, taxes, insurance, depreciation, repairs)
- Pay tax only on net income (not gross income)
- Potentially reduce your US federal tax liability significantly

**How to file:** File Form 8288-B with your Form 1040-NR by the June 15 deadline. Once filed, the election applies to all future years unless revoked.

**With this election, you typically owe US federal income tax at the standard graduated rates (10%–37%, depending on income level), not a flat 30% withholding.**

This election is almost always beneficial for landlords with meaningful deductions (especially mortgage interest and depreciation).

## West Virginia State Tax Obligations

### File WV Form IT-100 (West Virginia Personal Income Tax Return)

**Filing deadline:** June 15, 2025 (same as federal).

**Tax rate:** West Virginia imposes a flat 6.5% state income tax on non-resident income sourced in the state.

**What to report:**
- Rental income (converted to USD for this return)
- Deductible expenses (same as reported on Schedule E)
- West Virginia income tax (if withheld)

**Deductions:** You can deduct the same rental expenses on your West Virginia return as you do on your federal return, reducing your taxable income from 6.5% to whatever your net income is.

### Estimated Tax Payments

If you expect to owe more than US$1,000 in West Virginia income tax for the year, you should make quarterly estimated tax payments.

- **Due dates:** April 15, June 15, September 15 (of the tax year), and January 15 (of the following year)
- **Form:** WV Form ES-1 (Estimated Tax Declaration)
- **Failure to pay:** Can result in penalties and interest

## Selling Your Property: FIRPTA Basics

If you sell your West Virginia rental property, you trigger **Foreign Investment in Real Property Act (FIRPTA)** withholding.
