# Form 1040-NR for Canadian Landlords with Oklahoma Rental Property

## What Is Form 1040-NR?

Form 1040-NR (U.S. Nonresident Alien Income Tax Return) is the primary federal income tax return filed by non-resident aliens—including Canadian citizens—who have income effectively connected with a U.S. trade or business. For Canadian landlords owning rental property in Oklahoma, this form becomes necessary when you make the Section 871(d) election, which allows you to deduct ordinary and necessary rental expenses (mortgage interest, property taxes, maintenance, depreciation) directly against your US rental income, rather than paying tax on gross rental receipts.

Without this election, your US rental income would be subject to a flat 30% withholding tax under Section 871(a), with minimal deduction opportunities. The 871(d) election is therefore a critical tax planning strategy for cross-border landlords.

## How Form 1040-NR Applies in Oklahoma

Oklahoma presents a specific compliance challenge for Canadian landlords: the state imposes a 4.75% income tax on rental income earned by non-resident individuals. This means your Oklahoma rental activity triggers both **federal 1040-NR filing** and **Oklahoma state return filing** obligations.

### Federal vs. State Requirements

On the federal level, you file Form 1040-NR to report your Oklahoma rental income and claim deductions. On the state level, you must file **Oklahoma Form 511-NR** (Nonresident Claim for Deduction—Individual Income Tax Return) or the full **Oklahoma Form 511** if your income exceeds Oklahoma's filing threshold (generally $3,150 for non-residents in recent tax years, though you should verify the current threshold).

Oklahoma's 4.75% state rate applies to your net rental income (gross rents minus allowable deductions). Combined with the federal rate of 10–37% (depending on your tax bracket), your effective Oklahoma rental income tax can be substantial.

### Property Tax Context

Oklahoma's average effective property tax rate is 0.9%, which is relatively low compared to national averages. This is significant because property taxes are a deductible expense on both your federal 1040-NR and Oklahoma state return, reducing your taxable income in both jurisdictions.

## Who Must File Form 1040-NR

You must file Form 1040-NR if you meet all of the following criteria:

1. **Non-resident alien status**: You are not a U.S. citizen, permanent resident (green card holder), or resident for tax purposes under the substantial presence test.
2. **US-source income**: Your income is derived from Oklahoma rental property.
3. **Effectively connected income (ECI)**: Your rental income is effectively connected with a U.S. trade or business (which it is if you actively manage or lease the property).
4. **Section 871(d) election**: You have filed (or intend to file) Form 8288-B to make the Section 871(d) election, which requires you to file a full 1040-NR.

Canadian citizens holding a U.S. rental property almost always file 1040-NR because the Section 871(d) election typically provides significant tax savings compared to the alternative 30% withholding regime.

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form 1040-NR for Oklahoma Rental Income

### Step 1: Confirm Your Filing Status and Residency

At the top of Form 1040-NR, you must indicate your residency status. Check the "Nonresident alien" box. You will also select a filing status; most Canadian landlords use "Single" unless married filing jointly with a U.S. spouse or another qualifying circumstance applies.

Verify that you do not meet the substantial presence test (generally, fewer than 183 days in the U.S. during the current year plus weighted presence in the two prior years). If you do, you may be classified as a resident alien for tax purposes, requiring Form 1040 instead.

### Step 2: Report Oklahoma Rental Income

On Schedule E (Supplemental Income and Loss), report your Oklahoma rental property details:
- Address of the property
- Gross rental income received
- Rental period (usually January 1–December 31)

If you own multiple properties, each appears on a separate Schedule E line.

### Step 3: Claim Deductions

This is where the 871(d) election delivers value. Claim all ordinary and necessary deductions:

- **Mortgage interest**: If you financed the Oklahoma property, interest (not principal) is deductible.
- **Property taxes**: Oklahoma property taxes are fully deductible on both federal and state returns.
- **Repairs and maintenance**: Materials, labor, and contractor fees for maintaining the property.
- **Management fees**: If you hire a property manager or real estate agent.
- **Utilities and insurance**: Property insurance, liability coverage, and any utilities you pay.
- **Depreciation**: A non-cash deduction allowing you to recover your basis in the building (not land) over 27.5 years for residential property. Ensure you have a qualified cost segregation study if purchased recently.
- **HOA fees, advertising, and office expenses** directly tied to rental activity.

Enter total deductions on Schedule E. If expenses exceed income, you may report a loss (subject to passive activity loss limitations under Section 469).

### Step 4: Complete Form 8288-B

If you have not already done so, file **Form 8288-B** (Certificate of Withholding—Section 1446 Withholding from Foreign Persons' U.S. Real Property Interests) with your 1040-NR. This form formally makes the Section 871(d) election and certifies that you are liable for taxes on net income rather than the flat withholding rate.

Form 8288-B requires you to estimate your U.S. income tax liability for the year. This estimate is crucial for withholding purposes if you have a U.S. agent or if the IRS demands prepayment.

### Step 5: Calculate Tax and Apply Foreign Tax Credit

Your federal tax is calculated on your net Oklahoma rental income using the nonresident alien tax table or rates. 

Then, calculate the Oklahoma state tax: multiply your net rental income by 4.75%.

On Form 1040-NR (or the attached forms), you can claim a **foreign tax credit** under IRC Section 901 for taxes paid to Oklahoma (and Canada, if applicable). The foreign tax credit is the lesser of your actual tax paid or your U.S. tax attributable to that foreign-source income. This prevents double taxation.

### Step 6: File Your Canadian T1 Return

Concurrently, you must report the same Oklahoma rental income on your Canadian **T1 General Income Tax Return** (Schedule 11 for rental income). You can claim a foreign tax credit on your Canadian return for U.S. federal taxes paid, under ITA Section 126. The credit is calculated as Canadian tax times the ratio of foreign-source income to total income.

The Canada-US Tax Treaty (Article XXII) provides guidance on relief from double taxation and defines "resident" for treaty purposes. As a Canadian resident, you may benefit from treaty provisions that limit U.S. taxation on certain items or provide credits.

## Oklahoma-Specific Considerations

### State Return Requirement

You must simultaneously file **Oklahoma Form 511-NR**. Oklahoma requires non-residents to file if they have income from Oklahoma sources exceeding the threshold. The state does not recognize a blanket exemption for non-residents; even a small rental income may trigger a filing requirement.

### No Reciprocal Agreement

Oklahoma has no reciprocal tax agreements that would exempt Canadian residents. You will pay the full 4.75% state rate on net rental income.

### Estimated Tax Payments

If your Oklahoma rental income is substantial and you do not have adequate U.S. withholding, the IRS may require estimated tax payments (Form 1040-ES). Similarly, Oklahoma may require quarterly estimated payments if you owe more than $500 in state tax. Failure to pay estimated tax can result in penalties and interest.

### Statute of Limitations

The IRS standard statute of limitations is three years from the filing date. However, if you underreport foreign-source income by more than 25%, the statute extends to six years. Maintain meticulous records of all rental receipts, expenses, and property management documentation.
