# Form 8840: Closer Connection Exception for Canadian Landlords with Virginia Rental Property

## What Is Form 8840?

Form 8840 (Closer Connection Exception Statement for Aliens) is an IRS form that allows certain non-US residents to claim they maintain a "closer connection" to their home country despite meeting the **Substantial Presence Test (SPT)** in the United States. This form prevents you from being classified as a US resident alien for federal tax purposes in a given year—a status that would trigger worldwide income taxation by the IRS.

For Canadian landlords owning rental property in Virginia, Form 8840 is a critical compliance tool that bridges two tax systems. Filing it correctly can mean the difference between paying US federal tax on your worldwide income versus only on your US-source income (rental property returns).

## How the Substantial Presence Test Works

The Substantial Presence Test calculates your physical days in the US using a weighted formula:

- **Full calendar days** in the US in the current year
- **One-third of days** present in the immediately preceding year
- **One-sixth of days** present in the second preceding year

If this total exceeds 183 days, you **automatically become a US resident alien** for federal tax purposes—unless you file Form 8840 to establish closer connection.

**Example scenario:** A Canadian landlord managing Virginia rental property spends 120 days in Virginia in 2024, 90 days in 2023, and 60 days in 2022.
- 2024 calculation: 120 + (90 ÷ 3) + (60 ÷ 6) = 120 + 30 + 10 = **160 days** (below threshold)

However, if this landlord exceeded 183 days in any year, Form 8840 becomes essential.

## Virginia-Specific Tax Implications

Understanding Virginia's tax environment is essential before filing Form 8840, as your filing decision affects both federal and state-level obligations.

### Virginia State Income Tax on Non-Residents

Virginia imposes a **5.75% state income tax rate** on rental income earned within the state. Even if Form 8840 prevents federal resident alien classification, you remain subject to Virginia state taxation on rental income from Virginia property.

As a non-resident, you must file **Virginia Form 540-NR** (Non-Resident Tax Return) or **Form 540-NR-FY** (for fiscal year filers) reporting your Virginia-source rental income. This filing is independent of Form 8840 and is required regardless of your federal classification.

**Key point:** Form 8840 addresses federal classification only. It does not exempt you from Virginia state income tax obligations.

### Virginia Property Taxes

Virginia's average effective property tax rate is approximately **0.82%** of assessed property value. Counties set their own rates within state guidelines, typically ranging from 0.55% to 1.0%. For landlords, these property taxes are deductible against Virginia rental income, reducing your Virginia taxable rental income.

Example: A Virginia rental property valued at $400,000 in a county with a 0.82% rate generates $3,280 in annual property tax—fully deductible against rental income on Form 540-NR.

## Who Must File Form 8840

You must file Form 8840 if **all four conditions** are met:

1. You meet the Substantial Presence Test (183+ weighted days in the US)
2. You are a non-US citizen without a green card
3. You have a **closer connection** to Canada (your home country)
4. You want to avoid resident alien classification

**Closer connection** means:
- You maintain your permanent home in Canada
- You spend more time in Canada than in the US in the relevant year
- Your family, employment, and financial ties center on Canada
- You intend to return to Canada

For Canadian landlords, "closer connection" typically includes maintaining your principal residence in Canada, holding Canadian employment or business interests, and possessing Canadian banking/investments—all standard for cross-border property owners.

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form 8840

### Part I: Identification Information

Enter your name, address (Canadian address preferred to demonstrate closer connection), and date of birth. Your US taxpayer identification number (ITIN) goes here—you'll need one even as a non-resident.

### Part II: Substantial Presence Test Calculation

Complete the SPT calculation showing that you exceeded 183 days under the weighted formula. This section forces you to document why Form 8840 is necessary.

### Part III: Closer Connection Facts

This is the critical section. You must substantiate your closer connection through:

- **Principal residence location:** State your Canadian address and confirm you maintain a permanent home in Canada
- **Days present in each country:** List total days in the US vs. Canada for the tax year
- **Family and relatives:** Indicate that your spouse, children, or dependents reside in Canada
- **Employment/business:** List Canadian employment, business ownership, or professional licenses
- **Finances:** Reference Canadian bank accounts, investments, and assets
- **Intentions:** State your intent to return to Canada

For Virginia landlords, you might note: "Maintain principal residence in [Canadian province], own rental property in Virginia for investment purposes only, conduct business from Canadian office, maintain Canadian employment with [employer], and intend to return to Canada upon retirement."

### Part IV: Certification

Sign and date the form. You must sign under penalty of perjury.

## Virginia-Specific Considerations

### Interaction with Virginia Tax Filing

Filing Form 8840 **does not eliminate** your Virginia Form 540-NR filing requirement. Virginia taxes non-residents on state-source income regardless of federal classification. You will still report Virginia rental income on Form 540-NR at the 5.75% rate and receive a Virginia credit on your Canadian T1 return.

### Foreign Tax Credit Coordination

When you file your Canadian T1 return, you'll claim a **federal foreign tax credit** for US federal taxes paid (if any) and a **provincial foreign tax credit** for Virginia state taxes paid. Form 8840 reduces or eliminates federal tax liability, but Virginia state tax remains.

The foreign tax credit coordination requires:
- US federal return (Form 1040) with Form 8840 attached
- Virginia state return (Form 540-NR) showing Virginia tax paid
- Canadian T1 return claiming the foreign tax credit for all US taxes paid

### Document Retention

Maintain documentation proving closer connection for at least six years:
- Lease agreements for your Canadian residence
- Utility bills at your Canadian address
- Canadian bank statements
- Proof of Canadian employment or business
- Calendar or travel records showing days in each country
- Vehicle registration (if applicable)

The IRS frequently challenges Form 8840 filings. Virginia rental activity (requiring management time and travel) can appear to establish US ties; comprehensive documentation is essential.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Filing too late:** Form 8840 must attach to your Form 1040, filed by June 15 (for non-residents). Filing after June 15 may result in rejection and resident alien classification retroactively.

**2. Conflicting documentation:** Don't claim Canadian closer connection while reporting extensive US rental activity details that suggest US ties. Be consistent across all forms.

**3. Forgetting Virginia state returns:** Many landlords file Form 8840 for federal purposes but forget Virginia Form 540-NR, creating state compliance problems and audit risk.

**4. Understating days in the US:** Intentionally under-reporting days to avoid SPT triggers audit flags. Report actual days accurately.

**5. Insufficient closer connection evidence:** Simply stating closer connection isn't enough. Document it thoroughly with residence proof, employment verification, and financial records.

**6. Ignoring property tax deductions:** On Virginia Form 540-NR, ensure you deduct Virginia property taxes, mortgage interest, depreciation, and operating expenses. These reduce taxable rental income significantly.

Note: If you extend your US return to October 15, Form 8840 must still file by June 15—an unusual requirement that many filers miss.
