# Form 1040-NR for Canadian Landlords with North Dakota Rental Property

## What Is Form 1040-NR?

Form 1040-NR (U.S. Income Tax Return for Nonresident Alien Individuals) is the primary federal income tax return filed by non-residents of the United States, including Canadian citizens, who have U.S.-source income subject to U.S. tax. For Canadian landlords owning rental property in North Dakota, this form is essential for reporting rental income, claiming deductions, and making the Section 871(d) election—a critical decision that allows you to deduct mortgage interest, property taxes, utilities, maintenance, and other operating expenses against your rental income rather than facing a flat 30% withholding tax on gross revenue.

Without the Section 871(d) election, the IRS treats U.S. rental income for non-residents as "fixed or determinable annual or periodical" (FDAP) income, subject to a 30% gross-basis withholding tax. By filing Form 1040-NR and making the election, you shift to net-income taxation—reporting income after legitimate deductions—which typically results in significantly lower tax liability.

## How Form 1040-NR Applies to North Dakota Landlords

North Dakota presents a unique scenario for Canadian landlords because it combines federal taxation with state-level obligations. Here's what makes North Dakota specific:

**Federal Tax Obligations:**
Your U.S. rental income from North Dakota is considered "effectively connected income" (ECI) under Internal Revenue Code Section 871(d). This means it is subject to U.S. federal income tax at graduated rates (currently 10% to 37%, depending on income level) as if you were a U.S. resident.

**North Dakota State Tax Obligations:**
North Dakota imposes a state income tax at a rate of 2.5% on rental income earned within the state by non-residents. This means you must file both a federal 1040-NR and a North Dakota state non-resident return (ND Form 40-NR). The state return is filed with the North Dakota Office of State Tax Commissioner and typically follows the federal filing deadline.

**Property Tax Context:**
North Dakota's effective property tax rate averages 0.98%, which is relatively low compared to many U.S. states. These property taxes are fully deductible on Form 1040-NR Schedule E, reducing your taxable income at the federal level. They are also deductible on the North Dakota state return.

## Who Must File Form 1040-NR?

You must file Form 1040-NR if you meet all of the following criteria:

- You are a non-resident alien (not a U.S. citizen or permanent resident)
- You have U.S.-source income from North Dakota rental property
- Your income exceeds the filing threshold (for 2024, generally $1,150 for non-residents, though rental income typically requires filing regardless of amount if you claim the Section 871(d) election)
- You wish to deduct rental expenses against your rental income (Section 871(d) election)

Canadian citizens who own North Dakota rental property almost always meet these criteria. Even if your net income is zero or negative (due to expenses), filing is mandatory to maintain the Section 871(d) election and substantiate deductions.

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form 1040-NR

### Part I: Identification and Basic Information

Begin by entering your name, mailing address (your Canadian address), and Individual Identification Number (ITIN) or Social Security Number (SSN). If you don't have an ITIN, you must obtain one from the IRS before filing; this requires Form W-7 submitted with your 1040-NR. Most Canadian landlords use an ITIN since they are not U.S. residents.

Indicate your country of citizenship (Canada) and your visa type status (typically "No visa").

### Part II: Income and Deductions

**Report rental income on Schedule E (Supplemental Income and Loss).** Schedule E is attached to Form 1040-NR and is where rental property income and expenses are reported. For your North Dakota property:

- Report gross rental income (rent received)
- List all deductible expenses: mortgage interest, property taxes, insurance, utilities, repairs and maintenance, depreciation, property management fees, and advertising
- Calculate net rental income or loss

**Critical Election: Section 871(d).** To claim deductions, you must make the election on Form 8288-B (Statement of Withholding on Dispositions by Foreign Persons) or through a statement attached to your 1040-NR indicating your intent to be taxed on net income rather than gross income. Many tax professionals file this statement affirmatively with the return to ensure clear documentation.

### Part III: Credits and Withholding

If U.S. federal income tax was withheld from your rental income (often at 30% on gross rental income prior to making the election), report it in the withholding section. Additionally, if you have paid estimated quarterly taxes on your rental income, include those payments.

### Part IV: North Dakota Considerations in the Federal Return

While Form 1040-NR is federal, you will use the same Schedule E data to prepare your North Dakota state return. The North Dakota state return will calculate your state tax liability at 2.5% on your net rental income.

## North Dakota-Specific Considerations

### State Return Filing Requirements

After completing Form 1040-NR federally, you must file **North Dakota Form 40-NR** (Nonresident/Part-Year Resident Income Tax Return) with the state. This return is due on the same date as your federal return: **April 15** (or June 15 if you do not have U.S. wages subject to withholding). 

North Dakota allows you to claim a credit for federal income taxes paid, which helps offset double taxation on the same income.

### Border State Coordination

As a Canadian resident near the Manitoba or Saskatchewan border, ensure that you have not incorrectly characterized your property as being in a neighboring province. The distinction is critical: only properties physically located in North Dakota are subject to North Dakota and U.S. federal taxation under this regime.

### Canada-US Tax Treaty

The Canada-U.S. Income and Goods Tax Treaty addresses taxation of rental income. Article XIII specifies that income from real property may be taxed in the state where the property is located. The treaty also provides a foreign tax credit mechanism: you may be entitled to claim a credit on your Canadian T1 return for U.S. federal and state taxes paid on the same income, preventing double taxation.

When you file your Canadian T1 return, report the U.S. rental income in Canadian dollars and claim the foreign tax credit (using Form T2036) for all U.S. federal and North Dakota state taxes paid. This is essential to your overall cross-border tax planning.

### Currency and Reporting

Report all amounts in U.S. dollars on Form 1040-NR. When you file your Canadian T1, convert U.S. dollar amounts to Canadian dollars using the average exchange rate for the tax year (provided by the Bank of Canada). Keep detailed records of conversion rates used for CRA audit purposes.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Failing to make the Section 871(d) election:** Many Canadian landlords file 1040-NR but do not affirmatively elect net-basis taxation, resulting in 30% withholding on gross income. Always clearly state the election on your return.

**2. Not filing the North Dakota state return:** Omitting the state return triggers penalties and interest from the North Dakota Office of State Tax Commissioner, even if federal taxes are paid.

**3. Ignoring the foreign tax credit on the Canadian T1:** Failing to claim the foreign tax credit on your T1 results in double taxation on the same income. This is a significant mistake that reduces your overall tax efficiency.

**4. Mishandling depreciation:** U.S. depreciation claimed on Form 1040-NR does not align with Canadian CCA calculations. When you eventually sell the property, recapture can be significant. Coordinate U.S. and Canadian tax reporting carefully.

**5. Missing the April 15 deadline:** Late filing of 1040-NR incurs substantial penalties. If you cannot meet the deadline, file Form 4868 (Application for Automatic Extension) by April 15 to extend the deadline to October 15.
