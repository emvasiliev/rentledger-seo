# Form 8840: Closer Connection Exception for Canadian Landlords in New Jersey

## What is Form 8840?

Form 8840 (Closer Connection Exception Statement for Aliens) is an Internal Revenue Service form that allows certain foreign nationals—including Canadian citizens—to establish that they maintain a "closer connection" to their home country despite meeting the US Substantial Presence Test (SPT). When filed successfully, it prevents you from being classified as a US resident for federal tax purposes, even if you've spent significant time in the United States.

For Canadian landlords who own rental properties in New Jersey, this form is a critical tool. Without it, time spent in the US for property management, maintenance inspections, or seasonal residency could push you over the SPT threshold, triggering full US resident status and complicated filing requirements.

## Understanding the Substantial Presence Test

The SPT uses a points-based calculation:
- **Days present in the current year**: Counted as 1 point each
- **Days present in the prior year**: Counted as 1/3 point each
- **Days present in the year before that**: Counted as 1/6 point each

If your total reaches 183 or more points, you're automatically considered a US resident for tax purposes—unless you file Form 8840 to claim the closer connection exception.

For a Canadian landlord visiting New Jersey regularly to manage rental properties, these days accumulate quickly. A week-long property inspection trip in spring, summer management visits, and fall inspections can easily total 60–90 days annually, pushing many into SPT territory over a multi-year calculation.

## How Form 8840 Applies Specifically in New Jersey

New Jersey presents a particular challenge for Canadian landlords because:

1. **High property tax burden**: New Jersey's average effective property tax rate of 2.49% means significant time investment in management and oversight
2. **State-level residency claims**: New Jersey may independently claim you as a resident for state income tax purposes, creating a mismatch between federal and state treatment
3. **Rental income taxation**: Non-resident rental income from NJ property is taxed at 10.75% in addition to federal rates

Filing Form 8840 establishes your federal non-resident status, but **New Jersey state taxation operates independently**. You'll still file NJ-1040-NR (New Jersey Non-Resident Return) to report rental income. However, proper federal treatment via Form 8840 provides documentation that protects against IRS challenges and creates a clear record for Canadian tax purposes.

## Who Must File Form 8840

You should file Form 8840 if:

- You're a Canadian citizen or permanent resident
- You meet the Substantial Presence Test (183+ points) in the current year
- You maintain a "closer connection" to Canada (typically demonstrated by maintaining your principal residence, family connections, employment, financial accounts, and social ties there)
- You don't want to be classified as a US resident for federal tax purposes

**You should NOT file if:**
- You're a US citizen
- You're a lawful permanent resident (green card holder)
- You don't meet the SPT
- You want to voluntarily elect US residency

## Step-by-Step Completion Guide

### **Part I: General Information**

Enter your name, address, and identification number. Use your Social Insurance Number (SIN) if you don't have an ITIN. Your "US address" should reflect your New Jersey property address or the address where you were present during the relevant tax year.

### **Part II: Determination of Closer Connection**

This is the substantive section. You must establish that your "tax home" remains in Canada by demonstrating:

**1. Permanent Home Availability**
Provide evidence that you maintain a permanent home in Canada—owned or leased in your name. Include:
- Property deed, mortgage statement, or lease agreement showing your name and Canadian address
- Documentation dated before or concurrent with your US property ownership

**2. Residential Ties**
Document your closer connections to Canada:
- Spouse/partner and dependent children resident in Canada
- Immediate family members with Canadian residence
- Membership in professional associations or clubs in Canada
- Medical and dental care providers in Canada
- Bank accounts, investment accounts, and financial relationships in Canada

### **Part III: Days Present**

List your presence in the US by date ranges during the tax year:
- Specify dates you were in the US (not just total days)
- Include purpose if relevant (property management, maintenance, inspection)
- Days in New Jersey for rental property management are counted—proximity to the property doesn't exempt you

### **Part IV: Visa Status and Prior Year Information**

Indicate you're in the US under a visitor status (typically B-1/B-2 visa or visa-exempt status for Canadians). Provide SPT calculations and prior years' presence data if relevant to the overall calculation.

## New Jersey-Specific Considerations

### **State Income Tax Return Coordination**

Even if Form 8840 successfully establishes federal non-resident status, New Jersey applies its own residency rules. A Canadian landlord with New Jersey rental property income must file:

- **Form NJ-1040-NR**: Non-Resident Return reporting rental income
- **Schedule A (NJ)**: Detailing rental property income and expenses
- **New Jersey Schedule D**: Capital gains reporting (if applicable)

New Jersey taxes non-resident rental income at its regular rates (up to 10.75% on rental income). Property tax payments and mortgage interest are deductible on Schedule A, reducing your taxable income.

### **Property Tax Deduction Tracking**

Maintain detailed records of New Jersey property tax payments. These are deductible on your US federal return (Form 1040, Schedule A) and on your New Jersey NJ-1040-NR. Your Canadian T1 return may allow foreign tax credits for both US federal income tax and NJ state income tax paid (subject to Canada-US Tax Treaty Article XXIV provisions).

### **State Residency Challenges**

New Jersey's Division of Taxation may independently audit your residency status based on:
- Days spent in-state
- Ownership of rental property
- Driver's license or vehicle registration
- Utility accounts at the property

Form 8840 provides federal documentation but doesn't automatically prevent NJ state audits. Maintain a contemporaneous log of your US presence days and ensure your Canadian permanent home documentation is robust.

## Canada-US Tax Treaty Relevance

Under Article IV of the Canada-US Tax Treaty, your "resident" status is determined by:

1. Permanent home availability (Art. IV(2)(a))
2. Centre of vital interests—family, economic interests, social/political life (Art. IV(2)(b))
3. Habitual abode (Art. IV(2)(c))
4. Nationality as tiebreaker (Art. IV(2)(d))

Form 8840 aligns with these treaty provisions by documenting your closer connection. When claiming foreign tax credits on your Canadian T1 return (Schedule 11), having filed Form 8840 provides evidence supporting your non-resident treaty status.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Inconsistent Documentation**
Don't claim a Canadian permanent home while maintaining a New Jersey primary residence. Form 8840 requires genuine closer connection—not just technical compliance.

**2. Inadequate Record-Keeping**
Vague day counts or unexplained absences from Canada weaken your case. Maintain calendars, flight records, and utility bills documenting your locations.

**3. Missing the Filing Deadline**
Form 8840 must be filed by **June 15 of the following year**. Missing this deadline can result in SPT inclusion and back-tax liability. File with Form 1040 or amended Form 1040-X if necessary.

**4. Forgetting NJ State Return**
Focusing only on federal Form 8840 leaves you exposed to New Jersey state tax obligations and audits. Always file NJ-1040-NR concurrently.

**5. Overlooking Carryback Rules**
If you file Form 8840 to avoid SPT classification after previously filing as a US resident, IRS rules may limit retroactive corrections. Consult a cross-border tax professional before changing positions.

## Key Deadlines

- **Tax Year Ends**: December 31
- **Form 8840 Filing Deadline**: June 15 of the following year (not April 15)
- **NJ-1040-NR Deadline**: April 15 (same as federal)
- **Canadian T1 Return Deadline**: June 15 (June 15 is also the personal tax deadline for all Canadian residents)

## Key Takeaways for New Jersey Landlords

- **Form 8840 is federal-only**: Successfully establishing closer connection prevents SPT inclusion for IRS purposes, but you must separately file NJ-1040-NR to satisfy New Jersey state income tax obligations on your 10.75% taxed rental income.
