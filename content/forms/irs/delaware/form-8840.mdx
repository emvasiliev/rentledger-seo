# Form 8840: Delaware Landlord's Guide to Claiming Closer Connection to Canada

## What is Form 8840?

Form 8840 (Closer Connection Exception Statement for Aliens) is an IRS document that allows certain individuals to establish they maintain a closer connection to a foreign country—in your case, Canada—despite meeting the Substantial Presence Test (SPT) in the United States. This form is essential for Canadian landlords who own rental properties in Delaware while spending significant time in the US, as it can prevent unwanted US resident alien status and its associated tax consequences.

The Substantial Presence Test automatically classifies you as a US resident for federal tax purposes if you:
- Are physically present in the US for at least 183 days during the current year, OR
- Are present for 31 days in the current year AND 183 days during a three-year period using a weighted calculation

Without Form 8840, meeting the SPT triggers full US tax residency, requiring you to file a complete US federal tax return and potentially expose your worldwide income to US taxation.

## How Form 8840 Applies to Delaware Landlords

Delaware presents a unique scenario for Canadian property owners. While Delaware has no state income tax on residents, it does require non-residents who earn Delaware-source income—including rental income from real property—to file Form 8840's equivalent state return and pay Delaware income tax at a flat 6.6% rate on net rental income.

**Critical point:** Form 8840 addresses only your federal residency status. Filing Form 8840 does *not* exempt you from Delaware's non-resident income tax obligations on Delaware rental income. You must file both:
1. Federal Form 8840 (if eligible)
2. Delaware Form 205-C (Non-Resident Return of Income) or similar, reporting rental income at the 6.6% rate

Delaware's effective property tax rate averages 0.57% statewide, making it relatively attractive for investment properties. However, this modest tax burden does not reduce your non-resident income tax filing requirement.

## Who Must File Form 8840

You should file Form 8840 if you meet *all* of these conditions:

1. **You are a Canadian citizen or permanent resident** (defined as an "alien" under US tax law)
2. **You meet the Substantial Presence Test** for the tax year in question
3. **You maintain a closer connection to Canada**, meaning:
   - Your permanent residence is in Canada
   - Your family, employment, and personal relationships are primarily centered in Canada
   - You have significant ongoing activities and financial interests in Canada
4. **You have a tax home in Canada** (the place where you maintain your principal abode)
5. **You were not a US resident alien in the previous three calendar years** (with limited exceptions)

Snowbirds who own Delaware rental properties commonly fit this profile. If you spend winters in Delaware managing your rental property but maintain your primary residence, employment, and family in Canada, Form 8840 may shield you from federal resident alien status.

## Step-by-Step: Completing Form 8840

### Part I: Identification
Enter your name, Canadian address, and tax identification number (your Canadian Social Insurance Number or US ITIN if previously assigned).

### Part II: Presence Days
Document your physical presence in the US for the tax year. Count:
- Full days you were physically present in the US
- Fractional days (arrival/departure days count as full days)
- Days you were not present due to medical conditions abroad (limited exception)

### Part III: Tax Home Information
Declare your tax home location. Your tax home is where you maintain a regular place of abode. For Delaware landlords, this typically remains your Canadian residence where you spend the majority of your time.

### Part IV: Closer Connection Details
This section requires narrative explanation. You must demonstrate:
- **Residential ties:** Your principal home is in Canada; your Delaware property is investment property, not your primary residence
- **Family and social ties:** Spouse, children, extended family, and close friends reside in Canada
- **Economic ties:** Primary employment, business interests, bank accounts, investment portfolio, and professional licenses are in Canada
- **Cultural and political ties:** Participation in Canadian civic, religious, or social organizations; Canadian voter registration (if applicable)

Supporting documentation might include:
- Canadian property tax bills and mortgage statements
- Canadian employment letter or business ownership documentation
- Canadian driver's license or provincial health card
- Utility bills showing your Canadian address
- Bank statements from Canadian institutions
- Proof of Canadian family relationships

### Part V: Declaration
Sign and date the form. Submit to the IRS with your federal tax return (Form 1040-NR).

## Delaware-Specific Considerations

### State-Level Non-Resident Return Requirements
Even if Form 8840 successfully establishes federal closer connection status, Delaware requires all non-residents earning Delaware-source income to file a non-resident income tax return. You must:

1. **File Delaware Form 205-C** (or current equivalent) reporting gross and net rental income
2. **Pay Delaware tax at 6.6%** on net income from Delaware rental property
3. **File by June 15** (the same federal deadline)
4. **Claim Delaware tax paid as a foreign tax credit** on your Canadian T1 return

### Coordinating with the Canada-US Tax Treaty
The Canada-US Income and Tax Treaty (Article IV) defines "resident of a Contracting State" using various tests. Form 8840 aligns with Treaty Article IV(2)(c), which allows a "closer connection" exception to prevent double taxation. If Form 8840 is accepted, you remain a resident of Canada under the Treaty, meaning:

- Canadian taxation of your worldwide income continues
- Delaware-source rental income is taxable only to Delaware and Canada
- US federal taxation of worldwide income is avoided

### Foreign Tax Credit Integration
Delaware tax paid ($X amount) can be claimed as a non-business credit on your Canadian T1 return (Schedule 1, line 405, or applicable line). This prevents double taxation on the same Delaware rental income. Keep Delaware tax receipts and correspondence for Canadian Revenue Agency (CRA) verification.

### Property Tax Considerations
Delaware's 0.57% average effective property tax rate applies regardless of your residency status. This property tax is *not* covered by Form 8840 and is a separate obligation based on real property ownership.

## Common Mistakes Delaware Landlords Make

**1. Failing to File Both Federal and State Returns**
The biggest error is filing only Form 8840 and assuming Delaware tax obligations disappear. Form 8840 is federal only; Delaware's non-resident income tax requirement remains independent.

**2. Miscounting Presence Days**
Fractional days matter. Arriving in Delaware on December 30 and leaving January 2 counts as three full days. Use a reliable spreadsheet or tax software to track accurately.

**3. Insufficient Documentation of Closer Connection**
Vague statements like "I live in Canada" are insufficient. The IRS requires detailed, contemporaneous evidence. Gather documents before filing, not after audit notice.

**4. Assuming Rental Property Status Alone Satisfies Closer Connection**
Simply owning investment property in Delaware does not constitute a closer connection to the US. You must prove stronger ties to Canada.

**5. Missing the June 15 Deadline**
The deadline is June 15 for Canadian residents filing Form 8840, not April 15. Missing this deadline can result in automatic resident alien classification and loss of closer connection protection retroactively.

**6. Not Coordinating with Canadian Tax Returns**
Fail to report US-source income on your Canadian T1 return, and you risk CRA correspondence and penalties. All worldwide income must be reported to Canada.

## Key Takeaways for Delaware Landlords

- **Form 8840 prevents federal resident alien status but does not eliminate Delaware state non-resident income tax obligations.** You must file both Form 8840 federally and Delaware Form 205-C at the 6.6% rate, coordinating deadlines and claiming Delaware taxes as a credit on your Canadian T1.

- **Closer connection requires robust, contemporaneous documentation proving your primary residence, family, employment, and economic ties remain in Canada.** Vague statements fail; detailed records, utility bills, employment letters, and family documentation succeed.

- **Coordinate Form 8840 with Canada-US Tax Treaty Article IV and your Canadian reporting to avoid double taxation and maintain Treaty resident status in Canada while eliminating unwanted US federal residency.**
