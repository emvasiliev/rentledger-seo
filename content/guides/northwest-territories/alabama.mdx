## US Rental Property Ownership: A Northwest Territories Landlord's Guide to Alabama Tax Obligations

Owning rental property across the Canada–US border creates a unique tax filing situation. As a Northwest Territories resident, you're subject to Canadian federal taxation on worldwide income, plus Alabama state income tax on US-sourced rental income. Without proper planning, you could face double taxation, withholding penalties, and missed deductions. This guide walks you through the Canadian and US tax requirements for Alabama rental properties.

## Why Northwest Territories + Alabama Creates Complexity

Northwest Territories has no provincial income tax, which simplifies your Canadian tax picture—but it doesn't eliminate it. The CRA still taxes your worldwide income, including US rental revenue. Alabama, conversely, taxes all rental income earned within its borders at a flat 5% rate, regardless of your residency. This creates a two-layer filing obligation:

1. **Canadian side:** Report all US rental income to the CRA on your annual T1 return.
2. **US side:** File a non-resident tax return with both the IRS (federal) and Alabama (state).

The key to minimizing tax burden is understanding which jurisdiction gets priority and where foreign tax credits apply.

## Your Canadian Tax Obligations

### Reporting Rental Income to the CRA

All rental income from your Alabama property must be reported on your Canadian tax return. You'll use **Form T776 (Statement of Real Estate Rentals)** to report:

- Gross rental receipts (in Canadian dollars)
- Operating expenses (utilities, property management, repairs, insurance, property tax)
- Capital cost allowance (CCA), if you choose to claim depreciation
- Net rental income or loss

**Important:** Convert all US amounts to Canadian dollars using the Bank of Canada exchange rate for the year the income is earned. For 2025 reporting (filed in 2026), use the 2025 annual average rate of **1 USD = 1.36 CAD**.

### Form T1135: Foreign Property Reporting

If the fair market value of your Alabama rental property exceeds CAD $100,000 at any point during the tax year, you must file **Form T1135 (Foreign Income Verification Statement)** with your T1 return. This form requires:

- Address and legal description of the property
- Country (United States)
- Fair market value at year-end (in Canadian dollars)
- Rental income earned during the year (in Canadian dollars)

Failure to file T1135 can result in a $25 per day penalty, up to $2,500 per year.

### Claiming a Foreign Tax Credit

This is where you recover the US taxes you've paid. On your Canadian return, you'll claim a **non-business income foreign tax credit** for:

- Alabama state income tax paid (5% of net rental income)
- US federal withholding or self-employment tax paid

The foreign tax credit prevents you from paying tax twice on the same income. Use **Schedule 1, line 40500** to report the credit. The credit is limited to the lesser of taxes paid or the Canadian tax otherwise payable on that income.

**Example:** If your Alabama rental generates $50,000 CAD in net income after expenses, and you've paid $2,500 in Alabama state tax, you can claim that $2,500 as a foreign tax credit (subject to federal limits).

### Deducting Interest and Expenses

All legitimate operating expenses reduce your taxable Canadian rental income:

- Mortgage interest (not principal)
- Property management fees
- Property taxes
- Insurance
- Repairs and maintenance
- Utilities (if you cover them)
- Advertising for tenants
- Professional fees (accounting, legal)

**Do not include:** Capital improvements, principal payments, or depreciation (unless you elect CCA).

## Your US Federal Tax Obligations

### Obtain an ITIN

The IRS requires a **US Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN)** for non-resident aliens filing US tax returns. Apply using **Form W-7 (Application for IRS Individual Taxpayer Identification Number)** through a Canadian tax preparer or directly to the IRS. Processing takes 4–6 weeks. You'll need your ITIN to file Form 1040-NR.

### File Form 1040-NR with Schedule E

Non-resident aliens with US rental income file **Form 1040-NR (US Nonresident Alien Income Tax Return)**, attaching **Schedule E (Supplemental Income or Loss)**. Report on Schedule E:

- Gross rental receipts (in USD)
- All allowable expenses
- Net rental profit or loss

The IRS taxes your net rental income at **ordinary income rates** (up to 37% at federal level in 2025), but you have two withholding options that dramatically affect your cash flow.

### Option 1: Default 30% Withholding (Avoid This)

Without any election, the IRS assumes **30% withholding** on your gross rent. This is extremely inefficient because:

- It applies to gross rent, not net income
- You only recover the excess withholding through your annual return
- It ignores your deductible expenses

If your Alabama property rents for $30,000 USD annually and you claim $12,000 in expenses, the 30% withholding applies to the full $30,000 ($9,000 withheld), even though your taxable income is only $18,000.

### Option 2: Section 871(d) Election (Recommended)

File **Form 8288-B (US Real Property Withholding Exemption Certificate)** to elect taxation of net rental income under **Section 871(d)**. This allows withholding to apply to net income only, not gross rent.

**Key advantages:**
- Withholding applies only to net rental profit
- You claim all business expenses
- Withholding rate is the same (30% federal), but on a smaller base
- You must file Form 1040-NR annually to claim foreign tax credits

**Important:** The 871(d) election is filed by your property manager or management company with the tenant—not directly with the IRS. Ensure your property manager has Form 8288-B on file before rent payments begin.

Using the same example: With 871(d), withholding applies to $18,000 net income (30% = $5,400 withheld), not $30,000 gross. Over a tax year, this saves substantial cash flow.

### File Your Return Before the Deadline

Non-resident aliens must file Form 1040-NR by **June 15, 2026** (for 2025 tax year)—not April 15. This is a 60-day extension built into the law for non-resident aliens. However, if withholding exceeds tax owed, file earlier to claim refunds sooner.

## Your Alabama State Tax Obligations

Alabama taxes all rental income from property located within the state at a **flat rate of 5%**. File **Form 40 (Alabama Individual Income Tax Return)** as a non-resident, reporting only your Alabama rental income (not worldwide income).

**What Alabama requires:**

- Net rental income from your property (gross rent minus all operating expenses, depreciation, and mortgage interest)
- Form 40, Schedule A if claiming itemized deductions
- Payment of 5% state income tax, or verification of withholding

**Filing deadline:** April 15, 2026 (for 2025 tax year). Alabama allows electronic filing through Modernized e-File system or paper forms mailed to the Alabama Department of Revenue.

**Credit for federal tax paid:** Alabama offers a limited credit for US federal income tax paid, but the mechanism differs from Canada's foreign tax credit. Consult a cross-border tax specialist to confirm eligibility in your situation.

## Property Tax Obligations in Alabama

Alabama's average effective property tax rate is **0.41%** on assessed value—relatively low compared to other US states. Taxes are typically paid to the county where the property is located. Your property manager can handle payment, but you'll receive a bill annually listing:

- Assessed value
- Tax rate (in mills, often 8–15 mills per $1,000 assessed value)
- Total amount due

Ensure you deduct property tax payments on both your US Schedule E (Form 1040-NR) and Canadian Form T776.
