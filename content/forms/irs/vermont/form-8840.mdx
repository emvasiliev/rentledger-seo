# Form 8840: Closer Connection Exception for Canadian Landlords with Vermont Property

## What Is Form 8840?

Form 8840 (Closer Connection Exception Statement for Aliens) is an IRS document that allows foreign nationals—including Canadian citizens—to claim they maintain a closer connection to their home country than to the United States, even if they meet the Substantial Presence Test (SPT) for US tax residency.

The Substantial Presence Test is the IRS's primary mechanism for determining tax residency. It calculates your US presence based on:
- Days physically present in the current year (each day = 1 point)
- Days present in the prior year (each day = 1/3 point)
- Days present in the year before that (each day = 1/6 point)

If your weighted total equals 183 days or more, you're deemed a US resident for tax purposes—unless you file Form 8840 to establish a closer connection to Canada.

For Canadian landlords owning Vermont rental property, this distinction is critical. US residency triggers:
- Worldwide income taxation by the IRS
- State income tax obligations in Vermont
- Different filing requirements on your Canadian T1 return
- Potential double taxation without proper treaty relief

## How Form 8840 Applies in Vermont

Vermont presents a unique situation for Canadian landlords, particularly those from Quebec. The state's proximity to the Canadian border, combined with attractive real estate opportunities and seasonal appeal, makes it common for Canadians to own property there and spend extended time in-state.

### Vermont's Tax Environment

Vermont imposes an 8.75% state income tax on rental income earned within the state. If you're classified as a US resident for federal purposes without filing Form 8840, you'll owe:
- Federal income tax on worldwide income (plus self-employment tax if applicable)
- Vermont state income tax on all rental income from your property
- Vermont property tax (average effective rate: 1.9% of assessed value)

Non-resident rental income taxation in Vermont follows specific rules. Typically, non-resident landlords file Vermont Form BI-471 (Nonresident Withholding) and report rental income on Vermont's non-resident return. However, if you're a US resident under the SPT, you file a full Vermont resident return.

The Canada-US Tax Treaty (Article IV and V) provides relief mechanisms. Canadian residents can claim foreign tax credits on their Canadian T1 return for taxes paid to Vermont and the US. Form 8840 is your foundation for maintaining Canadian residency status and accessing these treaty protections.

### The Closer Connection Test

To successfully file Form 8840, you must demonstrate:

1. **Permanent home availability in Canada**: You must maintain a home in Canada where you have the right to reside. This can be a home you own, rent, or where family members reside.

2. **Closer personal ties to Canada**: The IRS examines:
   - Family relationships and residence locations
   - Economic and professional interests
   - Social and cultural organizations
   - Religious affiliations
   - Where you conduct banking and investments

3. **Closer residential ties to Canada**: More significant than connections to the United States.

For Vermont landlords, maintaining these connections is often straightforward. You likely have:
- A primary residence in Canada
- Family and employment in Canada
- Canadian bank accounts and investments
- Membership in Canadian professional or social organizations

## Who Must File Form 8840

You must file Form 8840 if **all three conditions** are met:

1. You meet the Substantial Presence Test for the current tax year
2. You do **not** meet the SPT for the prior two years
3. You maintain a closer connection to Canada than to the United States

**Practical scenario**: You're a Quebec resident who owns a rental property in Vermont and spent 120 days in Vermont and 80 days elsewhere in the US during the tax year. Your SPT calculation: (120 × 1) + (prior year days × 1/3) + (year-before days × 1/6). If this totals 183+, you've met the SPT and should file Form 8840.

Form 8840 is filed with your federal Form 1040-NR (US Nonresident Alien Income Tax Return) or relevant Form 1040 variant. You cannot file Form 8840 alone; it must accompany a US federal tax return.

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form 8840

### Part I: Personal Information
Enter your name, Social Security Number (or ITIN), and address in Canada. Include your passport number and country of citizenship (Canada).

### Part II: Days of Presence in the US
Count calendar days physically present in the US during the tax year. Partial days count as full days. Days when you crossed the US border count toward your presence total.

### Part III: Permanent Home Information
Provide details of your Canadian home:
- Address in Canada
- Whether you own, rent, or have the right to reside there
- The availability period (typically "throughout the year")
- Who else resides there (spouse, children, etc.)

The permanent home does **not** need to be exclusively yours. A spouse or family member's residence where you have the legal right to live satisfies this requirement.

### Part IV: Closer Connection Ties
Explain your ties to Canada. The IRS provides checkboxes and space for narrative explanation:
- Family members and their locations
- Your country of employment and nature of employment
- Bank accounts and investment accounts (locations)
- Professional licenses or memberships
- Rental properties in Canada (if applicable)
- Country of voter registration and driver's license

For Vermont landlords, use this section to document:
- Spouse/children residency in Canada
- Canadian employment
- Canadian professional credentials
- Investment accounts in Canada
- Canadian tax filing status

### Part V: Closer Connection to US (Negative Evidence)
Demonstrate why you are **not** closely connected to the US:
- You have no permanent home in the US
- Your Vermont property is rental property, not a personal residence
- You have no employment in the US
- Your bank accounts and investments are in Canada
- Your family connections are primarily in Canada

## Vermont-Specific Considerations

### Rental Property Designation
Your Vermont property must be clearly identified as a rental property, not a personal residence. The IRS distinguishes between:
- **Personal residences**: Properties where you stay during visits (weighs toward US connection)
- **Investment properties**: Rental properties you do not occupy (neutral to positive for closer connection claim)

If you rent out the Vermont property year-round and maintain Canada as your primary residence, this strengthens your Form 8840 position.

### Multi-Property Owners
If you own multiple US properties (Vermont plus additional states), document all holdings. Ownership of multiple investment properties doesn't necessarily trigger US residency if you maintain Canadian residency ties and don't occupy them.

### Estate and Trust Considerations
If your Vermont property is held through a trust or LLC, clearly document the ownership structure on Form 8840. The IRS will examine whether you have beneficial ownership or merely trustee duties.

### Snowbird Status
Vermont attracts Canadian snowbirds who spend winter months (December–March) in the state. If you spend 60–90 days annually in Vermont but maintain year-round Canadian residence, you're likely below or near the SPT threshold. However, if you exceed 183 weighted days across the three-year lookback period, Form 8840 becomes essential.

### Vermont Nonresident Return Obligation
Even with successful Form 8840 filing, Vermont requires nonresident landlords to file Form BI-471 reporting Vermont-source rental income and ensuring proper withholding. This is separate from federal Form 8840 compliance and must be filed by April 15 or June 15 (depending on your situation).

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Incomplete Documentation**
Provide specific evidence of Canadian ties. Vague claims ("I have family in Canada") don't suffice. List names, cities, and relationships.

**2. Failing to File Form 8840 When Required**
If you meet the SPT and fail to file Form 8840 when eligible, the IRS classifies you as a US resident. This retroactively triggers full US tax obligations and penalties.

**3. Inconsistent Statements Across Returns**
Ensure your Form 8840 aligns with your Canadian T1 return and any state tax filings. Contradictions invite IRS scrutiny and CRA review.

**4. Underestimating Day Counts**
Carefully count and document US days. IRS records (arrival/departure records, credit card usage) may contradict your tally.

**5. Ignoring the Prior-Year SPT**
Form 8840 eligibility requires that you **did not meet** the SPT in the two preceding years. If you met it previously, you're generally locked into US residency for that year and must wait until you fall below the threshold.
