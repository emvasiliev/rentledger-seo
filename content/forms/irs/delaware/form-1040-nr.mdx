# Form 1040-NR for Canadian Landlords: Your Complete Delaware Rental Property Guide

## What is Form 1040-NR?

Form 1040-NR (U.S. Income Tax Return for Nonresident Alien Individuals) is the primary federal income tax return filed by non-resident aliens—including Canadian landlords—who have U.S.-source income subject to federal taxation. Unlike U.S. citizens and residents who file Form 1040, non-resident aliens use Form 1040-NR to report specific categories of income that the IRS has determined are "effectively connected income" (ECI).

For rental property owners, the 1040-NR becomes mandatory when you make an election under **Section 871(d) of the Internal Revenue Code**. This election allows you to treat your rental income as ECI and deduct operating expenses (mortgage interest, property taxes, maintenance, insurance, etc.) against that income—rather than paying tax on gross rents. This election is almost always advantageous for Canadian landlords with rental losses or thin margins.

## How Form 1040-NR Applies to Delaware Rental Property

Delaware presents a favorable tax environment for rental property owners compared to many states, but it still imposes specific filing requirements.

### Federal Treatment

When you own rental property in Delaware and make the Section 871(d) election, your Delaware rental income becomes ECI under federal law. You must file Form 1040-NR with the IRS to:

- Report your rental income (gross rents received)
- Claim deductions tied to that property (expenses)
- Calculate your U.S. taxable income at the federal level

The IRS will assess federal income tax at graduated rates (ranging from 10% to 37% depending on your total net income).

### Delaware State Tax Obligation

**Delaware imposes a 6.6% state income tax on rental income earned within the state.** This is a flat rate that applies to Delaware-source rental income for non-residents. You are required to file a **Delaware Nonresident Income Tax Return (Form 500-N or the amended Form 500 series for non-residents)** separately from your federal 1040-NR.

Additionally, Delaware assesses annual property taxes on real estate at an average effective rate of **0.57%**, calculated on the county-assessed value. This is deductible against both federal and state rental income.

The combination of federal tax, Delaware's 6.6% state tax, and property taxes can significantly impact your net rental income. The Canada-US Tax Treaty allows you to claim foreign tax credits on your Canadian return (Form T1) for both federal and state taxes paid to the United States.

## Who Must File Form 1040-NR

You must file Form 1040-NR if you are:

1. **A non-resident alien** (not a U.S. citizen or permanent resident/green card holder)
2. **With U.S.-source rental income** from Delaware property
3. **Who has elected Section 871(d) treatment** (or your rental income is otherwise ECI)
4. **With a filing requirement** under IRS rules

Canadian citizens living in Canada are non-resident aliens for U.S. tax purposes, even if they have income from U.S. real estate. You do not need to be physically present in the United States to file.

**Exception:** If your only U.S. income is passive investment income (dividends, interest) and you do not elect Section 871(d), you may not be required to file. However, rental income from property you actively manage almost always requires filing once the election is made.

## Step-by-Step: How to Complete Form 1040-NR for Delaware Rental Income

### Step 1: Gather Documentation

Before you begin, collect:

- **Rental income records:** All rent payments received during the tax year
- **Property tax statement:** Delaware county assessment and taxes paid
- **Mortgage statement:** Interest paid (Form 1098 if provided by lender)
- **Operating expense receipts:** Maintenance, repairs, insurance, property management fees, utilities you paid
- **Depreciation calculation:** If claiming depreciation on the building (typically 27.5 years for residential property)
- **Your SIN/ITN:** You will need an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN) if you do not have a U.S. Social Security Number

### Step 2: Calculate Net Rental Income

On Form 1040-NR, Schedule E (or similar schedules for non-residents):

1. **Enter gross rental income** received from your Delaware property
2. **Subtract allowable deductions:**
   - Property taxes paid to Delaware
   - Mortgage interest (not principal)
   - Insurance premiums
   - Repairs and maintenance
   - Property management fees
   - Utilities (if you cover them)
   - HOA fees (if applicable)
   - Depreciation (if eligible)
3. **Calculate net rental income or loss**

The resulting figure is your taxable rental income (or loss carryforward if negative).

### Step 3: Complete Form 1040-NR Lines

- **Line 1a–1z:** Report your rental income and deductions
- **Line 19:** Enter your net income from rentals
- **Lines 24–30:** Calculate adjusted gross income (AGI)
- **Lines 31–36:** Claim the standard deduction (if eligible as a non-resident alien—rules vary) or itemize
- **Lines 37–43:** Calculate taxable income and federal tax

### Step 4: Section 871(d) Election Statement

You must attach a statement to Form 1040-NR explicitly electing Section 871(d) treatment if this is your first year making the election. The statement should indicate that you are electing to treat your rental income as ECI under Section 871(d). Once made, this election typically remains in effect for all future years unless you formally revoke it.

### Step 5: Estimate and Pay Withholding

If you do not have a U.S. agent or mortgage servicer withholding tax on your behalf, you may need to make **estimated tax payments** using Form 1040-ES (NR). Delaware-source rental income typically does not have automatic withholding, so quarterly estimated payments may be necessary to avoid penalties.

## Delaware-Specific Considerations

### Property Tax Deductibility

Delaware's 0.57% average property tax rate is **fully deductible** against your rental income on both Form 1040-NR (federal) and Delaware Form 500-N (state). This creates a favorable feedback loop: the deduction reduces both your federal and state taxable income.

**Example:** If your Delaware rental property is assessed at $300,000 with a 0.57% tax rate:
- Annual property tax: $1,710
- This $1,710 deduction reduces your federal and Delaware state taxable income

### Delaware Form 500-N Filing

Concurrently with your federal Form 1040-NR, file **Delaware Nonresident Income Tax Return (Form 500-N)** with the Delaware Division of Revenue. This is a separate state return that reports the same rental income and deductions. The 6.6% Delaware tax is applied to your net taxable rental income after deductions.

**Delaware filing deadline:** Typically April 15 (same as federal), though an extension to June 15 may be available if you request federal extension.

### Canadian Foreign Tax Credit

On your Canadian **T1 return (Schedule 11)**, you can claim a foreign tax credit for:

- U.S. federal income tax paid on the rental income
- Delaware state income tax (6.6%)

The foreign tax credit cannot exceed the Canadian tax you would have paid on that same income at your marginal rate. This prevents double taxation, though you must file Form T1161 (Application for Carryforward of Unclaimed Non-Business Income Tax Losses) if your U.S. taxes exceed your Canadian tax liability on the rental income.

### FIRPTA Withholding Considerations

If you ever sell the Delaware property, the buyer's agent or title company must withhold **15% under FIRPTA (Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act)** unless you obtain a withholding certificate. This is separate from the annual Form 1040-NR but relevant for your planning.
