# Form W-7: ITIN Application Guide for Canadian Landlords with Texas Rental Property

## What Is Form W-7?

Form W-7 (Application for IRS Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) is the official IRS application to obtain an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN). An ITIN is a nine-digit tax identification number formatted as XXX-XX-XXXX, issued by the IRS to individuals who are not eligible for a US Social Security Number (SSN) but have a US tax filing requirement.

For Canadian landlords, an ITIN serves as your unique US tax identifier and is required to:
- File Form 1040-NR (US Non-Resident Alien Income Tax Return)
- Comply with FIRPTA (Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act) withholding obligations
- Report rental income from Texas properties
- Claim deductions and potentially foreign tax credits on your US return

## Why Canadian Landlords Need an ITIN for Texas Property

### The Texas Tax Advantage

Texas has **no state income tax**, making it an attractive investment destination for Canadian landlords. However, the absence of state income tax does not eliminate federal US tax obligations. Nor does it avoid the requirement for an ITIN.

As a Canadian non-resident alien owning rental property in Texas, you remain subject to:
- **Federal income tax** on US-source rental income (typically 30% withholding under FIRPTA rules, reduced under the Canada-US Tax Treaty)
- **Texas property tax** averaging **1.8% of assessed value** annually
- **Form 1040-NR filing** requirement if your net rental income exceeds filing thresholds

### FIRPTA and the ITIN Connection

When you sell Texas rental property, the buyer's closing agent is required under FIRPTA to withhold 15% of the sale price (as of 2024) and remit it to the IRS. This withholding is credited against your US tax liability. To process this withholding correctly and to file a tax return claiming a refund of excess withholding, you need an ITIN.

Even if you're not currently selling, obtaining an ITIN early prevents delays when a sale transaction occurs.

## Who Must File Form W-7

You must file Form W-7 if you are:
- A **Canadian citizen or permanent resident** (non-US citizen)
- **Deriving income** from US sources (rental income from Texas property qualifies)
- **Required to file** a US tax return (Form 1040-NR)
- **Not eligible** for a US Social Security Number
- **Not filing** as a US resident alien (you remain a Canadian resident for tax purposes)

### Canadian Resident Test

To confirm you remain a non-resident alien for US purposes, you must not meet the "substantial presence test." Generally, if you spend 183+ days in the US in the current or preceding years (with weighted calculations), you may be considered a resident alien. Most Canadian landlords managing properties remotely from Canada satisfy the non-resident alien definition.

## Step-by-Step: Completing Form W-7

### Step 1: Gather Required Documentation

Before submitting Form W-7, prepare:
- **Valid passport** (Canadian passport required; copy certified by a notary public or acceptable verifier)
- **Birth certificate** (original or certified copy)
- **Proof of tax filing requirement** (rental property deed, lease agreement, or property tax statement from Texas)
- **Completed Form W-7** (6 pages)

Texas property tax records are publicly accessible through county appraisal district websites (e.g., Travis Central Appraisal District for Austin properties). Print or request a tax certificate as evidence of ownership.

### Step 2: Complete Form W-7 Accurately

**Part A – Applicant Information:**
- Enter your full legal name (as it appears on your passport)
- Provide your Canadian mailing address
- Enter your Canadian phone number and email

**Part B – Type of Applicant:**
- Select the appropriate category. For Canadian landlords, the relevant category is typically **"Individual with US source income."**

**Part C – Reason for Applying:**
- Check the box for **"Requesting an ITIN for US tax return filing."**
- Also check **"Nonresident alien filing a US tax return"** if applicable.

**Part D – Prior ITIN:**
- If this is your first ITIN application, answer "No."

**Part E – US Visa Status:**
- Most Canadian landlords will indicate they are **not in the US on a visa** and are visiting as a Canadian resident.

**Part F – Signature & Declaration:**
- Sign and date the form. Both you and a witness (or IRS representative if processed in person at an IRS office) must sign.

### Step 3: Submit Form W-7 with Supporting Documents

You have three submission options:

1. **By Mail:** Send Form W-7 with certified copies of your passport and birth certificate to the IRS:
   - **IRS ITIN Operation Team, Austin, TX** (processing center for Texas-submitted returns)
   - Mailing address: [Check current IRS.gov for the dedicated ITIN processing mailing address]
   - **Processing time: 4–6 weeks** from receipt (as of 2024)

2. **In Person at an IRS Office:** Visit the IRS office in Dallas, Houston, San Antonio, or Austin. You'll need an appointment (call 1-800-829-1040 to schedule).

3. **Through a Certified Acceptance Agent (CAA):** Some tax preparers and accountants in Canada or the US are IRS-certified to verify documents and submit Form W-7 on your behalf. This option often accelerates processing.

### Step 4: Receive Your ITIN

Once approved, the IRS will mail your ITIN notice (Form W-7C or a letter confirming your number) to your Canadian address. **Record your ITIN carefully.** You'll use it on all future Form 1040-NR filings, FIRPTA withholding statements, and correspondence with the IRS.

## Texas-Specific Considerations for ITIN Applicants

### No State Income Tax—Federal Focus Required

Texas has **zero state income tax**, so your ITIN application and subsequent tax filing focus entirely on **federal US obligations**. However, this does not reduce compliance requirements:

- You still file Form 1040-NR federally.
- You still pay **Texas property tax** (approximately 1.8% of appraised value annually), which is **not deductible on your federal return** but may be deductible on your Canadian return.
- You must report Texas rental income on your Canadian T1 General return and may claim a **foreign tax credit** for US federal taxes paid (but not state taxes, since Texas has none).

### High Property Tax Context

Texas's 1.8% effective property tax rate is among North America's highest. Use this figure when calculating net rental income on Form 1040-NR Schedule E (Rental Income). Your property tax is a **deductible rental expense**, reducing US taxable income.

### Currency Considerations

If your Texas property is financed in USD and you report in CAD on your Canadian tax return, you'll need to track exchange rates for:
- Mortgage interest deductions
- Rental income conversions
- Property tax payments

On your Form 1040-NR, report all amounts in USD using the exchange rate on the date the income was received.

### Canada-US Tax Treaty Application

As a Canadian resident filing Form 1040-NR for Texas rental income, you may benefit from the **Canada-US Tax Treaty Article XII (Rental Income)**. The treaty generally allows you to claim a reduced withholding rate (typically 15% instead of 30%) on rental income, provided you properly disclose your ITIN and residency status.

On your Canadian T1 return, you'll report the same US rental income and can claim a **foreign tax credit** for US federal taxes withheld or paid, offsetting your Canadian tax liability.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Submitting Unverified Copies:** The IRS requires certified or notarized copies of your passport and birth certificate. Photocopies without verification will result in rejection.

2. **Delaying Application Until Needed:** Many Canadian landlords wait until a property sale occurs to apply for an ITIN. Apply proactively to avoid FIRPTA withholding delays and penalties.

3. **Incomplete Rental Income Documentation:** Ensure your Form W-7 submission includes evidence of your US tax filing requirement (property deed, tax assessment, or lease agreement).

4. **Misinterpreting "No State Tax" as "No US Tax":** Texas's lack of state income tax does not exempt you from federal Form 1040-NR filing. Federal filing is mandatory if you have net rental income.
