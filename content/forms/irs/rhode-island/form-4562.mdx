# Form 4562 for Canadian Landlords: Rhode Island Rental Property Depreciation

## What is Form 4562?

Form 4562 (Depreciation and Amortization) is the IRS form used to calculate and claim depreciation deductions on depreciable assets, including residential rental property. For Canadian landlords owning rental real estate in the United States, this form is essential for reducing taxable income on their US tax return.

Depreciation is a non-cash deduction that allows property owners to recover the cost of their building (not the land) over a specified period. The IRS treats residential rental property—which includes apartments, single-family homes, condos, and similar structures rented to tenants—as depreciable assets with a useful life of 27.5 years using the straight-line depreciation method.

## How Form 4562 Applies to Rhode Island Rental Property

Rhode Island presents a specific tax environment for non-resident Canadian landlords. The state imposes a 5.99% state income tax on rental income earned within Rhode Island, meaning you'll need to file both a federal US tax return (Form 1040-NR) and a Rhode Island state return (Form RI-1040-NR for non-residents).

**The depreciation benefit works as follows:**

1. **Federal Level**: You claim depreciation on Form 4562, which flows to Schedule E (Supplemental Income or Loss) and reduces your federal taxable income.

2. **Rhode Island Level**: The depreciation deduction carries through to your Rhode Island non-resident return, reducing the income subject to Rhode Island's 5.99% state tax.

3. **Canadian Tax Treatment**: As a Canadian resident, you must also report US rental income on your Canadian T1 return. Under the Canada-US Tax Treaty, you're entitled to a foreign tax credit for US taxes paid, including both federal and Rhode Island state taxes.

This creates a three-layer tax scenario: you depreciate the property for US purposes, claim the deduction in both federal and Rhode Island state calculations, and then reconcile the income and foreign taxes on your Canadian return.

## Who Files Form 4562

Any landlord—whether a US citizen, green card holder, Canadian resident, or other non-resident alien—who owns depreciable rental property in the United States must file Form 4562 if claiming depreciation. For Canadian residents, this requirement applies equally.

**You must file Form 4562 if you:**
- Own residential rental property in Rhode Island
- Place the property in service (first rent it or offer it for rent)
- Want to claim depreciation deductions
- Have other depreciable property (appliances, furnishings, improvements with useful lives under 27.5 years)

Non-residents file Form 4562 along with Form 1040-NR (US Non-Resident Alien Income Tax Return), attaching it to Schedule E. The filing deadline is April 15 of the following year, or June 15 if you obtain an automatic extension (and have a US address or agent).

## Step-by-Step: Completing Form 4562 for Rhode Island Rental Property

### Part I: Election to Expense and Other Depreciation (Not Commonly Used for Rental Property)
Most Canadian landlords skip Part I. This section applies to business equipment and assets you elect to expense immediately rather than depreciate. Residential rental buildings do not qualify for Section 179 expensing.

### Part II: Special Depreciation Allowance
Skip Part II unless you've made improvements or replacements to the property that qualify for bonus depreciation. For most established rental properties, this section remains blank.

### Part III: Depreciation and Amortization
**This is where you calculate building depreciation.**

**Step 1: Gather Property Information**
- Property address (your Rhode Island rental address)
- Date placed in service (when you first rented the property or completed acquisition)
- Basis in the property (purchase price plus acquisition costs, minus the land value)

**Step 2: Determine the Building Basis**
The critical step is separating the building from the land. Land does not depreciate. 

Example: You purchased a Rhode Island rental property for $400,000 CAD (approximately $300,000 USD at current rates). Local assessor records show the land represents 20% of value and the building 80%.
- Building basis: $240,000 USD
- Land basis: $60,000 USD (no depreciation)

**Step 3: Calculate Annual Depreciation**
For residential rental property placed in service after 1986, use the 27.5-year straight-line method.

Annual depreciation = Building basis ÷ 27.5 years

Using the example above:
- $240,000 ÷ 27.5 = $8,727.27 per year

**Step 4: Complete the Form**
On Form 4562, Part III, enter:
- Column (a): Property description (e.g., "Residential rental property, 123 Main Street, Providence, RI")
- Column (b): Date placed in service
- Column (c): Cost or basis ($240,000)
- Column (d): Convention (Mid-month for real property)
- Column (e): Depreciation method (Straight line)
- Column (f): Recovery period (27.5 years)
- Column (g): Annual depreciation ($8,727.27)

Sum all depreciation and transfer the total to Schedule E, line 18.

## Rhode Island-Specific Considerations

### State Return Requirements
Rhode Island requires non-residents to file Form RI-1040-NR if they had Rhode Island source income exceeding certain thresholds. The depreciation deduction you claim on Form 4562 reduces your Rhode Island taxable income, lowering your state tax liability.

At Rhode Island's 5.99% tax rate, the depreciation deduction saves approximately $523 in state tax annually (using the $8,727 example above).

### Property Tax Coordination
Rhode Island's average effective property tax rate is 1.63%, among the highest in the US. Form 4562 depreciation is a federal and state income tax deduction only; it does not reduce Rhode Island property tax assessments, which are based on assessed value, not depreciated basis.

### Multi-Year Depreciation Tracking
Keep meticulous records of your depreciation deductions by year. If you sell the Rhode Island property, the accumulated depreciation is recaptured at a 25% federal rate (and Rhode Island state rates), even though the deduction provided ordinary income deduction benefits.

### Currency Conversion
Convert your purchase price from CAD to USD at the exchange rate on the date you placed the property in service. Document this rate from the Bank of Canada or a reliable source.

## Common Mistakes Canadian Landlords Make

**1. Failing to Separate Land from Building**
The most frequent error is depreciating the entire purchase price. Land basis cannot be depreciated. Use property tax assessments, appraisals, or the seller's closing disclosure to allocate the purchase price.

**2. Starting Depreciation Before Placing Property in Service**
Depreciation begins only when the property is ready and available for rental, not when you purchase it or close the deal.

**3. Overlooking Component Depreciation**
Major renovations or replacements (roof, HVAC, appliances) may have shorter useful lives (5–15 years) under MACRS rules and can be depreciated faster than the building. Many landlords miss this opportunity for additional deductions.

**4. Not Tracking Basis Adjustments**
If you've made capital improvements, your basis increases, and so does your depreciable amount. Conversely, casualty losses decrease basis.

**5. Forgetting Depreciation Recapture on Sale**
Forgetting that selling the property triggers recapture of all accumulated depreciation as taxable gain at preferential rates (but still taxable).

## Key Deadlines for Canadian Landlords

- **Form 4562 Attachment**: Must accompany Schedule E and Form 1040-NR by April 15 (or June 15 with extension)
- **Extension Request**: File Form 4868 by April 15 if you need additional time
- **Rhode Island Return**: Due by April 15 (same as federal deadline)
- **Canadian T1 Return**: Due June 15, 2024, for 2023 tax year (payment due April 30 if owing)
- **Record Retention**: Keep all depreciation schedules and supporting documents for at least 6 years
